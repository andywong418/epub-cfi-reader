4 The solid state

4.1 Introduction

So far we have focused our attention almost entirely on the NMR spectroscopy of molecules and ions in solution. There is very good reason for this: the rapid tumbling of solution (or gas phase) species averages many line broadening interactions to zero. As a result, high resolution spectra (linewidths typically less than 0.1 Hz for spin 1/2 nuclei) are obtained from which it is (relatively) easy to extract detailed structural/chemical information. In the solid state however, these line broadening interactions are not removed by rapid molecular motions and can result in lines tens, or even hundreds, of thousands of hertz wide. These effects are illustrated in the spectra in figure 4.1 which shows the 1H NMR spectra of methanol adsorbing on a Cu/ZnO/alumina catalyst; the spectra have been recorded every few minutes. Initially two sharp resonances due to the methyl and OH protons of methanol in the gas phase are seen. These rapidly disappear to be replaced by a broad, barely visible resonance assigned to physisorbed methanol molecules. This in turn disappears apparently to be replaced by nothing! The methanol has chemisorbed to give methoxy, an immobile species that gives lines too broad to be observed. The Knight shifted resonance of hydrogen adsorbed on metallic copper is also seen at ca. 90 ppm. Clearly, this last adsorbed species is highly mobile.

4.2 Nuclear interactions in the solid state

The big difference between a solid sample and a solution sample as far as NMR is concerned is the obvious one; in solution the molecules are free to rotate rapidly and isotropically whereas in a solid the molecules are more or less rigidly fixed in the lattice. Consider, for example, the interaction of the nuclear magnetic moment with the spectrometer magnetic field - the Zeeman interaction. Intuitively we would expect the magnitude of this interaction to depend on the angle between the nuclear spin and the spectrometer magnetic field, thus spin a and spin b

In the solid, however, the molecules are fixed in place; the Zeeman interaction is now a tensor property and a range of resonance frequencies is expected resulting in a broad line.

Important line broadening interactions affecting the NMR spectrum in the solid state are:

(i) shielding interactions
(ii) dipolar interactions

Shielding anisotropy

Like the Zeeman interaction, the effect of chemical shielding depends upon the orientation of the molecule with respect to the spectrometer field, it is a tensor property. Consider the tetrahedral molecule triphenylphosphineoxide. The electrons are not isotropically distributed around the phosphorus. The chemical shift of the phosphorus nucleus will thus vary with the inclination of the C3 axis to the magnetic field, figure 4.4. If we record the 31P NMR spectrum of a powder sample of Ph3PO, in which all possible orientations are present, we will get the NMR lineshape shown in figure 4.5 The molecule has axial symmetry and we can define two important orientations (and shifts) when the symmetry axis is parallel, σ∥, or perpendicular, σ⊥, to the magnetic field.

The principal axis system

We can always define an axis system (called the principal axis system) in which all off-diagonal elements of the shielding tensor are zero. This allows us to define the shielding with three quantities σ11, σ22, and σ33. These are shown for arbitrary symmetry in figure 4.6 By convention, σ11 < σ22 < σ33.

One obvious way to minimize the effect of dipolar interactions, since this is a through space effect, is to work with dilute spin systems such as 13C at natural abundance; however this restricts the number of systems available to study, does nothing to remove the chemical shift anisotropy and can cause signal to noise problems - the sensitivity of a system of dilute spins is necessarily low. Clearly another method of removing these interactions is required.

4.3 Magic angle spinning

Fortunately, chemical shift anisotropy, dipolar interactions, (and first order quadrupolar terms) all depend on the geometric factor 3cos2⁡θ−1. In solution rapid tumbling of the molecules occurs and the time average of cos 2 ⁡θ is 1/3, so 3cos2⁡θ−1=0 and these effects that depend on 3cos2⁡θ−1 vanish. If a solid sample is spun at an angle θ to the magnetic field, figure 4.8, then the time averaged values of the CSA etc are scaled by ½ (3cos2⁡θ−1 ); by setting θ equal to 54.74°, the magic angle, we can thus make these effects vanish. The only proviso is that the spinning rate must be larger than the interaction we are trying to remove. In modem solid state spectrometers spinning rates up to 12 kHz are routine with rates up to 35 kHz possible. The effect of magic angle spinning on the solid state 31P NMR spectrum of Ph3PO is shown in figure 4.9. As the spinning rate increases, the effect of the chemical shift anisotropy is reduced. At high spinning rates a single peak at the isotropic chemical shift is seen, at lower rates this is flanked by spinning side bands from which the principal components of the chemical shielding tensor can be obtained.

Application of MAS-NMR

Molecular sieve materials such as zeolites and aluminium phosphates form an important class of catalytic materials that is used in a wide range of applications from cat crackers in petrol refining (CATalytic reactors used to CRACK long chain hydrocarbons to short chains using the acidity of aluminosilicates), through shape selective reactions (using the size of the pores to control the shape of the transition state) to supports for enantioselective heterogeneous catalysts. A problem frequently encountered in characterizing these materials is that it is often impossible to grow single crystals of sufficient size for an X-ray crystallographic study. Solid state MAS-NMR, since it gives information about the local environment of the silicon, aluminium, Page 85, Chapter 4 The solid statephosphorus, etc in the structure, is an important tool in determining the structure of molecular sieves.

For example, the structure of the aluminophosphate VPI-5 has been studied by powder diffraction techniques and two different structures proposed. Fig 4.10 shows a slice through one channel in each proposed structure along the [001] direction. The structures extend in all directions, thus, in the high symmetry structure, there are two types of phosphorus and two types of aluminium each in the ratio 2:1 and all the aluminiums and phosphoruses are four co-ordinate. (In the solid state, chemical equivalence requires that nuclei occupy identical crystallographic sites.) In the low symmetry structure however, some of the aluminiums carry two co-ordinated water molecules and so are octahedral, resulting in three types of phosphorus, two four co-ordinate aluminium sites and one six co-ordinate Al. The 31P MAS-NMR spectrum, recorded at room temperature, figure 4.11a, shows three phosphorus sites in the ratio 1:1:1 consistent with the low symmetry structure. Subsequently it was shown, also by 31P MAS-NMR, that the structure undergoes a fully reversible transformation between the two structures at about 350 K, the 31P NMR spectrum now showing two resonances in the ratio 2:1 as predicted for the high temperature structure, figure 4.11b; the co-ordinated water is released from the framework aluminium into the pores of the zeolite as the temperature is raised.

4.4 Cross polarization

High power decoupling is routinely used to eliminate dipolar coupling between (usually) proton spins and the observed nucleus in the solid state. The removal of dipolar interactions decreases the linewidth aiding sensitivity. However, since the aim is to remove the large dipolar couplings rather than the much smaller scalar couplings as in the solution experiment, very high decoupling powers are needed. Fortunately, the high power decoupling irradiation can be used to enhance the sensitivity of the solid state NMR further through a technique known as cross polarization. Cross polarization is, in some sense, the solid state equivalent of the INEPT family of solution experiments - polarization is transferred from an abundant, high γ nucleus to a dilute spin.

The aim of cross polarization is to transfer spin polarization from the proton spins, I, to a less sensitive/abundant nucleus, S. This can be achieved by arranging for the I and S spins to precess in their respective
