3 Dynamic processes and NMR

3.1 Introduction

So far we have looked at how NMR spectroscopy can be used to determine a rigid molecular structure. Not all compounds, however, have rigid structures; for example rotation can occur about the bonds in a molecule, rings can spin or change conformation, and ligands can migrate between different sites in a molecule, figure 3.1.

 

These are all intramolecular processes that can be studied by NMR and are called fluxional processes.

NMR can also be used to study intermolecular processes such as the exchange of bound and free ligands, figure 3.2 - these are called exchange processes. In this chapter we will look at how NMR can be used to study fluxionality and exchange.

3.2 Some theory

The NMR timescale

If we look at a changing system, say a brightly coloured child’s top, if the top is spinning slowly we will be able to see each separate segment of colour. As the top spins faster the colours start to merge until finally we only see an average, figure 3.3. In much the same way all resonance spectroscopies (IR, UV-Vis, NMR etc) can only distinguish the different sites in an exchanging system if the molecule, ligand, whatever, remains in each site for long enough. More exactly, for separate resonances to be observed, the rate of exchange between sites must be less than 2π∆v, where ∆v is the difference between resonance frequencies of the sites. Typically the NMR resonances of exchanging sites might differ by a few hertz to a few kilohertz, whereas in IR spectroscopy a difference in band position of only 1 cm-1 corresponds to a frequency separation of 3 * 1010 Hz! Clearly, even in very fast exchanging systems, IR will be able to distinguish each site whereas the NMR spectrum will be sensitive to quite slow motions.

For example, several isomers of Co2(CO)8 exist in solution, one of which adopts a bridged conformation, the other two all terminal conformations, figure 3.4a. The isomers interconvert by a fluxional process that exchanges the bridging and terminal the CO ligands. Not only is IR spectroscopy able to distinguish the resonances of each isomer but also the bridging and terminal ligands, figure 3.4b, whereas by NMR we see only a single band, figure 3.4c - all the CO ligands look the same due to the fluxional process. Clearly this process must be occurring at a rate much greater than the NMR, but less than the IR, timescale.

We can define NMR and IR timescales for exchange of around 10-7 s and 10-14 s respectively.

We can alter the rate of a dynamic process by varying the temperature - at low temperatures the process will be slowed down, we are likely to be in the slow exchange regime, whilst at high temperatures, the process will likely be fast, we are in the fast exchange regime. At intermediate temperatures the lines first broaden, then coalesce into a single broad peak which sharpens up as the fast exchange limit is reached. For example, (C5H5)2Fe2(CO)4 exists as cis and trans isomers, which interconvert in solution. At low temperature, separate resonances are observed in the lH NMR spectrum for the cyclopentadienyl ligands of the two isomers. As the temperature increases these first broaden, then coalesce until finally a single sharp resonance is observed, figure 3.5.

 

Concerted vs non-concerted exchange

Clearly some movement of the carbonyl ligands is also required by the isomerization described above. The l3CO NMR spectrum reveals that at high temperature all the CO ligands become equivalent, however Page 62, Chapter 3 Dynamic process and NMR equivalencing of all the CO’s is not required by the cis – trans isomerization – we can imagine an isomerization process in which the cyclopentadienyl and terminal CO ligands interchange by a localized rotation at a single iron centre, figure 3.6. Such a process does not exchange the bridge and terminal CO’s.

We could then envisage a second fluxional process in which the terminal and bridging CO’s interchange but leave the cyclopentadienyls unaffected, figure 3.7.

Together, these two processes could explain the lH and l3C NMR data at both high and low temperature. Since the processes are quite separate from each other they represent a non-concerted mechanism for the exchange.

Alternatively we can propose a concerted mechanism for the exchange in which cis – trans isomerization and interchange of terminal and bridging CO’s is intimately linked via an all-terminal intermediate, figure 3.8. Interchange between the bridged and terminal structures equivalences the carbonyl ligands and rotation about the iron – iron bond in the all-terminal intermediate provides a pathway for cis – trans isomerization.

Fortunately, analysis of changes in the NMR lineshapes with temperature allows us to extract the rates of the fluxional processes and, more importantly, ∆G‡, ∆H‡, and ∆S‡ for both the cis – trans isomerization and for the equivalencing of the carbonyl ligands and hence to decide between the concerted and non-concerted mechanisms.

Differential broadening

The rate at which each peak in the low temperature spectrum broadens depends on the rate of site leaving, equation 3.5, therefore the resonances of sites with low populations broaden first. This phenomenon is called differential broadening and can be seen in the spectra of figure 3.9. For example in the series of spectra labelled C, in which complexed lithium is the major species, the line of “free” lithium broadens significantly more than that of the complexed ion, whilst in series A, in which “free” lithium dominates, it is the complexed line that broadens first.

Multisite dynamic processes

Similar lineshape analysis can be performed on exchanging systems in which multisite exchange occurs. For example, exchange of all the carbonyl ligands in Rh4(CO)12 is proposed to occur by a fluxional process in which the bridges open and then reform about any one of the four tetrahedral faces - the “Cotton” merry-go-round, figure 3.11. Lineshape analysis of the variable temperature 13C NMR spectra of the cluster has confirmed this proposal and allowed the free enthalpy of activation, ∆H‡ = 42.8 ± 0.4 kJmol-1 at 298 K, to be determined.

3.4 Effect of dynamic processes on relaxation

Exchange not only affects the position and linewidth of resonances in the NMR spectrum, it also affects the observed relaxation times. This can be simply understood. Consider the equilibrium 3.10

(3.10)

where A and B are two exchanging sites having different T1’s and τ is the mean site lifetime. Clearly, the nuclear spins will spend some of the time in site A, relaxing at TlA and some of the time in site B, relaxing at TlB. When exchange is fast on the timescale of the relaxation, the apparent relaxation rate will be the weighted average of the two relaxation rates. This technique can be made quantitative when exchange is sufficiently slow for separate resonances for the two sites to be observed; even qualitative data can, however, be used to show the presence of exchange.

For example, exchange of chemisorbed hydrogen between the copper and oxide phases of a Cu/ZnO/alumina methanol synthesis catalyst was demonstrated by comparing the apparent relaxation rate of Page 68, Chapter 3 Dynamic process and NMRthe “hydrogen on copper sites” in two experiments, figure 3.12. In the first the spins of all the hydrogens (on copper and on the oxide phases) were inverted whilst in the second selective inversion of the spins of hydrogen on copper was used. The “true” relaxation time constant, T1, of the hydrogen on copper is obtained from the first experiment and was found to be ca. 50 ms whilst in the second experiment an additional relaxation pathway is available to the hydrogen on copper spins viz exchange with the fully relaxed hydrogen spins on the oxide phases. If exchange is occurring then the observed relaxation of the spins on copper should be faster. A Tlobs of 14 ms was found confirming that exchange does indeed occur.

3.5 Saturation transfer

An alternative strategy for obtaining rate constants is to work in the slow exchange regime and selectively excite the nuclei at one of the exchanging sites. Since NMR relaxation is a slow process (chapter 1.10) the excited nuclei will carry the excitation with them as they move from one site in the molecule to another and so the excitation Page 69, Chapter 3 Dynamic process and NMR will appear at each site the excited nucleus visits during the exchange process. This is much like heating up a cup of water then pouring the hot water into another cup - the water takes its heat with it. If there are several possible alternative cups, we can find out which one the hot water has been poured into by monitoring the temperature of each cup.

Monitoring exchange using saturation transfer

Dynamic processes involving cyclic polyene and polyenyl ligands such as cyclopentadiene, benzene, cycloheptatriene, cyclooctadiene etc have been much studied since these ligands may spin about the metal to ligand axis rather like a “plate on a stick” or may contain free olefinic bonds allowing the possibility of exchange between the free and bound carbon-carbon double bonds.

A consideration of the geometry of such an exchange suggests that suprafacial (i.e. over a face) shifts of the metal should be favoured, the metal moving smoothly over the surface of the ligand whilst it is difficult to imagine a pathway for antarfacial (i.e. from one face to the other) shifts that does not involve deco-ordination of the metal. Woodward-Hoffmann rules can then be used to predict the allowed shifts.

Consider the migration of the metal fragment around of the ƞ4-cyclooctatetraenyl group in (ƞ4-cot)Fe(CO)2(CNPri). Several possible migration pathways exist, e.g. a stepwise migration by [1, 2] shifts, or migration by [1, 3], [1, 4], [1, 5] etc shifts of which only the [1, 5] shift is Woodward-Hoffmann allowed, figure 3.13.

 

 

This prediction can be confirmed by saturating the resonance of a specific carbon atom in the cyclooctatetraene ligand. As the metal fragment migrates around the polyenyl ligand we can identify the sites visited by the excited carbon atom by following the appearance of the excitation, figure 3.14. The initial saturation, which appears as negative NMR intensity at carbon b’ ‘moves’ initially to carbons a, c’ and c” consistent only with the occurrence of a [1, 5] migration pathway.

3.6 Effect of exchange on spin-spin coupling

The effect of exchange on coupling differs between a fluxional process and an intermolecular exchange process.

Averaging of the coupling constant in an intramolecular process

In a fluxional process the ligand/migrating group is bound at all times to the molecule and hence the coupling is retained; the observed coupling constant is the weighted average of the individual couplings and the coupling pattern is that expected assuming all the coupling partner nuclei are seen.

Consider for example the 31P NMR spectrum of [Rh(PPh3)3]C1O4 in dichloromethane solution, figure 3.18. At low temperature this shows two resonances in the ratio 1:2 due to the unique and trans- phosphines respectively. As the temperature is increased, both resonances broaden and then coalesce to form a doublet. The preservation of lJ(Rh-P) at high temperatures indicates that the bonds between rhodium and the phosphine ligands are retained throughout consistent with a fast intra-molecular fluxional process for exchange of inequivalent tripheny lphosphines.

Page 74, Chapter 3 Dynamic processes and NMR

Exchange decoupling in an intermolecular process

In an intermolecular exchange process the bond between the exchanging group and the rest of the molecule breaks. Since coupling is transmitted through the bonds (see chapter 1.7) decoupling will occur when the exchange rate - the rate of bond breaking - is greater than the coupling constant. This phenomenon is known as exchange decoupling; the well known absence of coupling between the OH proton in methanol or ethanol and the other protons in the molecule is not due to the intervening oxygen atom but to rapid intermolecular exchange of this proton catalysed by traces of acid or base in the sample. If the alcohol is rigorously purified the expected couplings are seen in the 1H NMR spectrum.

Exchange decoupling can also be seen in the 31P and 13C NMR spectra of [Rh(PBu3)2(13CO)Cl] in the presence of dissolved l3CO, figure 3.19. In the absence of CO, figure 3.19a, the 13C NMR spectrum shows a well resolved doublet of triplets due to coupling to the two equivalent phosphines and 103Rh. Addition of traces of CO results in loss of the small coupling to phosphorus, figure 3.19b. Simplistically, the rate of bond breaking is greater than the phosphorus-carbon coupling constant so the coupling disappears, the phosphorus is exchange decoupled from the carbon. This is consistent with the on-set of intermolecular exchange of free and co-ordinated CO giving exchange decoupling and loss of 2JPC.

The larger rhodium-carbon coupling, however, is still present, even though it is the rhodium-carbon bond that is breaking, since the rate of bond cleavage does not yet exceed this coupling constant. At high CO concentration, exchange of free and co-ordinated CO becomes fast enough to decouple the rhodium and carbon nuclei and a singlet is seen, figure 3.19c. Similar changes are seen in the 31P NMR spectrum.

This result seems somewhat surprising given the relative positions of the various ligands in the trans effect series (we might have expected the chloride ligand to be most labile) and may reflect the high affinity for phosphorus ligands of rhodium and the inability of the reaction solvent, toluene, to support the cationic rhodium species, [Rh(PBu3)2(13CO)]+, that would result from chloride loss from the complex.

3.7 2-D methods for studying exchange

So far we have described variable temperature and 1-D saturation transfer methods for studying exchange. In those experiments we looked at relatively simple systems where the identification of the dynamic process was straightforward. However in more complex systems in which there are several, possibly interdependent exchange pathways, these 1-D methods may give ambiguous results or be excessively tedious to perform. For example, VT spectra may reveal the overall exchange but not allow individual ligand movements to be identified, figure 3.20, or many 1-D saturation transfer experiments, exciting each site in turn, may be needed to map out the exchange. It would clearly be advantageous if we could excite all sites simultaneously in a saturation transfer experiment. In effect this is what the 2-D EXSY (EXchange Spectroscopy) experiment achieves.

Page 76, Chapter 3 Dynamic processes and NMR

Exchange Spectroscopy

In the EXSY experiment we first excite all the nuclear spins in the sample (c.f. the 1-D saturation transfer in which we selectively excited the nuclear spin at a particular site), and then allow a mixing delay for exchange to occur before measuring the FID. The experiment is repeated using a series of mixing delays; in this way a second time dimension – mixing time – that contains information about the exchange processes that are occurring is generated. We can now do a Fourier transformation of the FID’s in the conventional time domain generating a series of conventional NMR spectra each differing only in the amount of exchange that has occurred and in the second time dimension created by the mixing delays. This second Fourier transformation reveals the exchange information in the spectra - we end up with a 2-D map in which (in addition to the diagonal peaks) peaks appear linking sites between which exchange is occurring. In this way the exact ligand movements involved can be determined.

Multisite exchange

In most of the examples above there were two sites involved in the exchange process; the cyclopentadienyl rings in (C5H5)2Fe2(CO)4 were either mutually cis or trans, or free and co-ordinated ligands were exchanged. However in many systems there are more than two sites involved in the fluxional process and it can be difficult, using variable temperature methods, to determine exactly which ligand moves where. EXSY is particularly useful in studying dynamic processes in complex systems involving multisite exchanges in which the exact atom movements cannot readily be obtained from conventional variable temperature measurements.

Virtual exchange

Virtual exchange occurs when a symmetry transformation, as a result of movement of some other ligand in the compound, equivalences two otherwise distinct sites. Virtual exchange is thus likely to occur in systems where multisite exchange is possible.

The variable temperature 13CO NMR spectra of Rh6(CO)15PPh3, figure 1.8, are shown in figure 3.20 (see figure 1.8, for the assignment of the resonances at 213 K). Clearly a fluxional process is occurring, however it is not obvious exactly which CO ligands are involved and which moves where.

The 13C EXSY spectra, figure 3.21, of the cluster shows correlations corresponding to the pairwise equivalencing of C(4)O with C(1)O, C(2)O with C(3)O, C(5)O with C(7)O and C(6)O with C(8) for the Page 77, Chapter 3 Dynamic process and NMR lowest energy scrambling process. The rates of each pairwise equivalencing can be evaluated from the crosspeak integrals and are equal indicating an overall concerted mechanism.
