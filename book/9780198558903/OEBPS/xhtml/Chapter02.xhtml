2 Structure Determination

2.1 Introduction

The last chapter presented the essential theory of NMR in solution together with a formalism to predict the appearance of the NMR spectrum for a given compound/spin system. Emphasis was placed on the fact that the appearance of the spectrum depended on the arrangement of nuclear spins and not on the particular elements present, thus the interpretation of NMR spectra follows the same rules whether we are studying an “organic” or “inorganic” compound; it is only the chemistry that changes not the NMR!

In this chapter we approach the subject from the other end; given the relevant NMR spectra can we deduce the structure of the compound? This of course is the problem that the practising chemist will usually encounter; the hope is that the understanding of the basic principles gained in the previous chapter will inform our chemist's interpretation of the spectra we meet in this.

In the interpretation of an NMR spectrum we are, of course, not interested solely in the topology of the molecule but also in obtaining as much information as possible about, e.g. oxidation state, co-ordination number, bond angles etc. For this reason this chapter also surveys the chemical factors that influence the chemical shift and the magnitude of coupling constants.

2.2 Interpretation of an NMR spectrum

Differentiation of chemical shifts and scalar couplings

The two most important effects in the NMR spectrum, from the point of view of structure determination, are the chemical shift and scalar couplings. The chemical shift results in a separate multiplet of resonances for each chemically different group of NMR active nuclei in the molecule, i.e. each group of chemically different nuclei will have its own resonance position determined by the chemical nature of its surroundings. Furthermore the intensity of the resonance tells us the number of nuclei responsible for each chemical shift. The structure within each multiplet results from scalar coupling and is determined by the number and arrangement of neighbouring (inequivalent) spins. Fortunately it is a simple matter to distinguish the two effects; the multiplet structure arising from scalar coupling is symmetrical about the Page 32, Chapter 2 Structure Determination midpoint of the multiplet whilst the chemical shift places resonances according to the chemical nature of the groups giving rise to each resonance. Thus, there will be no pattern, i.e. no symmetry, in the distribution of the chemical shifts other than that determined by the chemistry. Using the symmetry present in a scalar coupled multiplet we can easily distinguish scalar couplings from chemical shifts and hence arrive at the number of chemically distinct groups of NMR active nuclei in the molecule.

Consider the 19F NMR spectrum shown in figure 2.1, that of a compound that contains fluorine, phosphorus, and alkyl groups, PxFyRz. The spectrum contains ten lines, however we notice that six of the lines give a symmetrical pattern, recognizable as a doublet of triplets, about a chemical shift of 88 ppm. The remaining four lines also give a symmetrical pattern, a doublet of doublets, around 5 ppm. Since we can identify two sets of symmetrical resonances we know that the compound must contain two chemically different types of fluorine atoms. We can use the intensities of the multiplets to tell us the relative number of fluorines in each group. The total intensity of the four line pattern is twice that of the six line therefore there must be twice as many fluorine atoms in the former group as in the latter.

Determination of couplings and coupling patterns

Scalar coupling is reciprocal, that is the magnitude of the coupling between two nuclei A and B must be the same in the spectrum of A as it is in B. This allows us to determine easily which resonances come from mutually coupled groups of nuclei, we simply measure the coupling constants and look for two that are the same in different multiplets. We must, of course, remember that in the NMR spectra of inorganic Page 33, Chapter 2 Structure Determination compounds the coupling partner may be a nucleus of a different element – a heteronuclear coupling – and so appear in a different NMR spectrum.

Looking at the 19F NMR spectrum of figure 2.1 we notice that the separation of adjacent lines in each branch of the doublet of triplets is 26 Hz and is the same as the separation of the lines in each branch of the doublet of doublets. Immediately we can see that the 26 Hz coupling relates the two multiplets and so must be the coupling between the fluorines. We also notice that the intensities of the outside two lines of the triplet are in the ratio 1:2 confirming that the coupling is due to two equivalent neighbouring spins – the other fluorines. In fact, the separation of the outside two lines of a multiplet must correspond to a coupling constant and the relative intensity of the outside line to the next line must equal the number of coupling partners responsible for this coupling. These are useful rules when the coupling pattern is not immediately obvious, however it does not hold in coupling in second order systems (vide chapter 1.8).

The large doublet coupling in the resonance centred at 88 ppm is 968 Hz whilst in the resonance at 5 ppm it is 780 Hz. Clearly these couplings do not link the two multiplets.

So far we can deduce that the compound contains two types of fluorine in the ratio 2:1, that the fluorines are close enough to each other to be mutually coupled, and that both types of fluorine see coupling to another spin ½ nucleus. Since the coupling partner is clearly not in the 19F NMR spectrum we can guess that it must be phosphorus.

The 31P{1H} NMR spectrum, figure 2.2, shows several lines around 8 ppm. The multiplet structure is less clear than that in the 19F NMR spectrum, figure 2.1, however the whole pattern is symmetrical indicating that it arises from scalar couplings on a single phosphorus chemical shift; i.e. there is only one type of phosphorus in the molecule. We know that the separation of the outside two lines must equal a Page 34, Chapter 2 Structure Determinationcoupling constant, here 780 Hz, and that the ratio of the two outside lines, here 1:2, tells us the number of coupling partners responsible for this, i.e. the 780 Hz, coupling. We can deduce that the 780 Hz coupling is to a group of two identical nuclei. Inspection of the 19F NMR spectrum reveals a 780 Hz coupling is also associated with the 19F resonance centred at 5 ppm so these fluorines must be the coupling partners.

By looking for this coupling recurring in the 31P multiplet we can identify two 1:2:1 triplets, lines 1,2 and 4 and 3,5 and 6. Having identified the triplets, the multiplet structure is seen to be a doublet (968 Hz) of triplets (780 Hz), the 968 Hz coupling being due to the other fluorine, δF = 88 ppm, in the molecule.

We now know the relative numbers of phosphorus and fluorine atoms in the molecule. Each fluorine resonance is split into a doublet by the phosphorus so each set of fluorines sees one phosphorus; there is only one phosphorus chemical shift so this must be the same phosphorus, hence the molecule must contain only one phosphorus atom. This in turn fixes the number of fluorine atoms in the molecule as three, two of one type and one of another. (It is also possible that the molecule might be a polymer of some sort containing isolated, but identical {P(Fa)2(Fb)} units.)

The common oxidation states of phosphorus are III and V; since we know the phosphorus has alkyl substituents as well as the three fluorines we must have a P(V) compound and we might guess that there are two alkyl groups, i.e. the molecule is PF3R2, figure 2.3. The nature of the alkyl groups could be determined by a combination of 1H and 13C NMR however it is impractical to use couplings to 13C in the 31P or I9F NMR spectra to determine the number of alkyl groups since 13C is only 1% abundant - the multiplets due to coupling to 13C will appear as satellites on the main peak and will contain only 1% of the intensity in the spectrum (see chapter 1.9). The type and number of alkyl groups would usually be determined another way, for example by microanalysis, whilst mass spectroscopy could be used to determine the molecular mass (and hence whether the molecule is a monomer or polymer).

The probability that any given phosphorus is adjacent to one 119Sn is the natural abundance of 119Sn times the number of tin atoms adjacent to the phosphorus = 8.6% * 2 = 17.2%. The chances that given phosphorus is adjacent to two 119Sn nuclei is 8.6% * 8.6% = 0.7%.This is so small it can be ignored. Thus we would expect to see a doublet for the tin satellites on each phosphorus resonance. The intensity of each line in the doublet is 17.2% divided by 2 = 8.6%.

Satellites

We saw in chapter 1.9 that satellites occur as a result of scalar coupling when the coupling partner is less than 100% abundant. Satellites, since they contain all the coupling information, can also be used to obtain structural information from the NMR spectrum. For example the 119Sn{7Li} and 31P{7Li} NMR spectra of [Sn4(Pcy3)6Li4] (cy = cyclohexyl) are shown in figure 2.4. The satellites due to coupling to tin are clearly visible in the phosphorus spectrum.

2.3 General trends in the chemical shift

Several factors, for example co-ordination number, oxidation state, and geometry influence the shielding of the nucleus and hence the chemical shift in inorganic complexes. Although it is possible, by carefully chosen examples where a single factor dominates the shielding, to establish trends for the effects of these factors on the chemical shift, it is difficult to establish general rules for the chemical shifts of nuclei in inorganic compounds since many interdependent factors, often having competing affects on the shift, are involved. Shifts can be very large – up to 10 000 (or more) ppm in metal complexes. This is perhaps unsurprising given the often strong interactions of the metal electrons with the ligands.

It is worth reiterating at this point that an increase in shielding, σ, results in a decrease in the chemical shift, δ, i.e. in a shift upfield, i.e. to less positive/more negative δ values.

Factors influencing the chemical shift – geometry

We have already seen that geometric factors can make otherwise equivalent nuclei distinct, e.g. the axial and equatorial fluorines in the PF3R2 example above. Other such geometric factors might be cis vs trans or mer vs fac isomerism in a complex or the overall symmetry present or Page 36, Chapter 2 Structure Determinationabsent in the compound, figure 2.5. It is therefore important that we can recognize when geometry makes, say, the ligands in a complex different. A useful starting point is to look at the group trans to the ligand of interest, thus the triphenylphosphine ligand trans to chloride in Wilkinson's catalyst, figure 2.6, is clearly different to the two mutually trans triphenylphosphines. Thus, there will be two phosphorus chemical shifts in the 31P NMR spectrum of Wilkinson's catalyst of intensity ratio 2:1 and the resonances will show coupling to rhodium and between the dissimilar phosphines.

In some compounds it may simply be the spatial arrangement that makes otherwise identical ligands different. For example, in Fe3(CO)12, figure 2.7, we can easily see that the two bridging carbonyls will be distinct from the ten terminal CO ligands and by considering the differences between the iron atoms (Fea is unique whilst the two CO bridged atoms Feb are equivalent) we can see that the terminal carbonyls on Fea must be different from those on Feb since the iron atoms are different. Two of the carbonyls on Fea, (C2O), lie in the Fe3 plane and are thus distinct from the two ligands, (C1O), that lie in symmetry equivalent positions above and below the Fe3 plane. In a similar way we can divide the terminal CO ligands on Feb into two groups, C3O lying in the plane whilst C4O lie above and below the Fe3 plane. Thus there are four distinct terminal CO environments and one type of bridging CO in Fe3(CO)12.

Factors influencing the chemical shift – electronegativity, charge, and oxidation state

All of these factors affect the electron density around the nucleus and so affect the chemical shift. The shielding generally becomes smaller, i.e. the chemical shift increases, as the electron density at the nucleus decreases. Thus electronegative substituents, positive charge or an increase in oxidation state all (usually) result in downfield shifts.

Co-ordination shifts for a ligand are usually small – a few tens of ppm – and can be of either sign. 2.4 General trends in the coupling constant

Just as we have seen for the chemical shift, many, often competing, factors affect the size of coupling constants. Nevertheless some general trends can be found, and the magnitude of the coupling constant can give useful chemical and structural information when variations in coupling constant between related complexes are considered.

An important point that must be borne in mind when discussing variations in coupling constants is that couplings can have positive or negative signs, however most chemists report only the magnitude of the coupling. An increase in the value of a negative coupling constant will result in a decrease in the magnitude of the coupling.

Typical values for scalar coupling constants are given in Brevard's book – a valuable ready reference guide to the magnitude of scalar couplings and chemical shifts.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – gyromagnetic ratio

For a given pair of elements, the scalar coupling is proportional to the product of the gyromagnetic ratios of the nuclei provided all other factors remain the same. Thus, where two NMR active isotopes of a given element exist, e.g. 10B and 11B, the ratio of the coupling constants to a third nucleus will be as the ratio of the gyromagnetic ratios, table 1.2. For example, in BH4-, 1J(10BH) is 28 Hz whilst lJ(11BH) is 80 Hz, figure 1.22. (The gyromagnetic ratios of 10B and 11B are 2.87 and 8.58 *107 rad T-1 s-1 respectively.) However when we move from one element to another, other factors also change e.g. the nature of the bonding, thus 1JPH is Ca. 200 - 500 Hz whereas 1JPPt is several thousand hertz despite the gyromagnetic ratio of the proton being roughly five times that of 195Pt.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – periodicity

The magnitude of the reduced coupling constant, eqn 2.1, increases down a group. For example, KEC increases from 8.3 to 18.0 to 30.0 to 39.3 *1020 NA-2m-3 in the tetramethyl complexes of Group 14 metals SiMe4, GeMe4, SnMe4, PbMe4. Note that the more familiar J varies (apparently Page 42, Chapter 2 Structure Determination randomly) from -50 to -19 to -340 to 250 Hz, illustrating the benefit of using the reduced coupling constant when comparing couplings between different pairs of nuclei.

K X Y = 4 π 2 J X Y / h γ X γ Y

Eqn 2.1 A reduced coupling constant, K, can be defined that removes the influence of the gyromagnetic ratios allowing comparison of coupling constants between different pairs of nuclei. K has units NA-2m-3.

Two bond coupling constants generally increase as the intervening atom moves down a group. For example, the 2JSnESn in cyclic heterotristannanes, figure 2.13, increases from 198 to 234 to 247 Hz as E descends the group from S to Se to Te. Note also the increasing shielding of the tin nuclei as the electronegativity of the substituents decreases.

As always some care is needed if only the magnitude (but not the sign) of the coupling constant is reported, e.g. although 2JPH in the series of olefin hydroformylation catalysts HM(CO)(PPh3)3, M = Co, Rh, Ir are reported to be in the order Co > Rh < Ir, figure 2.14, it is probable that the coupling constant in the cobalt complex is negative.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – s-character in the bond

We saw in chapter 1.7 that coupling between nuclei is transmitted via the bonding electrons rather than directly through space. Clearly for coupling to occur the bonding electrons must be able to sense, or contact, the nucleus. Only s-electrons have a finite density at the nucleus so it should come as no surprise that the amount of s-character in the valence hybrids used to form the chemical bond plays an important role in determining the strength of the coupling. Percentage s-character will be influenced by factors such as hybridization, co-ordination number, the electronegativity of substituents, interbond angles, the nature of the trans-ligand, oxidation state, etc.

Factors influencing the coupling constant– hybridization

The formal hybridization of the nuclei affects the coupling constant; the coupling (which is transmitted by the bonding electrons) increases with increasing s-character in the bond. For example, PtC coupling constants increase from 450 - 700 Hz in platinum alkyl complexes (sp3 hybridized Page 43, Chapter 2 Structure Determination carbon) to 750 - 850 Hz in carbene complexes (sp2 carbon) to 990 - 2250 Hz in terminal platinum carbonyls (sp carbon). This effect is also illustrated in the unusual complex shown in figure 2.15, (PMe3)2W(≡CCMe3)(=CHCMe3)(-CH2Me3), which contains an alkyl (sp3 carbon), alkylidene (sp2 carbon) and alkylidyne (sp carbon) ligand. The tungsten-carbon coupling constants are 80, 120 and 210 Hz respectively.

Similarly, in rhodium carbonyl cluster compounds JRhC falls from ca 70 Hz for terminal carbonyl ligands (sp) to 25 Hz for face bridging ligands (sp3), figure 2.16.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – co-ordination number

Increasing co-ordination number leads to a decrease in coupling constant, e.g. SiF4 (JSiF 170 Hz), SiF5- (JSiF 140 Hz), SiF62- (JSiF 108 Hz) and in [Ag(P{OEt}3)n]X, JAgP falls from 794 to 535 to 402 to 342 as n increases from 1 to 4. This might easily be explained by changes in the hybridization of central atom; the amount of s-character in the bond to any given ligand decreases as the number of ligands increases.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – electronegativity

Coupling constants increase with increasing electronegativity of the substituents in line with a (presumed) increase in s-character in the bond. For example the selenium-phosphorus coupling in organophosphorus selenides, R3PSe, increases as the electronegativity of the substituents on phosphorus increases, Me < Ph < OMe, table 2.5.

Note also the general trend of increasing downfield shift of the phosphorus nucleus as the electronegativity/electron withdrawing power of the substituent increases (see section 2.3, p. 40). The reason for the reverse trend observed for the selenium chemical shift is unclear and has been attributed to an increase in electron density on selenium as phosphorus substituent electronegativity increases, figure 2.17.

Similar effects are observed in couplings involving transition elements. For example 1JPtP in cis-PtCl2(PBu3)2 is 3508 Hz and in cis-PtCl2{P(OEt)3}2 is 5698 Hz. Interestingly, the ratio of the phosphine and phosphite coupling constants is fairly independent of the nature of the acceptor; being 1.62 in these platinum complexes and 1.53 in the borane complexes Me3P-BH3, 1JBP 64 Hz, and (MeO)3P-BH3, 97 Hz indicating that the ratio of the phosphine-acceptor to phosphite-acceptor coupling constants is determined by the phosphorus s-character in the valence bond.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – trans- influence

The magnitude of couplings between the ligand and acceptor is strongly influenced by the other ligands in the complex. This effect is particularly evident in square planar complexes of the noble metals, π-acid ligands in the trans position reduce the magnitude of the coupling between the metal and the ligand. Thus, in the series of complexes shown in figure Page 45, Chapter 2 Structure Determination2.18, 1JRhP falls from nearly 200 Hz in the trans-chloro complex (π-donor) through 140 Hz in the trans-phosphine complex to only 95 Hz in the trans-carbonyl complex (strong π-acceptor).

However, the explanation for this effect cannot simply be the π interaction with the metal since hydride ligands also have a high trans-influence. In fact, the explanation, once again, is the degree of s-character in the bond and the s-electron density at the nucleus which is least for π-acid ligands and for hydride.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – interbond angles

The s-character in the bonds between the coupling nuclei is expected to vary with bond angle; simplistically a bond angle of 90° is obtained using pure p orbitals; as the bond angle increases, increasing s character must be added to the bonding hybrid orbital 109° (sp3), 120° (sp2), 180° (sp). We would expect, therefore, that the magnitude of a coupling constant should correlate with the interbond angle(s) between the coupling nuclei, i.e. for two bond couplings, trans couplings will normally be larger than cis couplings. Thus for example the coupling between the bridging phosphido ligand and the trans phosphine in the palladium hydride complex shown in figure 2.19 is ca. 213 Hz whereas the coupling to the cis phosphine is ca. 0 Hz. Similar trends are seen in the phosphorus-proton couplings being 74 Hz when the phosphine ligand is trans but only 14 to 21 Hz when the phosphorus ligand is cis, figure 2.19. With experience, typical values for cis and trans couplings for any given pair of nuclei can be learnt and become useful in assigning the geometry of new complexes.

For three bond couplings, not only are the interbond angles important but also the dihedral angle, ϕ, between the planes containing the coupling nuclei and the bonds between them; the dihedral angle dependence of Page 46, Chapter 2 Structure Determination three bond couplings is expressed in the Karplus equation, eq. 2.2, which predicts that 3J will normally be greatest when ϕ is close to 0° or 180°.

3J=Ccos2ϕ+Bcosϕ+A

Eqn 2.2 The Karplus equation describes empirically the dependence of the coupling constant on the dihedral angle. B is usually negative and | C | > | B | thus 3J is usually greatest when the coupling partners are trans, ϕ = 180°.

Factors influencing the coupling constant – lone pairs

The presence of lone pairs usually makes the coupling constant more negative however, when the lone pair is used in co-ordination the contribution from the lone pair becomes positive. We would therefore expect the magnitude of the coupling constant to decrease on coordination. For example, the lJPSe in free Ph3PSe is -655 Hz becoming -585 Hz on co-ordination to cadmium. Similarly the magnitude of 1JPSe IN Bu3PSe falls on co-ordination to HgX2. 2.5 Relaxation and structure

So far we have concentrated on using the chemical shift and scalar couplings to determine the molecular structure. Another important effect in NMR that can give useful structural information is relaxation. At times relaxation effects can be a nuisance, for example, the widely differing relaxation rates of primary, secondary, tertiary and quaternary carbons prohibit the use of line intensities in 13C{1H} NMR as a guide to the number of each type of carbon atom in a molecule. On the other hand the nuclear Overhauser effect, nOe, which uses through space relaxation of one nucleus by its neighbours – the stronger the nOe the closer the two nuclei must be is widely used in organic chemistry and in protein chemistry to determine molecular conformation. On rare occasions, however, a strong nOe can be misleading as in the dimanganese compound shown in figure 2.23 in which the strong nOe from the directly bonded proton in the diphenylphosphine ligand doubles the intensity of the 31P{1H} resonance of this ligand with respect to that of the phosphido bridge.

Molecular hydrogen complexes

Kubas' report in the early 1980's of a neutron diffraction crystallographic structure determination of the complex W(H2)(CO)3(PiPr3)2 in which molecular hydrogen acts as a ligand caused great excitement since up till then it had been believed that such complexes could not exist. In fact (and with the benefit of hindsight) it is now known that many polyhydride complexes synthesized in the years before Kubas' report are in fact molecular hydrogen complexes.

Characterizing molecular hydrogen ligands in transition metal complexes poses certain difficulties: due to its low atomic number hydrogen is difficult to locate in X-ray diffraction crystallographic studies in the presence of a strongly diffracting transition metal; neutron diffraction studies require large crystals and access to a high energy neutron source; the H-H bond stretch comes in the range 3100 to 2400 cm-1 – a region in which C-H bonds also absorb. The explosion of studies into molecular hydrogen ligands relied heavily on a wide variety of NMR techniques to characterize the compounds and provides an interesting review of the techniques available.

Both molecular hydrogen and classical hydride ligands resonate upfield of TMS. Unfortunately the chemical shift ranges of the two types of ligand overlap – the shifts of molecular hydrogen ligands range between 0 and -15 ppm, and of classical hydrides between -2 and -50 ppm. For example the 1H NMR spectrum of a mixture of the tautomers W(CO)3[P(i-C3D7)3]2(H2), A, and W(CO)3[P(i-C3D7)3]2(H)2, B, is shown in figure 2.24. The inequivalent hydrides of the classical tautomer appear as a pseudo-triplet and a doublet of doublets at ca. δ -2.4 and δ -4.5 ppm Page 50, Chapter 2 Structure Determinationdue to coupling to the two inequivalent phosphine ligands. The broad resonance of the molecular hydrogen ligand overlaps the resonance of one of the two inequivalent hydrides of the classical complex. Clearly chemical shift alone is insufficient to distinguish the two types of ligand.

If we replace the H2 by HD in the molecular hydrogen complex then we could use the scalar coupling between the deuterium and hydrogen to distinguish the two tautomers since we would expect the one bond HD coupling in A to be larger than the two bond coupling in B. Typically, 1JHD is between 25 and 34 Hz in molecular hydrogen complexes whereas 2JHD in classical hydrides is less than 5 Hz. Figure 2.25 shows the 1H NMR spectrum of W(CO)3[P(i-C3D7)3]2(HD) in which the 1:1:1 triplet expected for coupling to 2D is clearly seen. The coupling constant is 33.5 Hz confirming the presence of a molecular hydrogen ligand.

Another method of distinguishing the two types of ligand was hinted at in figure 2.24. The 1H NMR resonance of the molecular hydrogen ligand is very broad. In chapter 1, section 1.10, we noted that dipole-dipole relaxation falls away as r-6, and increases as the product of the squares of the gyromagnetic ratios of the relaxing pair of nuclei. In molecular hydrogen ligands the two protons are bound to each other i.e. the H - H distance is pretty much as short as possible. Furthermore, protons have one of the largest of all nuclear spin magnetic moments. The combination of these two factors results in very efficient relaxation of the two protons in a molecular hydrogen ligand. Efficient spin-spin relaxation accounts for the observed large line width. Unfortunately other factors, such as chemical exchange and fluxional processes, see chapter 3, can also cause broadening of NMR lines so a broad line is not enough to distinguish unequivocally molecular hydrogen from classical hydride complexes. Fortunately no such ambiguity arises if we use spin-lattice relaxation to distinguish the two tautomers. T1 for the protons of a molecular hydrogen ligand will clearly be much faster than that for a classical hydride, figure 2.26. Care is still needed in the interpretation of these results since inspection of figure 1.26 and equation 1.8 clearly shows that T1 is both temperature, through the effect of temperature on the molecular correlation time, and field, through the terms in co, dependent. To eliminate these effects it is essential that T1 is measured at the same spectrometer magnetic field and that the value at the T1, minimum is determined. When this is done spin-lattice relaxation times of less than 10 ms (at 5.875 T, i.e. 1H = 250 MHz) indicate a molecular hydrogen ligand whilst T1's greater than 100 ms indicate classical hydrides.

Spectral editing using relaxation times

The difference in relaxation times can be used to resolve the overlapping resonances of the molecular hydrogen and classical hydride ligands in the two tautomers of W(CO)3[P(i-C3D7)3]2H2, figure 2.24. Inspection of figure 1.38 reveals that after application of a 180° pulse, the magnetization relaxes back along the z-axis passing through zero. The rate at which relaxation occurs is, of course, related to T1. If we invert the magnetization of all the protons in a mixture of W(CO)3[P(i-C3D7)3]2(H2) and W(CO)3[P(i-C3D7)3]2(H)2, the resonances of the protons of the molecular hydrogen ligand, which are relaxing ten times faster than those of the classical hydrides, will pass through zero whilst those of the classical hydrides are still large (and negative). If the 1H NMR spectrum of the excited system is measured at this point, there will be no resonance due to the molecular hydrogen ligand – its magnetization is zero – and a strong set of resonances, of negative intensity, for the classical hydride ligands, figure 2.27.

Hydrogen on a metal surface

A similar problem to distinguishing molecular and classical hydride ligands in complexes exists if we adsorb hydrogen on a metal surface – does the hydrogen adsorb dissociatively, i.e. as atoms, or non-dissociatively as molecules? This apparently trivial question was a cause of furious debate throughout the 1970's and 80's for the industrially important case of hydrogen adsorption on copper based methanol synthesis catalysts.

The dissociative chemisorption of hydrogen on copper is highly activated; although early studies had indicated that dissociative chemisorption occurred readily on evaporated copper films, later ultrahigh vacuum measurements and theoretical calculations showed that the sticking probability for dissociative chemisorption of hydrogen on clean, polycrystalline copper surfaces is very low – a result that was frequently mis-interpreted as meaning that dissociative chemisorption on copper did not and/or could not occur.

A resonance, assigned to hydrogen adsorbed on metallic copper, is observed in the region 80 to 95 ppm when a variety of oxide supported copper catalysts are exposed to H2 or HD, figure 2.28. This shift, called a Knight shift, results from contact of the hydrogen nuclear spin with the metal conduction electrons and is consistent with a bonding interaction between metallic copper and hydrogen.

As we have seen above, a simple test to distinguish molecular from atomic hydrogen is the presence or absence of an intramolecular dipole - dipole contribution to the nuclear spin relaxation, such an interaction being expected to dominate the relaxation in molecular hydrogen due to Page 52, Chapter 2 Structure Determinationthe shortness of the H-H bond. Direct measurement of the spin-lattice relaxation time could not be used to distinguish atomic from molecular hydrogen in this case since, obviously, it was not known what the expected relaxation times for the two species absorbed on copper should be. Instead the spin-lattice relaxation times for hydrogen ex H2 and ex HD on supported copper were determined. 2.6 Multiple pulse methods

The presence of a nuclear spin on the metal is a great help in characterizing inorganic compounds by NMR through the couplings that result between the ligand and the metal. However, the sensitivity of some metal nuclei in the NMR experiment is very low making direct detection difficult, whilst for others the low natural abundance makes it difficult to detect the satellites that result from coupling. Thus we can divide the NMR active metals into the good, the bad, and the ugly on the basis of Page 53, Chapter 2 Structure Determination how easy it is to obtain useful structural information either via scalar couplings/satellites in the ligand spectra or from direct observation of the metal spectra. Thus 107Ag, 109Ag, 113Cd, 119Sn, 195Pt and 199Hg are easily observed. 103Rh is particularly useful through its couplings (100% natural abundance) but its low sensitivity makes direct detection difficult. 57Fe, 99Ru, 183W and 187Os can be used although the low abundance causes problems whilst 49Ti, 51V, 91Zr and 59Co 201Bi would be very useful but for the large quadrupole broadening that, except than in a few special cases, washes out most of the structural information.

Fortunately, it is now possible to use multiple pulse techniques to enhance the NMR spectra of insensitive nuclei that are coupled to sensitive spins such as 1H and 31P. Even 13C can be used as the sensitive nucleus if the compound is enriched in 13C.

1-D methods

1-D multiple pulse experiments, of which INEPT (Insensitive Nucleus Enhancement by Polarization Transfer), figure 2.30, and its derivatives are perhaps the most used, selectively transfer energy (transfer polarization) in a coupled spin system between the sensitive, S, and insensitive, I, spins in such a way that the population difference between the lower and upper levels is increased, figure 2.31.

We saw in chapter 1.4, that the intensity of the NMR signal depends on this population difference hence the INEPT experiment increases the intensity of NMR signal of the insensitive nucleus. The increase in intensity is equal to the ratio of the gyromagnetic ratios of the two nuclei, Page 54, Chapter 2 Structure Determinationtable 1.2. Thus the intensity of signals from 59Fe or 103Rh can be increased 30-fold on transfer of polarization from 1H using INEPT. Furthermore, using the refocussed INEPT experiment, the resulting NMR spectrum looks exactly like a conventional NMR spectrum; all couplings are seen allowing the number of ligands (containing an NMR active donor atom) attached to the metal nucleus to be determined. Care must however be taken in using intensity information from the INEPT family of experiments since this no longer depends simply on the number of nuclei present but on the efficiency and degree of enhancement.

Detection of both 103Rh and 15N via INEPT has been used recently to characterize a new class of alkene hydrogenation catalysts based on rhodium that include phosphine and hydrazine ligands, figure 2.32. The presence of coupling between rhodium and nitrogen conclusively demonstrated that the active species in the catalysis is not Wilkinson's catalyst, RhCl(PPh3)3.

2-D NMR spectroscopy

Chemical systems are often complex, for example there may be extensive coupling between the nuclei, the resonances of interest may be hidden by more intense nuclei or chemical exchange may be occurring. It would clearly be helpful if we could probe the nuclear spin system in a way that highlighted the information we are interested in. This is what 2-D NMR spectroscopy seeks to do. The basis of 2-D spectroscopy is beyond the scope of this book and readers are referred to the texts in the bibliography. In essence 2-D experiments work by recording a series of one dimensional FID's. In each one dimensional experiment the nuclear spin system is prepared by a series of excitation pulses and delays to ensure that the FID will be modulated by the NMR property of interest Page 55, Chapter 2 Structure Determination(e.g. exchange, or coupling), figure 2.33. One of the delays in the excitation sequence (for example, the time allowed for exchange to occur in an EXSY experiment) is sequentially increased (incremented) between one 1-D experiment and the next. In this way we create two time dimensions, one is “real” the acquisition time of the 1-D FID, (called F2), the other, (called F1), is “synthesized” by the incrementing delay in the preparation sequence, figure 2.34. We can now do Fourier transformations in both time dimensions to produce a two dimensional map in which crosspeaks occur that relate the chemical shifts in the 1-D spectrum to the property selected by the pulse sequence in the second.

There are many 2-D experiments available of which the most interesting to the inorganic chemist are: correlated spectroscopy or COSY; heteronuclear multiple quantum coherence or HMQC; and exchange spectroscopy or EXSY.

COSY

A COSY spectrum can be looked on, at least as far as the information it contains, as a two dimensional version of a 1D selective decoupling experiment. Crosspeaks appear in the COSY spectrum that link coupled nuclei. COSY spectra are useful in assigning couplings and are Page 56, Chapter 2 Structure Determinationfrequently used in this way in “Organic” chemistry. By selecting for one bond couplings crosspeaks will appear that link adjacent sites in a molecule thus COSY spectroscopy can also be used to determine the topology of a molecule.

Consider for example, the carbaborane clusters 1, 2-C2B10H12 and 1, 7-C2B10H12 that we met in chapter 1.10. In the 1, 2 isomer B(3) and B(6) are adjacent to both carbons and are equivalent, borons 4, 5, 7, and 11 are adjacent to one of the carbons and are equivalent, borons 9 and 12 are diametrically opposite a carbon and are equivalent and B(8) is equivalent to B(10). In the 1, 7 isomer however, B(2) and B(3) are adjacent to both carbons and are equivalent, borons 4, 6, 8, and 11 are adjacent to one carbon and one of B(2) or B(3) and are equivalent, B(5) and B(12) are opposite to one the carbon atoms and are equivalent and B(9) and B(10) are equivalenced by symmetry. In each case the 1D spectra show the predicted four resonances in the ratio 2:2:4:2, figure 1.27, but it is not possible to assign the resonances to particular atoms. The atom connectivities do however allow the isomers to be differentiated and the spectra to be assigned. Thus, for example, in the 1, 7 isomer, boron atomsPage 57, Chapter 2 Structure Determination (2, 3) are linked to B(4, 6, 8, l1) but no others whereas in the 1, 2 isomer all borons see at least two other boron environments. We can see these connectivities in the 1, 7 isomer displayed in the COSY spectrum in figure 2.35. A knowledge of the atom connectivities derived from the COSY spectrum can be a great aid in deducing the structure of the new boranes.

HMQC

HMQC uses the coupling between a sensitive, S and an insensitive, I, nucleus to observe indirectly the NMR spectrum of the insensitive nucleus through the effect its couplings have on the spectrum of the sensitive nucleus. The two dimensional spectrum produced gives the chemical shifts of the sensitive (i.e. the directly observed) nucleus in the F2 dimension (horizontal axis) whilst the vertical axis (F1 dimension) relates to the insensitive (i.e. indirectly observed) nucleus. One disadvantage of HMQC is that only I spins that couple to an S spin are detected, however, HMQC is still preferable to the alternative of observing no signals at all for the insensitive nucleus by “conventional” methods. Couplings to third nuclei are observed in the HMQC spectrum thus, for example, coupling to 3lP is observed in both F1 and F2 in the {13C, 103Rh} HMQC experiments described below.
