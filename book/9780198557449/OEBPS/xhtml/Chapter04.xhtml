4 Solvent effects on reaction rates

4.1 Introduction

The solvent may have several roles in a chemical reaction: the provision of a reaction medium, solvation of the reaction components, and direct involvement as a reactant. At the simplest level, the presence of the solvent alters both the frequency and pattern of collisions between the solute molecules compared with the gas phase. The kinetic energy of the molecules is approximately the same in solution as in the gas phase (at the same temperature), but there are two modifications with respect to the collision frequency.

(i) The free space between the solvent molecules amounts to only approximately one quarter of the total volume, assuming close-packed spheres. This tends to increase the collision frequency, and the effect is maximal when r(solute) ≪ r(solvent)—at the other extreme the effect vanishes.

(ii) The grouping of the collisions is different, as illustrated in Fig. 4.1. In solution, collisions tend to be repeated, with multiple collisions occurring between the same two molecules. These sets of collisions, usually referred to as encounters, arise because the solvent molecules are inclined to form a ‘cage’ surrounding the colliding pair, holding them for a period until they diffuse apart.

For the fastest reactions, the so-called diffusion-controlled reactions, which occur at every collision (Ch. 5), the encounter number is of prime significance. The majority of reactions, however, involve an activation energy, and it is the total number of collisions which is relevant; this number is substantially the same in the gas phase and in solution.

Of much greater importance for reactions in solution are the interactions between the solute molecules or ions and the solvent molecules, with which they are always in intimate contact. Solvent-solute interactions are responsible for the large and even spectacular changes in reaction rates which often accompany changes in solvent. Reactions involving ionic species are particularly sensitive to solvent, because of the very large magnitudes of ion-solvent interaction energies. These amount to several hundred kJ mol-1 even for monovalent ions.

4.2 Transition-state theory

The potential energy of interacting molecules during collision varies with the relative positions of the various nuclei, and the dependence of energy upon the Page 45, Solvent effects on reaction ratescoordinates of the nuclei for any particular reaction system can be represented by a potential energy surface. The exact calculation of potential energy surfaces remains difficult, but within any such surface there will be a minimum energy pathway between reactants and products, the so-called reaction pathway. The energy changes along this pathway are illustrated schematically in Fig. 4.2 for the reaction of Cl- with CH3I, in which the starting and end-regions correspond to Cl- being far removed from CH3I, and I- being far removed from CH3Cl, respectively. The small region around the position of maximum energy between reactants and products is known as the transition state, and a system in this region, represented by [Cl...CH3...I]-‡, is known as the activated complex or the transition-state complex.

The fundamental assumption of transition-state theory is that there is an equilibrium between reactants and the activated complex, X‡,

A+B ⇌ K ‡ X ‡ → products (4.8)

The rate of reaction is given by the rate of crossing the transition state, i.e., the concentration of transition-state complexes (X‡ = K‡AB)m ultiplied by the frequency with which the complexes cross the barrier. It can be shown from this that the rate constant, k, is given by eqn 4.9

k=( k B T /h ) K ‡ (4.9)

where kB is the Boltzmann constant, h the Planck constant, and T the absolute temperature. This simple and general equation predicts that the rate constant is a product of an equilibrium constant and a universal frequency factor, kBT/h, which has the dimensions of (time)-1.

A full derivation of the equations of transition-state theory and a discussion of the underlying assumptions is given by Moore and Pearson (1981).

Equation 4.9 provides the simplest basis for interpreting the effect of solvent on reaction rates, and in the following sections we consider the application of transition-state theory to some important reaction types. The energies involved are strikingly large and very sensitive to ion size and charge. Detailed experimental and theoretical studies of ions in the gas phase suggests that the major part of the energy comes from electrostatic interactions between ions and the first 1-2 layers of solvent molecules surrounding them. The very high energies required to form gaseous ions provides an immediate explanation for the chief difference between reactions in solution and the gas phase, namely the participation or not of ions.

The energies corresponding to these changes, together with those for nonelectrolytes, determine the solvent dependence of reaction rates. The most graphic conclusion from Table 4.4 is that small anions show a much bigger increase in free energy than larger, polarizable anions when transferred from water or methanol (protic solvents which contain relatively acidic protons) to aprotic solvents, such as DMF, Me2SO, MeCN, tetrahydrofuran, etc; compare the transfer of NaCl and Nal from water to DMF. This arises mainly because anions such as Cl–, and notably also OH–, F–, and OAc–, are strongly stabilized by hydrogen-bonding in protic solvents. By contrast, the larger anions with delocalized charges, such as picrate (2,4,6-trinitrophenolate) and tetraphenylborate (BPH 4 − ) , are relatively much more strongly solvated in typical non-aqueous media, where dispersion force interactions are larger. The effects are substantial — Δ G tr o values for Cl– are on average some 75 kJ mol-1 more positive than those for BPH 4 − and will be seen to account for the very large solvent effects observed for many important classes of reactions involving anions as nucleophiles or bases.

Such simple trends with size and solvent do not exist among the various cations—more specific binding, often described under the general heading of ‘hard-soft’ interactions, and factors such as ligand field effects for transition metal cations play an important role. However, the results suggest that in most instances solvents such as DMF and Me2SO (1), which are stronger Lewis bases than water, solvate cations more strongly. This is consistent with the highly polar nature of such solvents and the relatively high negative charge density on the carbonyl or sulfoxide oxygens. The reverse is true for solvents which are poorer Lewis bases; for example, MeCN and the vast range of nonpolar organic solvents.

(ii) Nonelectrolytes. Typical nonelectrolytes show little change in free energy when transferred among nonaqueous solvents, compared with electrolytes. However, they generally have substantially higher free energies (lower solubilities) in water. Uncharged reactants, therefore, do not normally contribute significantly to solvent effects on rates. Results for CH3I and t-BuCl are shown in Table 4.5 and are indicative generally of the behaviour of neutral organic substrates (Parker 1969, p. 5).

Cation-molecule reactions: metal ion complexation

Complexation reactions are amongst the most common and important undergone by cations. It is necessary here to recognize explicitly the involvement of the solvent, as in eqn 4.25 for cation Mn+,

MS m n+ +L ⇌ k d k f MLS m−1 n+ +S (4.25)

in which L represents the incoming ligand and S a solvent molecule. Thus the solvent acts directly as a leaving or as entering group, depending on the direction of the reaction. There is good evidence from kinetic and spectroscopic studies that the reactions may best be represented in terms of a two-step mechanism (Eigen and Wilkins 1965), eqn 4.26

MS m n+ +L ⇌ K os ( MS m L ) n+ → k 2 MLS m−1 n+ +S (4.26)

The first step is the formation of an outer-sphere complex, via a rapid preequilibrium, and the second is a rate-limiting reaction to form a complex in which the cation and ligand are in direct contact.

It is found experimentally that for a wide range of simple ligand exchange or complexation reactions the rate constant for the second (substitution) step, k2, is closely similar to the rate constant for solvent exchange between the bulk solvent and the coordination sphere of the cation, ks, and almost independent of the ligand—results for reactions in water are shown in Fig. 4.5. This means that the rate constant for complex formation can be approximated by

k f = K os k S (4.27)

The outer-sphere equilibrium constant, Kos, has a value typically of c. 0.1 M-1 for a neutral ligand, almost independent of solvent.

Nonpolar reactions

Reactions between neutral molecules which do not develop dipolar or radical transition states usually display only very small solvent effects. Indeed it is often possible to study such reactions in the gas phase as well as in solution, and very little difference in rate constant is observed. The reason is that the charge distribution in the transition state and the ground state is very similar. The large class of concerted reactions known as pericyclic reactions, of which the Diels-Alder 1,4-addition reactions of alkenes (dienophiles) to conjugated dienes (dienes) are prominent examples, fall into this category.

4.4 Properties of solvents

We have seen above that solvent effects on reaction rates reflect the differential solvation of reactants and transition states, expressed quantitatively by eqns 4.19 or 4.20. From a practical point of view it would be very useful to be able to define properties of solvents which provide a measure of their polarity or solvating powers. A wide range of physical and empirical parameters have been used for this purpose, and in this section we discuss briefly some of these. Properties of various solvents are listed in Table 4.12—more comprehensive compilations are given by Marcus (1985) and Reichardt (1988).

There is, however, no single measure of polarity for the more polar solvents, as their solvating abilities depend very much upon whether they are required to stabilize positive or negative charge.
