5 Fast reactions in solution

 

5.1 Introduction

Classical kinetic studies of the rates and mechanisms of chemical reactions normally permit only the determination of the overall rate constant for reaction, and this may include several equilibrium constants or sets of rate constants, depending upon where the rate-limiting step occurs. In chemistry, however, we are interested particularly in change at an atomic or molecular level and fundamental processes of this kind are generally so fast that special techniques are needed to follow them. The catalysis of reactions by acids and bases, for example, frequently involves the coupling of extremely fast proton-transfer reactions with other chemical processes to produce slow observable changes. We will, somewhat arbitrarily, define fast reactions as those with half-lives of less than one second, i.e., reactions which are too fast to be studied conveniently by conventional techniques. In studying such reactions it is necessary not only to be able to record the reaction in an appropriately short time interval, but also to initiate it in an equally short time. The latter is frequently the more difficult problem.

A summary of currently important techniques for studying fast reactions is given in Fig. 5.1. The upper limit for chemical transformations is set by the period of a molecular vibration, which is commonly in the range 1012 to 1013 s. At these extremely short times it is difficult to distinguish between ‘physical’ and ‘chemical’ transformations; energy barriers become large enough for a process to be considered chemical at times of approximately 1010 s or longer. Bimolecular reactions consist of at least two steps: the approach of the two species, controlled by diffusion, and the real chemical transformation. Second-order rate constants cannot, therefore, exceed those for the diffusion process; this limits the maximum attainable to around 109−1011 M−1 s−1 (see below). The corresponding time-ranges of such reactions depend upon the reactant concentrations used (t ~ 1/kA, where k is the second-order rate constant and A is the larger of the concentrations of the two reacting species), but it is clear that even the highest rate constants can be accommodated by one or other of the techniques in Fig. 5.1.

There is in principle no great difficulty with respect to monitoring a reaction in attaining a time resolution down to 10−9 s, provided that reactant concentrations are amenable to optical (absorbance, fluorescence) or electrical (conductance) measurements. The limiting factor in studying fast reactions is normally the mixing time, which for laboratory stirrers is at best around 0.1 s. This time can be reduced to 10−3 s using continuous-flow or stopped-flow techniques, in which jets of the two reacting solutions impinge in a carefully designed mixing chamber. However, even by combining these techniques with Page 58, Fast reactions in solutionlow reagent concentrations, the determination of second-order rate constants is restricted to values of c. 106 M-1 s-1. To go significantly beyond this it is necessary to circumvent the mixing problem.

Much faster reactions can be studied by the use of relaxation methods, whereby a system at equilibrium is perturbed by a rapid change in some external parameter, such as pressure or temperature. The re-establishment of equilibrium is then observed by optical or electrical means. The method can only be applied to systems in which detectable concentrations of both reactants and products are present at equilibrium, but mixing problems are avoided completely.

Further improvements can be achieved using specialized reactors in which the reaction is observed along the direction of flow and a correction is made for the finite time of mixing (Strehlow 1992, Ch. 3).

An important class of methods, which may be termed lifetime methods or line-broadening methods, is based upon the observation of spectroscopic lineshapes. The methods are applied to systems in perfect equilibrium and involve neither mixing problems nor the perturbation of equilibria. They depend upon the uncertainty principle, according to which a state having a lifetime τ has an uncertainty in its energy δE, given by δE ~ h/2πτ, and hence in the transition frequency, ν, (E = hν) of δν = δE/h ~ 1/2πτ Thus a reduction in the lifetime of a particular state through chemical reaction or other means will lead to a broadening of the spectroscopic line on a frequency scale. Optical spectra are rarely used because of the extremely fast reactions required to produce measurable kinetic line-broadening (Fig. 5.1). Of much wider utility is the line broadening technique in nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), applicable over the time-range 1−10−3 s for proton resonance, and down to 10−6 s for nuclei such as 17O and 13C. Closely related is the technique of electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR), which may be used to study species with unpaired electrons; the time-range extends from about 10−5−10−10 s.

The final technique which we shall consider is that of flash photolysis, in which the perturbing function is an intense flash of light. Here the photons emitted in a flash of short duration (a few μs or less) are absorbed by molecules or ions which may then undergo a variety of processes: fluorescence, radiationless transitions to the ground state, intersystem-crossing into a lower triplet state, or various chemical changes. With the advent of modern pulse lasers, the time resolution of flash photolysis has been reduced to the picosecond level.

Diffusion-controlled reactions

It is of some interest to enquire as to the maximum rate at which reactions can occur. For unimolecular reactions, we have seen that the rate constants could be as great as 1012−1013 s−1. Of more general importance, however, are bimolecular reactions. These must involve an approach of the reacting species as well as the chemical reaction. Where any limitations due to mixing (macroscopic diffusion control) are eliminated, through use, for example, of relaxation techniques (Sect. 5.3), the maximum rate of a bimolecular reaction is equal to the rate of encounter of the two molecules; such reactions are called diffusion-controlled or encounter-controlled.

The approximate magnitude of rate constants for diffusion-controlled reactions can be deduced from a simplified theoretical model for the diffusion process first introduced by Smoluchowski (1917), to account for the rates of co-agulation Page 59, Fast reactions in solutionof colloids. 5.2 Flow methods

The principle of flow or rapid-mixing methods is simple: the two reactant solutions are forced rapidly into a mixing chamber and the mixed solution passes down an observation tube with a velocity of several metres per second, Fig. 5.2. The extent of reaction at various distances along the observation tube (corresponding to various times) is normally found by measuring a convenient physical property such as the optical absorbance or electrical conductivity. The time resolution of most rapid-mixing devices is limited by the mixing time to about 1 ms.

The kinetics associated with continuous-flow devices of the type illustrated in Fig. 5.2 have been discussed (Ch. 2, Sect. 2.6)—they are identical in form Page 60, Fast reactions in solutionto those for conventional static reactions except that the extent of reaction is governed by the residence time, tr, in the observation tube. For example, for a first-order reaction, A → P, the concentration of A at various points along the tube is given by

A= A 0 e −k t r      

(5.4)

The residence time can be defined equivalently in terms of the distance, d, along the tube and flow velocity, u (expressed in m s−1), i.e., tr = d/u, or the volume traversed, V, and flow rate, ν m3 s−1, i.e., tr = V/ν. It is important to note that at any particular point along the tube the concentration remains constant with time, so that rapid recording of the concentration is not required.

Continuous-flow reactors are not widely used to measure reaction rates, because of the difficulty of construction and the requirement for quite large volumes of solution.

Stopped-flow

A much more widely used flow technique is that of stopped-flow, illustrated schematically in Fig. 5.3. As before, two reactant solutions are mixed rapidly by being forced into a mixing chamber, M, but from the observation tube the solution flows into a third syringe, the piston of which is suddenly stopped by coming up against a seating or an external stop. Stopping occurs within a millisecond or two. There is an observation point, O, close to the mixing chamber, and measurement via the detecting system is triggered at the moment of stopping by, for example, a microswitch operated by the piston of the stopping syringe. Flow velocities are typically around 10 m s−1 so that an element of solution which comes to rest at an observation point about 1 cm from the mixing chamber will have been mixed for about 1 ms. It is difficult to reduce this time significantly because flow velocities much above 20 m s−1 lead to cavitation. The major advantages of stopped-flow over continuous-flow reactors is that only very small volumes of solution are required and the flow rates need not be controlled.

The kinetics are identical to those in a normal static batch reaction (Ch. 2, Sect. 2.2) as it is the change in concentration with time of a particular (station-ary) element of solution that is measured. The difference between stopped-flow and conventional kinetics lies solely in the rapid mixing of the stopped-flow method. Rapid-response monitoring is required and an important consequence of real-time monitoring is the mandatory use of physical parameters to follow the reaction. It is therefore a considerable advantage to work under first-order or pseudo-first-order conditions. 5.3 Chemical relaxation

All chemical relaxation methods rely on the perturbation of a system at equilibrium by a change in an external parameter such as temperature or pressure. Provided the alteration in conditions is sufficiently rapid, there will be a time-lag between the application of the perturbation and the approach of the system to the new equilibrium position. This time-lag is related to the forward and reverse rate constants of the system. The perturbation may be effected in two distinct ways:

• single pulse or jump methods, which involve a single perturbation brought about by a sudden change in, for example, temperature, pressure, or electric field;

• periodic disturbance or stationary methods, in which a periodic disturbance, normally sinusoidal, is established using ultrasonic waves or a high-frequency electric field.

The principle of relaxation methods is illustrated in Fig. 5.4 for an endothermic metal ion complexation reaction with ligand, L,

Mn+ + L ⇋ MLn+

(5.7)

If the system, initially at equilibrium at 20°C, is subjected to a rapid temperature Page 62, Fast reactions in solutionchange to (say) 25°C (curve a) it will no longer be at equilibrium. Reaction will therefore occur to re-establish equilibrium (curves b and c), in this case by increasing the concentration of MLn+ and correspondingly reducing the free ligand (and metal ion) concentration. The rate constants for formation and dissociation of the complex, MLn+, can then be determined from the rate of approach to the new equilibrium position.

Jump methods

Temperature-jump. The temperature-jump (T-jump) method is probably the most versatile and widely used of all relaxation methods. Most chemical equilibria are quite temperature-dependent, and the rate of change of the equilibrium constant with temperature depends upon the standard enthalpy of reaction, ΔH0, eqn 5.27.

( ∂ln K ∂T ) P = Δ H 0 R T 2      

(5.27)

Even in the unlikely event that the enthalpy change is zero, the system can still often be perturbed by coupling it to another temperature-dependent equilibrium, such as a buffer with a strongly temperature-dependent ionization constant.

The most common method of producing a rapid temperature change is by passing a high current of short duration through an electrolyte solution via the discharge of a high-voltage capacitor (Joule heating). The friction of the ions moving in the electric field causes a rapid rise in temperature. The principle of the apparatus with spectrophotometric detection is outlined in Fig. 5.8.

The capacitor, C, is charged to a high voltage, U0, and the spark gap, G, is then fired, so that C discharges through the cell, S, containing the solution under investigation. The main requirements of the apparatus are apparent in the factors governing the rate and magnitude of the temperature rise. The temperature rises exponentially with a heating time constant τ = RC/2, where R is the cell resistance and C is the capacitance of the capacitor; the magnitude of the rise is proportional to the energy of the discharge, C U 0 2 /2 Taken together, these show that the most favourable results are achieved with a small capacitance and a low solution resistance (fast discharge of C), and a high voltage, U0 (reasonable temperature rise). The low resistance is achieved by adding an inert electrolyte to the cell, and typical values of the various parameters are: C = 0.1μF, U0 = 30 kV, R = 50Ω. Such a combination will produce a temperature rise of about 5°C with a heating time constant of 2.5 μs for a 2 ml cell containing an aqueous solution. The corresponding change in equilibrium constant (eqn 5.27) is about 6 % for a normal proton-transfer reaction with ΔH0 = 10 kJ mol−1.

A typical T-jump cell (Fig. 5.9) has a volume of a few ml and an optical path length of 1 cm. The incident light is focused in the middle of the cell using quartz lenses Q. The volume heated is greater than that needed for observation so that a uniform temperature increase is produced.

The shortest relaxation time measurable with this technique is about 1 μs the time needed to heat the solution. This can be reduced to about 5 × 10−8 s by using a coaxial cable as capacitor, from which a rectangular pulse can be obtained rather than the more usual exponentially decaying one. The longest time is about 0.1 s, beyond which reactions are disturbed by the cooling of the solution after the temperature jump. The main limitations of the Joule heating method are the necessity for highly conducting solutions and high electric fields (which can be detrimental to biological structures), and restrictions on the time-range.

Some of the limitations can be avoided by using optical heating, which is achieved most effectively using very short laser pulses (10−8−10−9 s). A good example is the iodine laser jump technique of Holtzwarth et ai (1977) which uses a powerful iodine laser with an emission wavelength 1.315 μm and a pulse width between 150 ps and 5 μs. The major advantage of this wavelength is that water absorbs strongly in this region and temperature rises of 1°C in the order of nanoseconds can be produced without the necessity for any added dyes to absorb the light energy. Such equipment is expensive and not widely available, but the technique clearly shows promise for observing relaxation processes in the nanosecond time region.

This is based upon the increase in dissociation of weak electrolytes on application of an electric field. The technique is of particular historical significance as it was used to determine the rate constant for the fastest known protolytic reaction—the formation of water from H+ and OH− (Eigen and De Maeyer 1955).

Stationary methods (ultrasonic absorption)

For very fast reactions it is often advantageous to use a stationary perturbation, which imposes a periodic variation of the external parameter, rather than a single jump. In practice, most applications of stationary relaxation methods have involved sinusoidal perturbations, especially the sinusoidal pressure and temperature changes accompanying the passage of sound waves through a liquid. Ultrasonic techniques span a frequency range of about 104−109 Hz and beyond ( τ ~ 10−5−10−10 s) and have the advantage that there are no restrictions on the types of equilibrium which can be studied.

When the exchange is sufficiently rapid such that the lifetimes of the states become less than this critical value, the signals cannot be separated. The spectra are then determined by the time-average environment of the nuclei under investigation. There are random deviations from the average frequency, but the higher the exchange rate, the more nearly the fluctuations average to zero, and the sharper will be the resonance line.

In purely classical terms, this shows up as a resonance phenomenon in which there are large oscillations in the angle between the magnetic moment and the steady field B.

5.5 Flash photolysis

The initiation of reactions by irradiation is the most effective and versatile technique for studying transient intermediates of chemical change. Flash photolysis allows the creation in a short interval of time of a nonequilibrium situation in a chemical system. In particular, reactive species such as radicals, excited states, and unstable ions can be produced at sufficiently high concentrations to allow their direct observation and the study of their behaviour in chemical and biological systems. Especially notable is the time resolution of flash photolysis, which is around a few microseconds for plasma flash-lamps and extends down to the picosecond range and beyond for pulsed lasers (West 1986).

Many important applications relate to the kinetics of transitions between excited states of molecules, producing valuable information on these states. However, in recent years the flash photolytic generation of reactive species fundamental to organic reactions, such as enols, ketenes, and carbocations, from the breakdown of the initial excitation products has greatly widened the scope of, and interest among chemists in, the technique. Activity is this area is likely to continue to increase, particularly as, at least for organic photochemistry in the microsecond region, the apparatus is easy to assemble and relatively inexpensive.

A schematic representation of the conventional flash photolysis apparatus most commonly employed in liquid phase kinetic studies is shown in Fig. 5.20. The excitation flash is obtained from the discharge of a high-voltage capacitor through a flash-lamp. Discharge energies lie in the range 50-2000 J and the lifetimes of flash durations are between 1 and 30 µs; excitation times below 1 µs invariably require the use of pulsed lasers (see below). Many of the considerations concerning the relationship between the total power output and the flash duration are exactly analogous to those described in Section 5.3 for the temperature-jump technique. Carefully designed circuits with low impedance will allow flash duration times of about 1 µs for a 300 J discharge. Detection is normally via optical absorption, but conductimetric detection has also been used to monitor reactions of ionic species.

A significant advance in the understanding of the kinetics and equilibria of simple enols

has come through the application of flash photolysis (Kresge 1990). Enols and enolate ions, which are essential intermediates in many important chemical and biological transformations, may be generated by a Norrish type II photoelimination, shown in eqn 5.56 for acetone enol.
