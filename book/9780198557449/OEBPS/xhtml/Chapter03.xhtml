3 Complex reactions

 

3.1 Introduction

The majority of chemical reactions do not take place at a single collision, but rather have a mechanism which involves several such elementary processes or reaction steps. The result is that the reaction mechanism cannot be described by a simple one-term rate equation and it is therefore designated as complex.

The clearest indication of complexity is a rate law which is inconsistent with the stoichiometric equation. An example is the oxidation of the formate ion by permanganate in water, which is kinetically second-order

(3.1)

The stoichiometry immediately establishes that the reaction is more complex than a single bimolecular reaction step. Thus, although reactions consisting solely of a simple bimolecular encounter process will give second-order kinetics, the converse is not true—second (or first) order kinetics can veil intricate reaction mechanisms.

There is no general method of solving complex rate equations, since each mechanism is commonly a special case. However, examples from three broad categories will be presented:

  • reversible reactions, or reactions proceeding towards equilibrium, wherein measurable concentrations of reactants remain;
  • concurrent or side reactions which control product distribution where more than one process is available to a reactant;
  • consecutive reactions in which the initial products act as reactants for subsequent reactions.

Combinations of these can describe many reaction mechanisms.

The steady-state approximation

For more complex reactions an exact mathematical treatment of the differential equations becomes impractical; the rapidity with which the mathematical complexity increases is apparent from a comparison of the kinetic equations for consecutive first-order reactions above with those for a single-step, first-order reaction. In some cases it is necessary to resort to numerical methods (Sect. 3.6), but where one (or more) of the reaction intermediates is highly reactive, and consequently is present only at very low concentrations because it reacts away as soon as it is formed, considerable simplification may be achieved through use of the steady-state approximation. According to this, it is assumed that over most of the reaction the concentration of such an intermediate may be considered essentially constant and its time derivative set to zero. In this way simple expressions for the concentrations of the intermediates may be obtained, thereby allowing the derivation of an equation for the overall reaction rate.

In exploring the validity of the steady-state approximation, it is useful to apply it to the consecutive first-order reaction (eqn 3.23), since this can be solved exactly. Qualitatively, as the reactivity of B increases relative to that of A, i.e., as k2 becomes progressively larger than k1, the maximum concentration of B decreases and it occurs at an earlier time in the reaction: compare Fig. 3.4, which corresponds to k2 = 10k1, with Fig. 3.3, for which k2 = 0.5k1. Substituting typical values of k2/k1 into eqn 3.31 shows that for k2/k1 = 100, Bmax < l%A0 and when k2 = 104k1, Bmax is less than 10–4/A0; the rate of formation of C will then be almost exactly equal to the rate of loss of A. Thus although B varies between zero and Bmax during the reaction, the numerical value for its rate of change of concentration will be very small compared to the corresponding quantities for A and C. This is the crucial factor in understanding the validity of the steady-state approximation: the absolute change in the concentration of the reactive intermediate is small compared with the concentration of reactants and products, though its relative change in concentration may be large.

Rate-limiting step

When a process has several consecutive steps, it is commonly stated that one of them is rate-determining or, preferably, rate-limiting. This bald assertion needs some qualification, however, as they may all be taking place at the same speed.

A useful case with which to illustrate the concept is that of the reaction of t-butyl bromide with hydroxide ion (Scheme 3.1), which occurs by two successive steps. We saw that under steady-state conditions, when as much t-butyl cation is formed from t-butyl bromide as reacts with hydroxide, the rate law is given by

Essentially, the reaction of hydroxide with the t-butyl cation waits on the formation of the t-butyl cation and does not affect the overall rate.

It is apparent from this example that the question of which of two successive steps is rate-limiting is not a matter of their reaction rates (indeed they are equal under steady-state conditions), but rather of the magnitude of their rate constants. Increasing the magnitude of k1, for instance by altering the solvent, would lead directly to an increase in the overall reaction rate. By contrast, altering k2 or [OH–] would not affect the overall rate, but merely cause a corresponding change in the steady-state concentration of the t-butyl cation. Provided that the rate constants have the same dimensions, the rate-limiting step is the one whose rate constant is much the smallest. Where the dimensions are different (e.g., a first-order reaction followed by a second-order reaction), there is no simple general rule. Indeed the rate-limiting step may alter with reagent concentrations, from the second-order step at low concentration to the first-order step at higher concentrations.

3.5 Enzyme kinetics

All enzymes are proteins, which are unbranched polymers of α-amino acids of general formula

The carbon–nitrogen bond linking the carboxyl group of one residue and the α-amino group of the next is called a peptide bond. The basic structure of the enzyme is defined by the sequence of amino acids forming the polymer. Many enzymes are entirely protein in nature but in other cases they utilize prosthetic groups (coenzymes) to assist in their catalytic function. These groups may be complex organic molecules or ions and may participate directly in the catalysis or act by modifying the enzyme structure.

Page 34, Complex reactions

The nature of the chemical reactions catalysed by enzymes is sufficiently varied such that a complete compilation is prohibitive. The two largest classes of enzymes are hydrolytic enzymes and oxidative enzymes. The former, which catalyse the hydrolysis of peptide linkages, esters, and glycosidic linkages in polysaccharides, are commonly complex acid–base catalysts which facilitate the transfer of hydrogen ions. Oxidative enzymes are concerned with oxidation–reduction processes, and accelerate electron-transfer processes. Mention may also be made of transferring enzymes, which promote the interchange of groups, such as amino and keto groups, between two molecules. Multiple enzyme activities may be combined in multienzyme complexes, which catalyse more than a single reaction; reaction intermediates are passed from site to site within the enzyme complex. For example, fatty acid synthases, which are common to a variety of organisms from microbes to mammals, catalyse the formation of long-chain fatty acids from acetyl (CH3CO–) and malonyl (HO2CCH2CO–) groups through a series of cycles involving acyl transfer, condensation, reduction, and dehydration.

Interest in enzymes as catalysts arises not only because of their biological importance, but also because of their remarkable specificity and efficiency as catalysts. Kinetic studies can be grouped under two headings: steady-state kinetics, in which the enzyme concentration is much lower than that of the substrate, and transient kinetics or presteady-state kinetics, which utilize much higher enzyme concentrations to allow detection of reaction intermediates.

Steady-state kinetics

The very high efficiency of enzymes as catalysts means that normally very low concentrations suffice to make reactions proceed at conveniently measurable rates, i.e., reaction times of a few seconds or more. Typical enzyme concentrations are in the range 10–8 M to 10–10 M, while substrate concentrations are usually greater than 10–6 M. Thus concentrations of enzyme–substrate intermediates are low with respect to the total substrate (reactant) concentrations, even when the enzyme is fully saturated, and so can be considered to be in a steady state after a very short induction period. This greatly simplifies the rate laws.

The form of the rate equation is the same regardless of the number of intermediates, and indeed it is very unlikely that a transformation such as that from fumarate to (–)-malate would involve only a single intermediate. If, for example, two intermediates are assumed, i.e., one essentially resembling the reactant bound to E and the other the product bound to E,

E+S ⇌ k −1 k 1 X 1 ⇌ k −2 k 2 X 2 ⇌ k −3 k 3 E+P

(3.61)

the rate law is again given by eqn 3.52, but now

k cat = k 2 k 3 ( k −2 + k 2 + k 3 ) ;     K S = k 2 k 3 + k −1 k 3 + k −1 k −2 ( k −2 + k 2 + k 3 )

(3.62)

This equation may be derived in a straightforward manner by applying the steady-state assumption to both intermediates, X1 and X2, and noting that E0 = E + X1+X2.

The above examples show that steady-state kinetics are unable to give decisive information on the number or nature of the intermediates. This is, perhaps, not too surprising as it is only the substrate and product which are observed under steady-state conditions, not the intermediates. Nevertheless, steady-state kinetics do provide valuable information:

  • they measure the catalytic activity of the enzyme under steady-state conditions in the cell and are therefore important for the understanding of metabolism;
  • the structural specificity of the enzyme can often be obtained by varying the substrate;
  • steady-state kinetic constants allow the determination of the lower bound for all the rate constants in the mechanism. For example, for n intermediates, ki+1 ≥ kcat (i ≠ 0).

The equality is valid for n = 1 and the inequality can be easily derived for n = 2. A complete list of inequalities for the rate constants is given by Hammes (1982, p. 42).

Transient kinetics

In order to gain information on the states occurring between the uptake of substrates by the enzyme and release of products, and to allow determination of individual rate constants, it is necessary to use much higher enzyme concentrations and to measure the approach to steady-state. Under these conditions, however, the rates become very fast, necessitating the use of fast-reaction techniques. More serious is the difficulty in solving the complex kinetic equations; even the simplest mechanisms involve consecutive reversible reactions.

The elementary reaction steps associated with enzyme catalysis generally occur in times considerably shorter than 1 s and often as low as 1 μs. Among the most common type of reaction to be studied is the formation of enzyme–substrate complexes:

E+S ⇌ k −1 k 1 E.S

These can be studied directly if there is no subsequent turnover of the enzyme—e.g., where a cosubstrate or a coenzyme is omitted in a multistage reaction. The second-order rate constants for complex formation, k1, are typically in the range 106–108 M–1 s–1, normally much too high to be measured by the conventional kinetic methods. The techniques applicable to the study of such fast processes, which include the use of rapid mixing devices (continuous-flow and stopped-flow), and the rapid perturbation of chemical equilibria (relaxation methods), are described in Chapter 5.

Other common, indeed almost universal, reaction types are the isomerization or conformational change of the enzyme triggered by the substrate, and acid–base catalysis. The latter frequently involve rapid proton transfers between electronegative atoms such as O, N, and S on the enzyme and substrate, which occur with bimolecular rate constants of the order of 1010 M–1 s–1, or slower proton transfers to and from carbon. An important advantage of enzymatic reactions is the availability of several different functional groups important in catalysis which can interact simultaneously with the substrate. This is essentially an entropic advantage, equivalent to raising the effective concentrations of the reactants. Detailed case studies illustrating the use of steady-state and transient kinetics in elucidating enzyme mechanisms are given by Fersht (1977) and Hammes (1982).

3.6 Numerical methods

The differential equations for complex rate processes can in many cases be simplified considerably by use of pseudo-order conditions or the steady-state approximation. Nevertheless, it is frequently not possible to convert rate equations into their integrated form and in such cases the problem can be handled using numerical integration (Wiberg 1974). This can be used to generate calculated values of reactant (or product) concentrations as a function of time for given rate constants, even for the most complicated reaction mechanisms.

Consider initially a first order reaction, –dA/dt = k1A. For a sufficiently small time interval, Δt, we may approximate the rate by –dA/dt ≃ –ΔA/Δt (Ch. 2, Sect. 2.4) and hence we obtain

A ′ 1 = A 0 − k 1 A 0 Δt ⋮ ⋮ A ′ i = A ′ i−1 − k 1 A ′ i−1 Δt

(3.63)

where is the approximation to the true concentration, Ai, and Δt is the time interval for the calculation. The procedure, which is due to Euler, is illustrated in Fig. 3.9.

This shows that starting from some initial position, successive points are calculated using the tangent to the curve. The error in the approximation may be made progressively smaller by using smaller values of Δt, so that each interval of the curve may be more closely represented by a straight line. It can be shown, for example, that the result for a first-order reaction using Euler’s method with Δt = t1/2/20 is within one per cent of the true value.

It is a simple matter to extend the method to more complex mechanisms. For example, we can immediately write down the appropriate equations for consecutive reactions , eqn 3.23)

A ′ i = A ′ i−1  −  k 1 A ′ i−1 Δt B ′ i = B ′ i−1 −  k 2 B ′ i−1 Δt+ k 1 A ′ i−1 Δt C ′ i = C ′ i−1 + k 2 B ′ i−1 Δt

together with the appropriate initial concentrations. The concentrations of all three species throughout the reaction can then be calculated to the desired level of accuracy, depending upon the time interval, Δt, chosen.

Less satisfactory results are obtained using Euler’s method where more complicated curves are involved, and more efficient methods have been developed. Among the most common is the so-called fourth-order Runge–Kutta method. In this, the time interval, Δt, is divided into four equal parts and the slope is evaluated at three intermediate points, in addition to the initial slope. A weighted average of the four slopes is then used to calculate the next point. Using as an example a second order reaction, –dA/dt = k2AB, the form most commonly used is

A ′ i = A ′ i−1 −( a 0 +2 a 1 +2 a 2 + a 3 )/6

(3.64)

where

a 0 = Δt[ k 2 A ′ i−1 − B ′ i−1 ] a 1 = Δt[ k 2 A ′ i−1 − B ′ i−1 + a 0 /2 ] a 2 = Δt[ k 2 A ′ i−1 − B ′ i−1 + a 1 /2 ] a 3 = Δt[ k 2 A ′ i−1 − B ′ i−1 + a 2 ]

The Runge–Kutta method takes into account the curvature more adequately than the Euler method. A disadvantage is that four separate calculations of Ai are required for each step. Furthermore, it is difficult to know what step-size Page 39, Modern liquid phase kineticsto take and the errors are not known. A good rule of thumb is to take Δt about 0.05t1/2, where t1/2 is the smallest half-life for the various rate processes contributing to loss of the reactant. The result can be checked by cutting Δt in half and repeating the calculation.

There are now commercial programmes available for the numerical treatment of reaction rate data.
