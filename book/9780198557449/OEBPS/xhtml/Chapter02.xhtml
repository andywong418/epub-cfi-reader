2 Empirical treatment of reaction rates

 

2.1 Introduction

The starting point for most kinetic investigations of chemical reactions is the determination of the rate of the reaction and its dependence upon the concentrations of the species involved. However, before the reaction rate can be meaningfully discussed it is necessary to define the system carefully. In particular, this means a consideration of which type of system is under investigation:

  • Closed or open. A closed system is one in which matter is neither gained nor lost, as is typical for a liquid phase reaction in a flask or closed reactor. In an open system matter may be exchanged with the surroundings; common examples can be seen in living organisms and in flow systems.
  • Constant volume. Liquid phase reactions are for the most part effectively constant-volume processes, but volume changes may occur when reagents are added continuously.
  • Isothermal. Isothermal (constant temperature) conditions are convenient to work with and they also allow the use of temperature as an independent variable in controlling the reaction rate.
  • Homogeneous or heterogeneous. Homogeneous reactions are those which occur entirely in one phase. A reaction which requires the presence of at least two phases to proceed at the rate it does is described as heterogeneous, irrespective of where the reaction is occurring or of the distribution of the reagents among the phases.

For the most part, we will be concerned with closed, constant-volume systems, maintained at constant temperature and pressure. These are the simplest to consider experimentally and theoretically, and are the most commonly encountered in practice in the laboratory. The experimental methods for determining rates and the kinetic treatment of the results described in the following sections normally relate to homogeneous reactions, but a separate chapter is devoted to heterogeneous liquid–liquid reactions. This latter area is currently among the most active areas of research in solution kinetics; it is of great importance industrially, it is frequently encountered in the laboratory, and there is now widespread interest in reactions occurring at phase boundaries and across cell membranes in biological systems.

The constant, k, appearing in the rate equation, eqn 2.9, is called the rate constant or occasionally the rate coefficient or specific reaction rate. It is independent of the concentrations or the scale of the reaction, but is usually dependent upon the temperature and the reaction medium. A simple analysis of the rate equation shows that the units of the rate constant are (concentration)1–n (time)–1. Thus for a first-order reaction (n = 1), the units of the rate constant are (time)–1, i.e., s–1, and for a second-order reaction (n = 2) the units are (concentration × time)–1, i.e., mol–1 dm3 s–1 (M–1 s–1).

 

2.3 Integrated rate equations

The reaction rate is not normally measured directly; rather, the concentration of one of the reactants or products is monitored as a function of time. A typical result is illustrated in Fig. 2.1 for the rearrangement of N-bromoacetanilide to 4-bromoacetanilide, eqn 2.11.

The concentration of the reactant, N-bromoacetanilide, decreases from its initial value more and more slowly with time and approaches zero asymptotically. The rate at any time is given by the negative slope of the curve, determined by graphical or other means.

In practice, however, since it is the concentration which is measured, it is much more convenient to work with the integrated rate equation. The form of the equation depends upon the rate law, and a commonly used way of determining both the rate law and rate constant is to compare the experimental results with the integrated rate equations for reactions of different orders. The procedure is best illustrated through examples.

Pseudo-order reaction

The results summarized in Table 2.2 show that the integrated rate equations and hence the treatment of kinetic data becomes rapidly more complex as the reaction order increases. However, if the reaction conditions are such that the concentrations of one or more of the species appearing in the rate law remain effectively constant then these terms may be included in the rate constant k. 2.4 Determination of the rate law

There is no general method for finding the rate law or the order of a reaction. A necessary prerequisite is a careful determination of the reaction stoichiometry, and this, or perhaps an assumed reaction mechanism may point to an initial guess at the rate law. Commonly, a trial-and-error procedure based upon various postulated rate laws is employed, but several useful and relatively systematic approaches may be identified.

Method of integration

Where a simple rate law is suspected, it is usual to attempt directly to fit concentration-time data for the reaction to the integrated rate equation appropriate to a particular reaction order, as described in the previous section. Thus a first-order reaction should show a linear relation between In (concentration) and time, as illustrated for the rearrangement of N-bromoacetanilide in Fig. 2.2. Similarly for other reaction orders, the corresponding integrated rate equation (Table 2.2) is applied to the kinetic data, by graphical or other means; the procedure is repeated until a satisfactory fit is obtained.

A cautionary note is that in order to get reliable results it is essential to study reactions over a wide concentration range. The method relies upon detecting systematic deviations from a predicted rate equation, and these may only become apparent over significant concentration changes. Single reactions should, therefore, be monitored over several half-lives.

Half-life method

An alternative method of determining the reaction order is to measure the half- life as a function of the initial concentration. This can be done by noting the time required for several successive half-lives within a single kinetic run, or by using a series of reactions with different starting concentrations. In the event of the rate law being of the form

−dA/dt=k A n (2.28)

a simple and characteristic relationship exists between the half-life and the reactant concentration. We have seen that for a first-order reaction, t1/2 (= 0.693/k) is independent of starting concentration, whereas it is inversely proportional to the starting concentration for a second-order reaction (t1/2 = 1/kA0). Isolation method

The principle behind the isolation method is a simple extension of that described for pseudo-order reactions. The method involves ‘isolating’ one reactant, say A, through use of a large excess of all other reactants, thereby ensuring that their concentrations remain effectively constant. The rate law of a reaction represented by, for example,

r=k A n 1 B π 2 C n 3 (2.30)

reduces under these conditions to a much simpler form in which the concentrations of B and C and their powers are included with the rate constant, k, to give a new rate constant, k ! =k B π 2 C n 3 hence

r= k ! A n 1 (2.31)

The concentrations of B and C will only change marginally during the reaction because of their large excess over A.

The order with respect to A, n1, can now be obtained by fitting to the appropriate integrated rate equation (integration method) or from the properties of the reaction half-life (half-life method). Repeating the process by successively isolating the remaining reactants will give the order with respect to each one.

The main limitation of this method is that for a complex mechanism involving several steps, the mechanism may be altered by the large concentration changes needed to isolate the various reactants and effects due to solution nonideality may complicate the interpretation.

Initial-rate method

A more obvious approach is to use the rate equation 2.30 directly to make a comparison between reaction rate and concentration, by measuring the initial rates for solutions of known concentration. For small changes in concentration (≤ 10%), the rate can be approximated to the corresponding ratio of increments, i.e., –dA/dt ≃ –ΔA/Δt, illustrated in Fig. 2.4. 2.5 Practical measurement of rates

It is not profitable to attempt to describe all of the methods used to determine rates, but many of the major advances in kinetics have followed from the development of new techniques. This is especially true in the area of rapid reactions (Ch. 5). For conventional reactions, the problem reduces essentially to one of determining concentration as a function of time. The most desirable analytical methods are those which allow a continuous and rapid measurement of the concentration of a particular component, but it is normally necessary only to measure some property of the system as a whole which indicates the extent of reaction. The methods may be broadly divided into two categories:

  • Chemical methods. These involve sampling, quenching of the reaction, and direct determination of concentration using standard analytical methods. Their principal advantage is that they give an absolute measure of the concentration of a specific component. It is, however, difficult to automate chemical methods and they are not applicable to the majority of fast-reaction techniques.
  • Physical methods. Any physical property which varies monotonically with the extent of reaction can, in principle, be used to determine the reaction rate. By far the most useful are those which vary linearly with concentration—conductance (ionic reagents), absorbtion of visible or ultraviolet light, rotation of polarized light (chiral molecules), volumes of dilute solutions, etc. A key feature of physical methods is the potential for continuous, rapid-response measurement, without the need for sampling.

2.6 Flow systems: continuous reactions

The rate equations described to date, all apply to closed systems, commonly referred to as batch or static systems: the reactants are mixed in a reaction vessel or cell and the concentration changes are observed as the reaction proceeds to completion. In some cases, however, it is more convenient to use a flow system or continuous reactor (i.e., an open system), in which the reactants flow through a reactor and the products are collected at the end. Flow systems may be advantageous in several situations: continuous reactors are often cheaper to operate and easier to control and automate for large-scale industrial production; they represent the best means of studying fast irreversible reactions; and they provide an excellent means of investigating and utilizing reactive intermediates. An additional bonus is that the mathematical analysis of the reaction kinetics is often simpler than for static systems.

Continuous-flow methods are of two general types, distinguished by the presence or not of stirring in the reactor (Denbigh 1984):

  • Plug-flow or tubular reactor. In this case, which is typical of that used in the study of fast reactions (Ch. 5), there is no stirring in the reactor and hence no mixing between one point and another along the direction of flow. There is complete mixing of reactants immediately prior to entering the reactor and the fluid moves through it like a plug.
  • Continuous stirred tank reactor (CSTR). This corresponds to the extreme of complete mixing within the reactor, so that the concentration is uniform throughout the entire volume. There is a continuous flow of reactants into the vessel, from which the partially reacted material passes continuously.

Prior to any detailed study of the kinetics, it is useful to consider some general points concerning batch and continuous kinetics. In static systems the change in concentration occurs and is observed as a function of time. The significant difference for continuous reactions is that they are run under steady-state conditions and the corresponding coordinate for observing the reaction is space (distance). Thus in a plug-flow reactor with constant flow rate of the solution, the concentration at any point along the tube achieves a steady value, and the variation of composition shows itself between different points along the tube. Similarly, there is a variation between one tank and another in a stirred continuous reactor, but within any individual tank the concentration remains constant. It should be emphasized that this steady state is not an equilibrium; it results from a balance between the flow rate and reaction rate.

The fundamental basis for the kinetics in flow systems under steady-state conditions is the mass balance of a given reagent within any volume element:

moles entering = moles reacting + moles leaving(2.43)

Continuous stirred tank reactor

The kinetics of reactions carried out in a continuous reactor with stirring are quite different and considerably simpler than those for a tubular reactor. The technique may be understood by reference to Fig. 2.8.

The reagents, A and B, are fed separately and continuously at a flow rate ν dm3 s–1 into the reaction vessel (volume V dm3), where reaction takes place—the reaction is assumed to be slow compared to the speed of mixing. The product(s), P, and any remaining reactants, are discharged constantly through an outlet tube. The key points to note regarding continuous stirred tank reactors are that:

  • the reactor achieves a steady state after a time, regardless of the kinetics occurring within it;
  • the composition of the liquid flowing out of the reactor is the same as that at any point within.

The composition is made uniform throughout by the stirring, and it is this uniformity which gives rise to the distinctive features of continuous stirred tank reactors.

2.7 Temperature-dependence of rate constants

The rates of most chemical reactions increase rapidly with temperature. For liquid phase reactions the rate is typically doubled or trebled by a rise of 10°C near room temperature.

For the vast majority of reactions, the number of molecules which react is only a tiny fraction of the number which collide. The reason for this and for the rapid increase in reaction rate with temperature is that normally molecules will react only if their (kinetic) energy is much greater that the average kinetic energy of the molecules at that temperature, i.e., above some value E′, Fig. 2.10. A slow chemical change is therefore a reflection of the statistical nature of molecular events and the distribution of energy within the system.

In 1889, Arrhenius pointed out that the increase in rate constants with temperature can be expressed with considerable accuracy by

k= Ae _Ea/RT (2.48)

where A and Ea are constants: A is called the pre-exponential factor, Ea the activation energy, R is the gas constant and T is the absolute temperature. Empirically, the activation energy is a measure of the temperature sensitivity of the rate constant; a high Ea corresponds to a rate constant which increases rapidly with temperature.

Values of the two parameters, A and Ea, may be determined from a plot of ln k against 1/T

1nk=1nA −Ea/RT (2.49)

illustrated in Fig. 2.11 using data for the release of Na+ from its complex with the macrobicyclic cryptand ligand (2,2,1) in methanol

The slope of this so-called Arrhenius plot is –Ea/R and the intercept (calculated by extrapolation to 1/T = 0; T → ∞) is ln A.

The simple Arrhenius equation, eqn 2.48, is still in widespread use and frequently provides a good description of the temperature dependence of rate constants for reactions of different orders over quite a wide range of temperature. In some cases, however, the factor A shows some temperature dependence, giving rise to a slight curvature of an Arrhenius plot, and the more general equation

k=A′ T n e − E a /RT (2.50)

is used to give a better fit to the data. Here n can have any value determined by experiment. Theories of reaction rates, including transition-state theory (Ch. 4), which give rise to expressions of the type shown in eqns 2.48 and 2.50, are described elsewhere (see background reading).

Pronounced deviations from simple Arrhenius behaviour do occur in particular cases. Probably the most common reason for a sharply curved Arrhenius plot for an apparently simple chemical reaction is the existence of two competing reactions, each with its own A and Ea values. The reaction with the higher activation energy will increasingly dominate as the temperature increases, whereas at low temperature the reaction with the lower activation energy predominates. A breakdown of a linear Arrhenius plot occurs within a transition region, where both reactions are contributing to the observed rate.

Very unusual temperature dependences of rates, including rate maxima, may be associated with reactions having very strict conformational or orientation requirements. The rates of enzyme–catalysed reactions, for example, drop precipitously at high temperatures due to thermal denaturation of the enzyme. An interesting chemical example showing dramatic deviations from Arrhenius behaviour is provided by the rearrangement of methyl or allyl p-(dimethylamino)benzenesulphonate to the corresponding quaternary ammonium sulphonate, Scheme 2.1.
