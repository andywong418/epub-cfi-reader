6 Two-phase liquid–liquid reactions

6.1 Introduction

Reactions occurring across phase boundaries or involving transport of reactants between phases are of widespread importance in laboratory chemical reactions, in industrial processes, and in biochemistry and physiology. In the latter fields, for example, we may mention particularly transport processes in cells which are required to maintain and regulate the ionic balance across cell membranes; indeed, in resting cells, they consume a major portion of the energy the cell utilizes (Racker 1979). In industrial processes, two-phase conditions often result from the requirement of high productivity (concentration) in systems which would normally be studied under homogeneous conditions. In other cases, heterogeneous conditions are dictated by the low mutual solubility of the reactants. Often the chemical reaction in question occurs entirely within one phase, but the reagent concentrations and hence the reaction rates are influenced by the presence of the second phase.

In this chapter we provide an introduction to three important aspects of two-phase liquid-liquid reactions:

• Chemical reactions in two-phase aqueous–organic systems. The observed reaction rates depend both upon the rate of the chemical reaction and the rate of transport of reagents from one phase to the other, i.e., mass transfer.

• Phase-transfer catalysis. Very slow chemical reactions which arise primarily from the mutual insolubility of the reagents, can often be catalysed by phase-transfer agents.

• Diffusion and transport in (biological) membranes. Transport of species through membranes can occur by simple diffusion or by facilitated diffusion, often involving ‘carrier’ molecules.

6.2 Diffusion

The classical description of diffusion across a plane boundary, area A (Fig. 6.1), is in terms of Fick’s first law

J=(1/A)(d n i /dt)=−D(d c i /dx)

(6.1)

The flow rate is measured as a flux, J, which is the number of moles of species i, ni, passing through unit area per unit time. This is found to be proportional to Page 81, Two-phase liquid-liquid reactionsthe concentration gradient, dci /dx, and the proportionality constant is written as –D, where D is the diffusion coefficient.

The simplest case is that of steady diffusion across a thin film, illustrated in Fig. 6.2. The solute diffuses from the left-hand side, concentration ci(1), to a less concentrated solution, ci(2), on the right through a film of thickness l. The flux J is constant at steady state and integrating eqn 6.1 from x = 0 to x = l gives

J= D l { c i (1)– c i (2)}

(6.2)

Equation 6.2 is basic to most of what follows.

6.3 Aqueous-organic reactions

Consider a reaction involving a neat organic liquid B (e.g. a long-chain ester) in contact with an aqueous solution with which it reacts, subject to constant stirring such that there are droplets of B in the aqueous phase. This system is most simply treated using film theory, the basis of which is illustrated in Fig. 6.3 for a reaction occurring in the bulk aqueous phase. In the absence of reaction, B would maintain its saturation level in the aqueous phase, cB0. However, as the reaction rate increases, the bulk concentration is depressed to an extent dependent upon the reaction rate. Dissolution of B tends to replenish its concentration, but this dissolution is not instantaneous. Adjacent to the liquid–liquid interface is a thin layer, called the diffusion film, which is little affected by stirring, and diffusion across this layer limits the rate at which B is delivered to the bulk aqueous phase. It will be seen that the diffusion time across this layer is typically 5–10 s, so that no reaction will occur within this film in cases where reaction half-lives are substantially in excess of this.

It is in general necessary to check in which phase the reaction occurs, but it will most commonly be the aqueous phase for ionic reagents in the absence of phase-transfer catalysts. The derivations are equally valid for either phase as the reaction site.

We define the observed reaction rate r as the total number of moles of B reacting per second in the aqueous phase, volume V, and for convenience consider only first-order reactions of B (genuine first-order reactions or reactions for which other reagents are present in large excess, i.e., pseudo-first-order reactions, Ch. 2, Sect. 2.3)

−d c B /dt= k 1 c B

(6.3)

It is useful to distinguish three main cases, according to the homogeneous reaction rate in the aqueous phase:

(i) Slow reaction. For a sufficiently slow reaction, B will remain at its saturation level, cB0, and the observed rate is give by eqn 6.4,

r= k 1 V c B 0

(6.4)

Note that the volume V occurs in eqn 6.4 (and 6.6 below) to convert the concentration of B to an absolute number of moles in accordance with the definition of r.

A typical example is the manufacture of benzyl alcohol by the base hydrolysis of benzyl chloride

(ii) Moderate reaction. As the homogeneous reaction rate constant increases, the overall rate will be partially controlled by the diffusion of B across the diffusion film, length δ; the consequence is a lower concentration of B in the aqueous phase, cB, and hence the creation of a concentration gradient, ( c B 0  −  c B )/δ across the film (Fig. 6.3). This system can be treated very simply, as in the steady state the rate of delivery of B to the bulk aqueous phase by diffusion will equal its rate of consumption through chemical reaction. The diffusion rate follows directly from eqn 6.2:

r=(D/δ) aV( c B 0 – c B )

(6.6)

where D is the diffusion coefficient of B and a is the interfacial area per unit volume of aqueous solution. In practice, the diffusion film thickness cannot usually be measured directly and the term D/δ is normally lumped as kL, the liquid–liquid mass transfer coefficient

r= k L aV( c B 0 − c B )

(6.7)

Equating this in the steady state to the rate of the homogeneous reaction, k1 VcB, and rearranging to eliminate cB gives

r= k 1 V c B 0 k L a k 1 + k L a

(6.8)

This important equation shows that the rate does not increase indefinitely with increased interfacial area, a (eg. from smaller droplet size arising from increased stirring), but approaches a limiting value of rlim = k1 VcB0 (compare eqn 6.4). The dependence of rate upon the interfacial area is illustrated in Fig. 6.4. Chemical reactions exhibiting this behaviour are the base hydrolysis of partially soluble ethanoate esters, and the sulfonation of benzene or toluene in concentrated sulphuric acid (Doraiswamy and Sharma, 1984, p. 62).

It may be noted that the slow reaction case (i) above is simply a particular instance of eqn 6.8, corresponding to k1 ≪ kLa. This may occur either through a slow chemical reaction (low k1) or through rapid stirring to generate a large interfacial area.

(iii) Fast reaction. In general, it is found that for aqueous systems,

kL ≃ 10–3 cm s–1;    D ≃ 10–5 cm2 s–1

and hence the film thickness δ ≃ 10–2 cm. The time taken to cross the film, t = δ/kL, is therefore about 10 s. Although this value is only approximate, it is apparent that reactions with half-lives substantially shorter than a few seconds (k1 >~ 1 s–1) will occur entirely within the diffusion film and the bulk concentration of B in the aqueous phase will be zero. The concentration gradient within the film is then determined both by diffusion and chemical reaction, and decreases as the concentration of B (and hence the reaction rate) decreases towards zero. Figure 6.5 shows a schematic representation of the concentration profile within the film.

A full derivation of the rate expression for a first-order reaction shows that

r= c B 0 a D k 1

(6.9)

where k1 is the first-order constant. The important practical feature of eqn 6.9 is that it shows that the rate is proportional to the surface area per unit volume of the aqueous phase, but is independent of the total volume. Thus the rate will effectively increase indefinitely with stirring rate, limited only by the eventual cavitation of the liquid phase. An example is the oximation of cyclohexanone, which is only slightly soluble in water, with excess aqueous hydroxylamine (Sharma and Nanda 1968),

At the extreme of very fast second-order reactions (reactions sufficiently fast such that the two reagents effectively cannot coexist in solution) the observed rate is simply determined by the rate at which the two reagents can diffuse together within the diffusion film.

The main features of reactions in two-phase aqueous-organic systems can be summarized as follows:

• Slow to moderate reactions (half-lives greater than a few seconds): reaction occurs in the bulk aqueous phase; reaction rate is proportional to volume, homogeneous rate constant, and to the saturated concentration of the organic reactant B in the aqueous phase, but shows an asymptotic dependence upon the interfacial area.

• Fast reactions: reaction occurs in the diffusion film; reaction rate is independent of volume, proportional to the square root of the homogeneous rate constant, to the saturated concentration of B in the aqueous phase, and to the interfacial area.

The differences are sufficient for practical purposes to identify the regime under which a given two-phase reaction system is occurring, i.e., slow-to-moderate or fast reaction, and hence to select appropriate reaction conditions.

6.4 Phase-transfer catalysis

Reactions between ionic reagents and organic substrates are often unacceptably slow due to the mutual insolubility of the reactants. Such reactions can frequently be accelerated by the use of phase-transfer catalysts, whose role is to transfer the anions of the reacting salt into the organic medium in the form of ion pairs. A typical example is the nucleophilic reaction of cyanide ion with alkyl chlorides or bromides (Starks and Owens 1973):

Heating and stirring a two-phase mixture of the reagents even for several days gives essentially zero yield of 1-cyanooctane, but the addition of a small amount of an appropriate quaternary ammonium salt leads to quantitative product formation in only a few hours.

A simple mechanism to account for this acceleration is shown in Scheme 6.1 for the general reaction of NaY with RX. An ion pair, formed between the quaternary ammonium ion Q+ and Y–, undergoes a ready substitution reaction with RX to give the desired product RY. The co-produced ion pair [Q+X–] returns to the aqueous phase, where Q+ picks up a new Y– for the next cycle.

The system is catalytic provided that [Q+X–](org) is rapidly converted back to [Q+Y–](org) and does not accumulate in the organic phase. This model can account for the majority of phase-transfer reactions involving displacement, oxidation, and reduction under neutral or acidic conditions, if we generalize it slightly to take into account two different modes of ion transfer between the phases. These are shown in Fig. 6.6 and correspond to either transport of the anion plus cation (Fig. 6.6a) or direct exchange of the two anions across the interface (Fig. 6.6b). The latter is of particular relevance when very lipophilic catalysts are used, as the catalyst cation tends to remain confined to the organic layer.

The driving force for the catalysis is the much greater solubility of ‘organic’ electrolytes compared with simple inorganic salts in nonpolar media—the principles behind this have been discussed in Chapter 4 (Sect. 4.3). Phase-transfer catalysts are commonly quaternary ammonium or phosphonium salts, such as tetraalkylammonium salts with alkyl = butyl or larger. The main requirements are that the cation has sufficient organic structure to ensure effective partitioning of the anion into the organic solvent, and that the cation-anion binding in the ion pair should be sufficiently ‘loose’ to allow high anion reactivity.

is coupled to one of two transport processes (Gordon and Kutina 1977),

[ Q + X − ] ( org ) ⇌ Q ( aq ) + + X ( aq ) −

(6.13)

Y ( aq ) − +Q ( aq ) + ⇌ [ Q + Y − ] ( org )

[ Q + X − ] ( org ) + Y ( aq ) − ⇌ [ Q + Y − ] ( org ) + X ( aq ) −

The time required to establish these equilibria is essentially that of transport across the diffusion film, about 10 s or less (Sect. 6.3).

The rate law corresponding to eqn 6.12 is given by

−d [RX] ( org ) /dt= k 2 [ Q + Y − ] ( org ) + RX ( org )

(6.16)

Provided [Q+Y–](org) remains constant during the reaction (see below), this reduces to a pseudo-first-order reaction

−d [RX] ( org ) /dt= k 1 [ RX ( org ) ]

(6.17)

where k1 = k2[Q+Y–](org). An essential prerequisite for first-order conditions is an excess of the incoming reagent Y–. This is, however, not sufficient as during the reaction the displaced anion X– may increasingly compete for the catalyst Q+, thus reducing [Q+ Y–](org). Two sets of conditions will lead to observed first-order kinetics over the major part of a phase-transfer catalysed reaction:

• Saturation of the aqueous phase with respect to the concentrations of the two anions; e.g., for the cyanide displacement reaction, eqn 6.11, adding sufficient NaCN and NaCl such that excess solid of each is present throughout the reaction will ensure that the relative thermodynamic activities of CN– and Cl– remain constant, as will [Q+ CN–](org).

• An excess of Y– combined with a favourable partition constant for the distribution of Q+ Y– into the organic phase relative to Q+ X– . This is primarily a function of the chemical system involved, but has been achieved, for example, in substitution reactions of 1-octyl methanesul-fonate with a variety of nucleophiles (Landini et al 1978).

The evaluation of [Q+Y–](org) in terms of the total (known) catalyst concentration Q0 is specific to the conditions adopted, but always involves a combination of mass-balance relationships and the phase extraction equilibria (Starks and Liotta 1978, Ch. 2, Gordon and Kutina 1977).

6.5 Membrane permeability

Diffusion in a membrane cannot be treated in quite the same way as for that across a plane boundary (Sect. 6.2), because the concentration profile within the membrane is not generally accessible. Rather, there is a known concentration difference between (stirred) inner (cin) and outer compartments (cout). This is illustrated in Fig. 6.7. The flux J ∞ (cin – cout) and Fick’s law becomes

J = P (cin – cout)(6.18)

where P is known as the permeability coefficient and has dimensions of distance per time.

The diffusion of nonelectrolytes across thin (organic) membranes involves partitioning of the solute into the membrane and diffusion within the membrane. A typical concentration profile is shown in Fig. 6.8 for a solute which is more soluble in the membrane than in aqueous solution. If we define the partition or distribution coefficient for the solute, Kd, by Kd = c1/cin = c2/cout then c1 = Kdcin; c2 = Kdcout. Proceeding exactly as in Section 6.2 for diffusion in a thin film, we obtain

J=( D m K d / l m )( c in − c out )

(6.19)

i.e.,       P= D m K d / l m

(6.20)

where Dm is the diffusion coefficient of the solute within the membrane and lm is the membrane thickness. The partition coefficient, Kd is more sensitive to the nature of the solute than the diffusion coefficient, Dm.

Equation 6.20 has had considerable success when tested for simple nonelectrolytes, such as alkyl ureas, alcohols, propionamide, in biological membranes. However, wider studies have pointed to a fundamental omission in this simple model, namely interfacial resistance (Dix et al 1978). In the derivation of eqn 6.20 it has been assumed that diffusion to the interface and equilibration across the interface are rapid. A complete model for the passage of a solute through a uniform membrane is given in Scheme 6.2. It includes diffusion through an inner aqueous diffusion film, thickness δ, diffusion coefficient, Daq; partitioning at the aqueous-organic interface, Kd = c2/c1; diffusion through the membrane, thickness lm, diffusion coefficient Dm; partitioning at the second interface, Kd = c3/c4 = c2/c1; and diffusion through an outer boundary layer.

The importance of eqn 6.22 is that it shows clearly the origin of the barrier to nonelectrolyte membrane permeability—viz., aqueous diffusion, interfacial transfer, and internal diffusion. It shows also that when expressed as resistances the terms contribute additively to the overall resistance to solute transfer and hence to the time required. This is a fundamental property of diffusional motion in systems involving phase transfer.

The diffusion of electrolytes across membranes is normally very slow because of their low solubility, but the principles are the same as those discussed above. It is constrained by the requirements of electrical neutrality so that the movement of a given ion may be limited by counter-ion diffusion. Where diffusion occurs through ion pairs, the rate depends upon the square of the concentration. This is because the concentration of ion pairs near the membrane surface is proportional to the product of the concentrations of anion and cation, i.e., the square of the electrolyte concentration.

An extremely important aspect of biological membranes is that of facilitated diffusion, particularly that involving mobile carriers (Saier and Stiles 1975, Burgermeister and Winkler-Oswatitsch 1977). Similar processes are also involved in the extraction and transport of metals in industrial processes (Albery and Choudhery 1988). Characteristics of such processes are much enhanced transport rates, high solute specificity, and a rate maximum with increasing solute concentration. The mechanism consists of four steps (Fig. 6.9): complex formation between the solute and carrier at the in interface; diffusion of the complex through the membrane; release of the solute at the out interface; diffusion back of the carrier.

An alternative mode of transport is via channel formation in the membrane (Burgermeister and Winkler-Oswatitsch 1977).
