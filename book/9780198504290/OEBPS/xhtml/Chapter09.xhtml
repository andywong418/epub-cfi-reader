9.2 Show how the determinant of a 3×3 matrix can be used to evaluate both a cross product and a scalar triple product.

Let the vectors a˜,  b˜   and   c˜ have components

a˜ =(a1,a2,a3)=a1i˜+a2j˜+a3k˜b˜ =(b1,b2,b3)=b1i˜+b2j˜+b3k˜c˜ =(c1,c2,c3)=c1i˜+c2j˜+c3k˜

Where i˜, j˜  and  k˜ are unit vectors in the x, y and z directions respectively. Then,

|i˜ j˜ k˜ a1a2a3b1b2b 3|=(a2b3−b2a3)i˜−(a1b3−b1a3)j˜+(a1b2−b1a2)k˜                              =(a2b3−b2a3,a3b1−b3a1,a1b2−b1a2)=a˜×b˜_

The general properties of determinants can be used to infer some of those of a cross and scalar triple product:

(i) a˜×b˜=−b˜×a˜, because the determinant is multiplied by –1 if two rows (or columns) are interchanged;

(ii) a˜×b˜=0 if a˜ is parallel to b˜, because a determinant is zero if two rows are the same;

(iii) (a˜×b˜)·c˜=0 if a˜,   b˜,  and   c˜ are not linearly independent, so that they lie in a plane (or along a line), for then any row minus a suitable combination of the other two will result in a whole line of zeros.

9.4 Show that the eigenvalues of an Hermitian matrix are real, and that the eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.

The equations for the jth and  kth eigenvalues, λj  and  λk, and the corresponding eigenvectors, x˜j   and   x˜k, of a matrix A˜˜ are

A˜˜x˜j=λjx˜j  — (1)   and   A˜˜x˜k=λkx˜k — (2)

The transpose of eqn (1), and the complex conjugate of eqn (2), gives

(A˜˜x˜)T=x˜TA˜˜T

λT = λ

(1)T⇒ x˜ jTA˜ ˜ T=ljx˜ jT     — (3)

 (2)*⇒ A˜ ˜ *x˜ k*=lk*x˜ k* — (4)

Then, subtracting eqn (4) pre-multiplied by x˜jT from eqn (3) post-multiplied by x˜k*, we obtain

(3)x˜k*−x˜jT(4)⇒ x˜jkA˜˜Tx˜k*−x˜jTA˜˜*x˜k*=λjx˜jTx˜k*−λk*x˜jTx˜k*

But A˜ ˜ T=A˜ ˜ *⇒ (λj−λk*)x˜ jTx˜ k*=0

If j=k   λj=λj*  (∵x˜jTx˜j*>0)

i.e.The eigenvalues of an Hermitian matrix are real

a˜Tb˜* is the general definition of the dot product between two potentially complex vectors a˜   and   b˜; thus if a˜=b˜, then a˜Ta˜*≥0 because it represents the square of the modulus (or magnitude) of a˜.

If j≠k  and   λj≠λk,  x˜jTx˜k*=0

i.e.The eigenvectors of distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal

The condition a˜Tb˜*=0  for   a˜≠b˜  (with a˜≠0  and   b˜≠0) is the definition of orthogonality, so that the vectors a˜   and   b˜ are perpendicular to each other.

If λj=λk, so that two eigenvalues are the same, then it is known as a degenerate case. The related eigenvectors do not then specify a unique direction, but correspond to a pair of vectors that lie in a given plane. Since any point in a plane can be attained through a linear combination of two (non-collinear) basis vectors lying in it, we are at liberty to choose two orthogonal directions in the degeneracy plane as being suitable eigenvectors. The preceding analysis applies equally well to real and symmetric matrices, satisfying A˜˜=A˜˜* and A˜˜T=A˜˜ , since these are just a special case of Hermitian ones.

When evaluating the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a real and symmetric matrix (or even an Hermitian one) it is always worth quickly checking that: (i) the sum of the eigenvalues is equal to the trace of the matrix; (ii) the eigenvectors are mutually orthogonal; and (iii) the product of the eigenvalues is equal to the determinant of the matrix (if this is not too difficult to calculate). A failure to meet any of these criteria is indicative of a mistake in the evaluation of the eigenproperties. Following the previous example, we should also make sure that all the eigenvalues are real.
