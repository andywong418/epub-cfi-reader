11.3 Prove that if Cv is independent of volume V, δq=CvdT+(RT/V)DV is not an exact differential (R being a constant). Show that by dividing this equation by T, it becomes exact. Comment on the relevance of this to thermodynamics.

But ∂∂Tv(RTV)=RV≠0 ⇒ ∂∂VT(CV)≠∂∂Tv(RTV)

∴ δq=CvdT+(RTV)dv is not exact_ 

∂∂VT(CVT)=0 and ∂∂Tv(RV)=0

∴ δqT=CvTdT+RTVdV is exact_ 

In thermodynamics, δq represents the change in heat. Since this is not an exact differential, the heat of the system does not only depend on the present and starting conditions but also on the details of how it go there; were the pressure and volume held fixed first and the temperature allowed to change, for example, or the other way around in going from (P1,V1,T1) to (P2,V2,T2), When divided by the temperature,δq/T represents the change in entropy. As this is an exact differential, so that ∫δq/T is independent of the specific way in which the system was altered in going from (P1,V1,T1) to (P2,V2,T2), entropy is said to be a state function: its value depends on the state that it’s in, and not on how it got there.

Incidentally, 1/T is the integrating factor for this problem: the multiplicative ‘weighting term’ which converts an inexact differential into an exact one. We could have derived it knowing only that it was a function of temperature, w(T), from the condition that

∂∂VT[CVw(T)]=∂∂TV[RTVw(T)]  ⇒  0=RTVdwdT+Rvw

where we have replaced (∂w/∂T)V by dw/dT because, by definition, w depends only on T. This yields a simple first-order ordinary differential equation, which we will meet in Chapter 13, and has the solution w=A/T where A is a constant.
