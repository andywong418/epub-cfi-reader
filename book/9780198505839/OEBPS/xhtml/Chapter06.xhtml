Answer

(a) This is a polar reaction.

The secondary carbocation is more stable than the primary alternative, C H 3 C H 2 C H 2 + .

(b) This is a radical reaction. It goes via the more stable secondary radical.

(i) R• + HCl → RH + Cl•

Cl• + CH3CH = CH2 → CH3C•HCH2Cl

And so the chain continues.

(b) Cl• + O3 → ClO• + O2

Ozone is consumed in this cycle and the radicals are regenerated, which leads to some depletion of the ozone layer. The major route for ozone depletion occurs by a similar chain involving the dimer Cl2O4.

(c) Because the anaesthetics are very expensive, excess is recycled. Very little escapes into the atmosphere. (d) The most likely reactions are nucleophilic substitution reactions in raindrops, either by a bimolecular (SN2) or a unimolecular (SN1) mechanism.

The small carbon atom is closely surrounded by large halogen atoms, so that access to the central carbon by a nucleophile (H2O) is very restricted; and as the transition state would be even more crowded, the activation energy would be high. So SN2 is difficult.

It is interesting that in contrast to CCl4, SiCI4 is easily hydrolysed. This may be because the greater size of Si means that this atom is more exposed to nucleophiles. More likely is that Si uses its 3d orbitals to receive initial attack by the nucleophile (H2O) so that reaction proceeds by an addition–elimination mechanism (which is not possible for carbon).

H & P sections 6.1, 6.2 (p 75) also 4.2, 4.5 (pp 41,45)

SN1 reactions start with the formation of a carbocation by loss of a halide ion. This would put positive charge on a carbon already denuded of electrons by the strongly electron-withdrawing nature of the halogen atoms: this is unfavourable, so Sn1 is difficult too.
