Answers to problems

 

1.1While εr is often a good polarity predictor, other chemical properties of solvents are important. Here the donor numbers of acetonitrile (14.1) and pyridine (33) are no higher than that of water. Copper(I) is a soft cation, and acetonitrile and pyridine are soft solvents which have a special affinity for soft cations.
1.2Acetic acid strongly dimerizes to give a low polarity liquid (see Section 3.1). In the presence of an excess of hydrogen-bonding water the dimers break up.
2.1(a) Solvents which cover the pH 0 ‒ 7 range on Fig. 2.2 include water, ethanol, and acetonitrile. All could be used.
(b) Solvents which cover the pH 20 ‒ 27 on Fig. 2.2 include acetonitrile, dimethylformamide, etc.
2.2(i) N3‒in liquid ammonia is ammonolysed to amide, 3 NH3 + N3‒ → 4NH2‒, and consequently is basic,
(ii) In liquid ammonia acetic acid is a strong acid, completely dissociating to NH4+ and OAc‒. KO Ac is thus the salt of a strong acid (HOAc) and a strong base (KNH2) and does not alter the acidity/basicity of liquid ammonia.
(iii) NaHSO4 dissolves in H2SO4 to give HSO4‒ ions, the lyate ion of H2SO4. It thus makes the solution more basic.
3.1N2O4 dissolves to give NO+ and NO3‒. So final products are NO+ + H3O+ +NO2+ + 3HSO4‒ (see Table 3.5).
3.2N2H4, NH2NO2 (nitramide), Hg3N2, CH3NH2, OC(NH2)2 (urea), [Cr(NH3)6]Cl3.
3.3Pure HF is a very powerful proton donor. So EtOH will be protonated to EtOH2+, NH3 will be protonated to NH4+, and PhCO2H will be protonated to PhCO2H2+.
4.1In chloride-rich melts the Lewis acid Ag+ forms soluble complex chloroanions [AgCln](n‒1)‒ (n = 2 – 4).
4.2Poly cations of iodine are powerful Lewis acids which are coordinated by, and then react with, Lewis bases. In superacids and AlCl3-rich melts the concentrations of Lewis bases are very low, and thus the very reactive cations have no ready reactant to decompose them.
