3 Some molecular solvents

In this chapter, chemistry in a number of electrolytic molecular solvents is discussed. The chapter concludes with a discussion of supercritical fluids, which, although strictly not liquids, share many of the properties of molecular liquids.

3.1 Acetic acid

Acetic acid (ethanoic acid) has a liquid range similar to water. With a dielectric constant, εr, of 6.2 it is a much less polar solvent than water (εr 78.3)

Acetic acid dimers, 3.1, account for a large fraction of the liquid. Acetic acid boils at roughly the same temperature as similarly sized molecules like octane.

The solubilities of ionic compounds in acetic acid are higher than expected from its dielectric constant. This is because the acetic acid monomers can solvate cations, and hydrogen bond to suitable anions. Thus, chlorides like CaCl2, ZnCl2are quite soluble, as are the corresponding nitrates, and acetates. Univalent chlorides, nitrates and acetates are less soluble. Compounds like AgCl and CaCO3, which are insoluble in water, are insoluble in acetic acid, as are many sulfates.

It is difficult to oxidize acetic acid, and so it is a good solvent for studying oxidations with powerful oxidants like lead tetraacetate, cobalt(III) acetate and chlorine dioxide. It is commercially used as the solvent for autoxidation of p-xylene to terephthalic acid, catalysed by cobalt(III) and bromide ions. Its resistance to oxidation means that it is also a useful solvent for studying reactions of halogens and interhalogens such as Cl2, Br2, and ICl and was so used in the elucidation of reaction mechanisms for the halogenation of aryl hydrocarbons.

Perchloric acid and sodium acetate act as a strong acid and strong base, respectively, in acetic acid. With the low dielectric constant of acetic acid these strong acids and bases are largely present as ion pairs [H2OAc]+[ClO4]– and Na+OAc– (see Section 1.7). In a 0.1 M solution only about 0.1% are present as free ions. As explained in Section 2.1, acetic acid is able to differentiate between acids which are strong in water. As it is an acidic solvent, bases which are weak in water become stronger. Thus, weak bases like diphenylamine can be titrated with perchloric acid in acetic acid.

Acetic acid readily solvates both ionic and molecular compounds. Indeed, removal of the final traces of solvent from a compound synthesized in acetic acid can prove difficult, and many crystal structures of compounds containing solvated acetic acid retained from its use in their synthesis are reported.

3.2 Acetonitrile

Acetonitrile, 3.2, is a popular dipolar solvent as it combines several desirable characteristics. It has a dielectric constant (εr = 37.5) which places it towards the lower polarity end of electrolytic solvents. It is a fair electrolytic solvent and many ionic compounds will dissolve in it. It has only modest donor power (DN = 14.1), with the nitrogen less basic than many nitrogen-containing species. Being a softer donor solvent than water it is especially good at solvating soft cations such as Cu+, Ag+, and Hg2+. Its softness also means it solvates polarizable anions such as I‒, SCN‒, BPh4‒. Table 3.1 shows these effects in action.

Acetonitrile is not a self-ionizing amphiprotic solvent. It is protophobic, and non protogenic, and cannot act as a hydrogen-bond donor. It therefore poorly solvates hydrogen-bond acceptor anions, such as F‒ and oxo anions. Perchloric acid in acetonitrile is more acidic than in water, while strong bases in acetonitrile are more basic than in water. Its range of effective pH (Fig. 2.1) is over 33. When halogen acids such as HCl dissolve in acetonitrile, the solvent is protonated to CH3CNH+. However, the halide anion is poorly solvated and is stabilized by undissociated halogen acid (eqn 3.1).

2HCl + CH3CN ⇌ CH3CNH+ + HCl2− (3.1)

Acetonitrile is a good electrochemical solvent as it is both difficult to reduce, and difficult to oxidize. Redox potentials are similar to those in water. While salts of group 1 and group 2 metals cannot be reduced to the metal, CuCl, AgNO3, MnI2, CoCl2, NiBr2, CdCl2, SnCl2, AsCl3, and SbCl3 can all be reduced to the metal in acetonitrile. It is widely used as a solvent for analytical electrochemistry, such as cyclic voltammetry. The ready solubility of support electrolytes such as tetraalkylammonium salts adds to its attractiveness. It readily binds loosely to both polar molecules and ionic compounds, giving solvates. There are many crystal structures reported of acetonitrile solvates for compounds prepared in the solvent.

3.3 Ammonia

Ammonia, 3.3, boils at –33 °C at atmospheric pressure. Its high latent heat of vaporization (∆Hv = 23.3 kJ mol‒1) means that it can be handled readily in De war flasks near its boiling point in a fume hood. The liquid is readily obtained by inverting cylinders of ammonia (see Fig. 3.1), although liquid ammonia obtained this way may contain impurities (such as iron salts from the container). Purer liquid ammonia is obtained by condensing ammonia vapour from a cylinder.

Under pressure, liquid ammonia can be used at room temperature. At 0 °C its vapour pressure is 4 atmospheres, while at 20 °C it is 8 atmospheres, both quite modest pressures to handle. Ammonia, like water, is extensively hydrogen bonded, but hydrogen bonds to nitrogen are weaker than to oxygen. Ammonia is less polar than water, as assessed by its physical properties (εr = 23.9 at bp). Chemically, it is a good donor solvent (DN = ca. 50) and transition-metal ammines are readily formed in liquid ammonia. They are even formed in aqueous solutions of ammonia, where the concentration of H2O is many times greater than that of NH3. Current models of liquid ammonia describe it as consisting of units containing seven ammonia molecules, similar to those in solid ammonia. In solid ammonia each nitrogen is surrounded by six nearest-neighbour nitrogen atoms, with the structure bound together by asymmetric, non-linear hydrogen bonds (Fig. 3.2). Each hydrogen atom is covalently bonded to one nitrogen atom, and hydrogen bonded to a second.

Solubilities in liquid ammonia favour non-polar molecular compounds, and inorganic compounds where arnmine formation can take place. Some typical solubilities in liquid ammonia are shown (Tables 3.2 and 3.4). Soluble organic compounds include low RMM aldehydes, ketones, amides, amines, alcohols, and ethers. Both dipole‒dipole attraction and hydrogen bonding between solute and solvent are important factors.

The majority of salts containing highly oxygenated anions such as SO42‒, PO43‒, CO32‒, are virtually insoluble, as are many oxides and hydroxides. The uninegative cations NO3‒ and C1O4‒ promote greater solubility. BaCl2 + 2AgNO3 →H2O 2AgCl↓ + Ba(NO3)2 (3.3)

Liquid ammonia can be made very basic. A 1 M solution of sodium amide, NaNH2, in liquid ammonia has a pH of about 35 compared with the water scale of pH. Such basic solutions (see Section 2.1) are resistant to reduction by group 1 metals, and consequently are very useful as media for group 1 metal reductions.

Metals in liquid ammonia

When group 1 metals, for example sodium, are added to liquid ammonia they dissolve without the violent reaction seen with water. With small quantities of metal, the solutions formed are deep blue, due to the absorption of red light by a short wavelength tail of a very broad and intense band in the near-infrared region, with a maximum at λ = 1500 nm (6700 cm–1). The absorption spectrum is independent of the metal, indicating that it is caused by a common species. The metal is effectively oxidized by loss of an electron (eqn 3.4).

M → Mam+ + e− (3.4)

The electron released is solvated by the ammonia (eqn 3.5) and is responsible for the blue colour.

e− + am → e−am (3.5)

Metal‒ammonia solutions conduct electricity well. At all concentrations the molar conductances are greater than those found for any other known electrolyte in any solvent. The electron is shared amongst a number of solvent molecules in their ground state. The conductance of the ammoniated electron is some three times that of the hydronium ion in water. The electron loosens the solvent structure, and the electrons are thought to reside in cavities in the solvent 3.2 – 3.4 Å in radius.

As more metal is added to the solution the molar conductance first decreases (due to ion association) (eqn 3.6) but then rapidly increases.

M+am + e−am ⇌ (M+am)(e−am) ion pair (3.6)

At the same time the solution becomes metallic in appearance, the deep blue colour giving way to a bronze metallic one. The conductivity approaches that found in metals, with the characteristic negative temperature coefficient for conductance found for metals. The deep blue solutions are paramagnetic, with magnetic susceptibilities supporting the existence of unpaired Page 51, Chapter 3 Some molecular solventsammoniated electrons. As the concentration of metal increases, the molar susceptibility falls, so spin pairing is taking place (eqn 3.7).

e−am + e−am ⇌ (e−am)2 (3.7)

The doubly charged, spin-paired electrons will form ion pairs more readily (eqn 3.8) further reducing the molar conductance in the concentration region where the molar susceptibility is falling.

2M+am + (e−am)2 ⇌ (M+am)2(e−am)2 (3.8)

When adding further group 1 metal to liquid ammonia, two-phase systems are formed. A less dense bronze, metallic phase floats on top of the deep blue, denser (but less concentrated in metal) phase. If ammonia is evaporated from these solutions, the metal is recovered.

Solutions of sodium in liquid ammonia are metastable. Equation 3.9

e−am + NH3 → NH2− + 12H2 (3.9)

is catalysed by high-surface transition-metal compounds, in particular rust (hence the need to take care with liquid ammonia from iron cylinders), and also by platinum black.

While sodium in liquid ammonia has received the most attention, the other group 1 metals and the group 2 metals (except beryllium) behave in a similar way, as do europium and ytterbium.

Reactions of ammoniated electrons

The deep-blue solutions containing ammoniated electrons are excellent reducing agents, which are in very basic solution.

The simplest reactions are reductions of a single species. Thus dioxygen, O2, can be reduced in two stages, firstly to superoxide (eqn 3.10),

O2 + e−am → O−2 (3.10)

then to peroxide (eqn 3.11).

O2− + e−am → O22− (3.11)

Ammoniated electrons reduce permanganate to manganate (eqn 3.12).

MnO4− + e−am → MnO42−. (3.12)

Unusual oxidation states of transition metals can be produced (eqn 3.13).

2[ Ni||(CN)4 ]2−+2e−am→[ Ni|2(CN)6 ]4−+2e−am                                                                                                       →2[ Ni0(CN)4 ]4− (3.13)

Compounds with homonuclear element–element bonds can be reduced (eqns 3.14 and 3.15).

(CO)5 Mn-Mn(CO)5 + 2e−am → 2[ Mn(CO)5 ]− (3.14)
R3Ge-GeR3 + 2e−am → 2R3Ge− (3.15)

Alkyl and aryl halides react readily. The first stage is reduction to a radical (eqn 3.16).

RΧ + e−am + → R⋅+ X− (3.16)

This can either dimerize (eqn 3.17),

2R⋅→ R2 (3.17)

or be further reduced and react with the solvent (eqn 3.18).

R⋅ + e−am + NH3 → RH + NH2− (3.18)

Two examples illustrate these two routes (eqns 3.19 – 3.20).

CH3Br + 2e−am + NH3 → CH4 + Br− + NH2− (3.19)
2Phl + 2e−am → Ph-Ph + 2l− (3.20)

Such reductions by sodium in liquid ammonia are called Birch reductions.

Elements from groups 14 to 16 are reduced by ammoniated electrons to form polynuclear anions, e.g. 3.4 and 3.5, known as Zintl ions from their discoverer (eqns 3.22 – 3.23).

9Sn + 4e−am → Sn94− (3.22)

Electrides and alkalides

Alkali metals dissolve in some amines (e.g. methylamine, ethylenediamine) and some ethers (e.g. tetrahydrofuran) with dielectric constants less than 10 to form deep blue solutions of varying stability. The metal solubilities are far less than in liquid ammonia, and, in general, increase with atomic number. In these solvents the first step is production of solvated electrons (eqn 3.24).

M → M+solv + e−solv (3.24)

The behaviour of group 1 metals in ammonia is not typical of group 1 metals in amines and ethers. Liquid ammonia solvates both M+ cations and electrons well and no further reaction occurs. In these amine and ether solvents the electron can reduce a metal atom to an alkalide M– (eqn 3.25).

M +  e+solv  → M−solv (3.25)

The equilibrium 3.26 is observable and the properties of the solutions reflect

M−solv ⇌ M+solv + 2e−solv (3.26)

the mixture of species M−solv, M+solv,  and e−solv.

As anions, M−solv, will be diamagnetic (filled s shell), with formation of M−solv, the paramagnetism of the solution will decrease. Solutions containing alkalides have a much lower conductance than those containing solvated electrons due to formation of the ion pairs (M+solv)(M−solv) A considerable advance in understanding of these was made by adding a polydentate cryptand ligand to the solution containing alkalides. If added in a 2:1 mole ratio of metal to ligand the cryptand stabilizes the cation (eqn 3.27).Page 54, Chapter 3 Some molecular solvents

M + cryptand → [ M(cryptand) ]+ + M−solv. (3.27)

Where M = K and cryptand = 18-crown-6, 3.6, crystals of the alkalide [M(cryptand)]+M‒ can be grown, and the crystal structure shows that that the large cations are roughly close packed, with the M‒ anions in the octahedral holes. With an excess of cryptand ligand, all M+ is complexed, and the electride, [M(cryptand)]+e‒solv is produced. Examples of these have been crystallized, and show the large cations, with the electrons presumably in the cavities between them.

Acid–base reactions in liquid ammonia

As we have seen in Chapter 2, any species which dissolves in liquid ammonia to produce the ammoniated proton (ammonium ion) will behave as an acid. Such compounds will range from ammonium salts (eqn 3.28),

NH4Cl → NH4+ + Cl− (3.28)

to conventional strong acids like HC1O4 and hydrogen halides, HX (eqns 3.29 – 3.30) and acidic oxides, like SO3 (eqn 3.31).

HClO4 + NH3 → NH4+ + ClO4− (3.29)
HX + NH3 → NH4+ + X− (3.30)
SO3 + 4NH3 → 2NH4+ + [ SO3(NH) ]2− (3.31)

Ammoniated cations (eqn 3.32), and anions like sulfamate (eqn 3.33), also act as acids.

[ Co(NH3)6 ]3+ + NH3 ⇌ [ Co (NH3)5 NH2 ]2+ + NH4+ (3.32)
[ SO(NH2)6 ]− + NH3 →  NH4+ + [ SO3(NH) ]2− (3.33)

Bases give the amide ion. They range from group 1 metal amides, imides, and nitrides (eqns 3.34 – 3.36),

NaNH2 → Na+ + NH2− (3.34)
Na2NH + NH3 → 2Na+ + 2NH2− (3.35)
Li3N + 3NH3 → 3Li+ + 3NH2− (3.36)

to many substances which are bases in water (eqns 3.37 – 3.38).

NaOH + NH3 → Na+ +H2O + NH2− (3.37)
Na2O + 2NH3 → 2Na+ +H2O + NH2− (3.38)

Any of the acids can be neutralized with a base to give a salt and the solvent (eqns 3.39 – 3.40).

NH4Cl + NaNH2 → NaCl + 2NH3 (3.39)
3HClO4 + Li3N → 3LiClO4 + NH3 (3.40)

Acid–base reactions, such as the neutralization of ammonium chloride by potassium amide (eqn 3.39), can be followed by conductance measurements, by potentiometry (using pH electrodes), or by use of an acid–base indicator such as phenolphthalein.

In liquid ammonia the NH4+ and NH2‒ ions responsible for acidity and basicity have similar mobilities to analogous cations and anions, the group 1 cations and halide anions, respectively. This is in marked contrast to H3O+ and OH‒ in water which have much greater mobilities than other ions as conductance is caused by proton switches (Fig. 3.4), not by anion/cation movement.

Metals can show amphoteric behaviour, as in water. When zinc nitrate is treated with potassium amide in liquid ammonia, at first a precipitate of zinc amide is formed (eqn 3.41).

Zn(NO3)2 + 2KNH2 → Zn(NH2)2↓ + 2KNO3 (3.41)

On adding further potassium amide the zinc amide dissolves, to form a zincate anion (eqn 3.42).

Zn(NH2)2 + 2KNH2 → 2K+ + [ Zn(NH2)4 ]2− (3.42)

Addition of ammonium chloride first reprecipitates the zinc amide (eqn 3.43),

K2[Zn(NH2)4] + 2NH4Cl → Zn(NH2)2↓  + 4NH3+ 2KCl (3.43)

then further ammonium chloride redissolves the zinc amide (eqn 3.44).

Zn(NH2)2 + 2NH4Cl → Zn2+ + 2Cl−+ 4NH3 (3.44)

This is exactly analogous to zinc hydroxide in water showing amphoteric properties by dissolving in both acids and alkalis.

3.5 N,N–Dimethylformamide (DMF)

DMF, 3.8, is a good donor solvent with εr at the lower end of the electrolytic solvents. It is completely miscible with H2O, and many organic solvents such as ethers, alcohols, esters, ketones, and aromatic hydrocarbons. A major problem with its use is keeping it water free. It is a good electrochemical solvent, in which Li+, Na+, and K+ can be reduced to the metals without solvent reduction. Strong acids promote solvent hydrolysis, and strong bases decompose the solvent. It is useful as a solvent for titration of weak acids with strong bases such as NaOMe. It dissolves many transition-metal salts and readily forms solvates. Hydrogen bonding is negligible, the high boiling point resulting from dipole–dipole attractions. DMF is a good hydrogen–bond acceptor solvent.

3.6 Dimethyl sulfoxide

Dimethyl sulfoxide, DMSO, 3.9, is a popular solvent, useful when a polar non-hydroxylic solvent is required. Its effective pH range, some 33 units, is long, like that of liquid ammonia. It is relatively easy to protonate DMSO on the sulfoxide oxygen to give the (CH3)2SOH+ cation, but hard to deprotonate to CH3S(O)=CH2‒ (the dimsyl anion). For titrations in DMSO, perchloric acid and tetraalkylammonium hydroxide are the strong acid and base usually used. DMSO is associated by dipole–dipole and van der Waals interactions, which results in a high boiling temperature, but intermolecular hydrogen bonding, if any, is extremely weak. It has a high donor number (29.8) and so reacts with many Lewis acids to give adducts and solvates. It is an example of a solvent with both ‘hard’ O and ‘soft’ S donor atoms. The oxygen will coordinate to the normal ‘hard’ Lewis acid metal ions, while the sulfur will coordinate to ‘soft’ metals ions such as Pt2+. Many molecular and ionic compounds crystallize with solvated DMSO.

3.7 Dinitrogen tetroxide

Dinitrogen tetroxide, 3.10, is a useful solvent for investigating nitrations. It has a fairly narrow liquid range of 30 °C, but its low boiling point means that it can easily be removed from products. The dissociation equilibrium (eqn 3.58)

does not complicate studies, as the amounts of NO2 present in the liquid are small, from 0.01% at the melting point to 0.1% at the boiling point. M + NO+ → M+ + NO (3.64)

3.12 Sulfur dioxide

Sulfur dioxide, 3.15, boiling at ‒10.1 °C, can be used in a Dewar in a well-ventilated environment, but is best used in a vacuum line equipped with grease-free fittings. With its low boiling point it is easy to remove excess. It has a εr of 15.4 at 0 °C, and so is of lower polarity then ammonia. A wide range of alcohols, aldehydes, amines, esters, aromatic compounds, and acids are soluble. Aliphatic hydrocarbons are not soluble. In general, salts are not soluble, but those with polarizable anions such as I‒ and SCN‒ are often soluble. Sulfur dioxide acts as a Lewis acid, and so is an electron-pair acceptor solvent. As a reaction medium liquid sulfur dioxide is fairly inert. It has been used successfully for reactions of SO3, and reactions of oxides of nitrogen. It has been used as a solvent for polymerizations, as it is easy to remove excess from the polymer. It makes a good diluent for superacids, and other Lewis acids, as it is not readily protonated. The unusual cations I3+, Bi53+, Hg32+, and Hg42+, readily decomposed in more basic solvents, can be prepared in sulfur dioxide by AsF5 oxidation of the elements (eqns 3.80 – 3.83).

Properties

Sulfuric acid is a difficult solvent in which to make accurate measurements to identify species present. Two techniques, which have been of much use, are cryoscopy and conductance.

Cryoscopy. The freezing point of a solution is depressed below that of a pure solvent proportionally to the number of solute particles in it. When one mole of NaHSO4 dissolves in sulfuric acid, one mole of Na+ ions and one mole of HSO4– ions are produced. The depression of the freezing point of the solution is twice that expected from the number of moles of NaHSO4 taken. The number of species per mole of solute, v, is thus 2.

These ions conduct by a proton transfer mechanism similar to that of H3O+ and OH‒ in water, see Fig 3.9, with ionic conductances much higher than other ions in the solution. Reactions in superacids

The interest in superacids as solvents comes from their powerful proton-donating properties. Compounds, e.g. alkanes, not normally thought of ai bases, can be protonated in superacids. The alkane neopentane, (CH3)4C, is first protonated, and then decomposes to give the t-butyl cation (eqn 3.107).

(CH3)4C + superacid → [ (CH3)3CCH4+ ] → (CH3)3C+ + CH4 (3.107)

Indeed, the superacid SbF5/HSO3F is sometimes called ‘magic acid’ because candle wax (a long-chain alkane) was seen to dissolve in it. Alkenes, RCH=CH2, are protonated to give carbocations (eqn 3.108).

RCH = CH2 + superacid → RC+H-CH3 (3.108)

With such a rich source of carbocations, extensive use has been made of superacids by organic chemists. The protonated benzene cation, C6H7+, has been isolated as a salt with an inert carborane anion from superacids.

The halogens I2 and Br2 react in superacids (in the presence of the oxidizing agent peroxodisulfuryldifluoride, S2O6F2) to give cations I2+, I3+, I42+, Br3+, while sulfur, S8, with the same oxidant gives S42+, S82+, S192+. Selenium behaves similarly. These catenated main-group cations require superacid conditions, i.e. a very low concentration of any base, to be stable enough to isolate.

The solvent properties of supercritical carbon dioxide can be extended to dissolve more polar molecules by addition of small amounts (usually up to 5 mol %) of a more polar co-solvent. Methanol and acetone are preferred co-solvents. With the pressure of environmental legislation to reduce the amounts of organic solvents used, and possibly Page 70, Chapter 3 Some molecular solventsreleased into the atmosphere, the use of supercritical carbon dioxide is expected to increase. Recently, supercritical carbon dioxide has been used as a solvent for Friedel‒Crafts alkylations, avoiding organic solvents and costly clean-up of products. It is also proposed as a solvent for solvent extraction of metal complexes in nuclear fuel reprocessing.

Use of carbon dioxide in this way is environmentally neutral. Carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere, used, then returned to the atmosphere when finished with. There is no net production of carbon dioxide.
