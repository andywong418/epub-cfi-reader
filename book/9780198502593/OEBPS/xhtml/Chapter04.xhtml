4 Molten framework solids

4.1 Molten salts and oxides

Solid salts and oxides include ionic compounds like NaCl, polar framework–covalent compounds like SiO2, and semi-molecular compounds like HgCl2. Molten framework solids have a long history of use as solvents. Glass making (molten silicon dioxide and calcium oxide) has been carried out since ancient times, with addition of various metal salts as solutes to give the desired colours. Molten salts have been used as electrolytes since Sir Humphry Davy isolated group 1 and 2 metals from their molten hydroxides by electrolysis, starting in 1807.

Such melts can be roughly divided into ionic melts, which are completely ionized; framework/glass melts, which are partially ionized; and molecular melts such as HgCl2, which show little ionization in the melt. Ionic melts may comprise binary ionic compounds (such as NaCl, KCl) or polyatomic cations (such as Et4N+) and anions (such as NO3–, SO42–).

Definitions in molten framework solids

The definitions of acids and bases which are useful in rationalizing chemistry in molecular solvents break down when considering molten framework solids. Definitions based on the species produced in a small amount of self-ionization of a molecular solvent are not appropriate for solvents where there are no ions (molten silicon dioxide), or where solvents are completely ionized (molten KCl). A general definition of acidity was given by Usanovych (1939).

An acid is any material that forms salts with bases by neutralization, gives up cations, combines with anions, or accepts electrons. A base is any material that forms salts with acids through neutralization, gives up anions, combines with cations, or gives up electrons.

This definition is very general, covering Brønsted acid‒base behaviour, Lewis acid‒base behaviour, and extends to describing redox reactions as acid‒base reactions. Although a useful idea, the Usanovych definition is too general to be practical.

Acids and bases react in molten salts. The definitions of acids and bases need to be extended from those used in molecular liquids.

A more limited theory developed by Lux and Flood describes acid‒base behaviour in terms of the oxide ion, O2–, and can be applied to high-temperature melts. The Lux‒Flood approach is to define an acid as an oxide ion acceptor, while a base is an oxide ion donor (eqn 4.1).

Bbase ⇌ Aacid + O2−oxide ion (4.1)

At high temperatures calcium oxide reacts with silicon dioxide to give calcium silicate (eqn 4.2).

CaO + SiO2 → CaSiO3. (4.2)

Here, CaO is behaving as an oxide ion donor (i.e. a base) (eqn 4.3), while SiO2 is an oxide ion acceptor (eqn 4.4)

CaO → Ca2+ + O2− (4.3)
SiO2 + O2− → SiO32− (4.4)

The Lux-Flood definition is clearly closely related to the Lewis definition. The oxide ion, O2‒, is a Lewis base, so in the reaction of CaO with SiO2 the Lewis acids Ca2+ and SiO2 are in competition for the Lewis base O2‒. The Lux‒Flood idea of oxide ion donor/acceptor power is very helpful in understanding chemistry in molten oxide solvents and consequently in geochemistry. A scale of relative acidities/basicities for oxides has been constructed. Oxides like BaO and Cs2O are the most basic, while SO3 and Cl2O7 are the most acidic.

The Lux‒Flood concept of acid–base reactions can be extended to that involving other negative ions, such as F‒ and Cl‒ (eqn 4.5).

The Lux‒Flood definition is useful in ionic melts with high concentrations of anions.

Bbase ⇌ Aacid + X−helide ion (4.5)

The reaction of sodium fluoride with aluminium fluoride (eqn 4.6) to give cryolite

AlF3 + 3NaF → Na3AlF6 (4.6)

can be thought of as a Lewis acid‒base reaction in which the Lewis acid AlF3 combines with three Lewis base ions F‒. Alternatively, in the Lux–Flood terminology, NaF is a fluoride ion donor (i.e. a base) and AlF3 is a fluoride ion acceptor (i.e. an acid).

4.2 Molten halides

The chlorides of the group 1 metals can be liquefied without decomposition, but their melting points are comparatively high, Table 4.1. Melting points can be lowered by mixing the halides to form eutectic mixtures. A mixture of 41 mole% KCl with 59 mole% LiCl has a melting point of 450 °C, and is convenient for study. Its disadvantage is that the small Li+ cations are liable to complex with any other anions present. A 1:1 molar mixture of NaCl and KCl has a melting point of 658 °C. Molten salts conduct electricity well. The specific conductance of molten KCl at 800 °C is 22 times that of a 1 M aqueous solution of KCl at 25 °C. Ionic compounds are very soluble in such molten salts.

Such molten chlorides contain poor Lewis acid cations (group 1 metals cations generally do not complex well), but a high concentration of the good Lewis base Cl‒. They are therefore good media in which to make complex Page 73, Chapter 4 Molten framework solidschlorides. If CrCl3 and NiCl2 are dissolved in molten KCl the anions [CrCl6]3‒and [NiCl4]2‒ are formed, respectively. These anions cannot exist in water. In molten KCl the concentration of Cl‒ is 35 M. Aqueous solutions have a water concentration of 55.5 M. The absence of water, coupled with the high Cl‒ concentration, favours the production of such complex chloro anions.

4.3 Metals in molten salts

Metals will usually dissolve only in one of their own molten salts. So Na will dissolve in molten NaCl, Cd in molten CdCl2. When a more electropositive metal (e.g. Cs) is dissolved in the salt of a less electropositive metal (e.g. KCl) the normal redox reaction will take place so that K+ is reduced and Cs is oxidized (eqn 4.10). The mixture then behaves like K in a KCl melt. Such solutions of metals can be judged on the extent of solute–solvent interactions

Cs + K+ →KCl Cs+ + K (4.10)

Negligible solute–solvent interaction

When potassium dissolves in molten KCl the conductance increases, so that at a mole fraction of 0.42 the conductance is 400 times that of the molten salt alone. Solutions of potassium in molten KCl are deep blue, reminiscent of group 1 metals in liquid ammonia (see Section 3.3). The initial reaction is eqn 4.11.

K → K+ + e− (4.11)

As the concentration of metal is raised, the solution takes on a metallic lustre, although the conductivity is near to that of the molten salt, rather than like that of the metal. This is analogous to the behaviour shown by group 1 metals in liquid ammonia. Of course the reformation of metallic potassium by evaporation of the solvent cannot be done as for liquid ammonia.

Appreciable solvent–solute interaction

Many metals, for example zinc, cadmium, mercury, dissolve in their molten chlorides to give unusual species.

Metal salts where covalent forces are dominant

Mercury(II) halides, HgX2, melt at modest temperatures (HgBr2 230 °C, HgCl2 276 °C). The melts are best regarded as covalent–molecular solvents, rather than ionic. The melts conduct electricity by virtue of self-ionization (eqn 4.16).

2HgX2 ⇌ HgX+[ HgX3 ]− (4.16)

Non-metals in melts

Investigation of I2 dissolved in an ICl/AlCl3 melt shows that polycations I3+ and I5+ can be formed, and the compounds I3[AlCl4] and I5[AlCl4] can be isolated. There is evidence for I2+ in more acidic melts. Tellurium in a TeCl4/AlCl3 melt forms the square-planar Te42+ cation, which can be isolated as Te4(AlCl4)2.

4.4 Low melting salts

Molten salts have high conductance, and are excellent media for electrochemical reactions. Salts which are liquid at room temperature have been vigorously investigated for commercial use. Two salts, N-butylpyridinium chloride (BPC), 4.4, (mp 131.5 °C) and l-methyl-3-ethylimidazolium chloride (MEIC), 4.5, (mp 87 °C) have proved to be particularly useful. When mixed with AlCl3 the melting point is lowered, and these mixtures are liquid at room temperature. For example, l-methyl-3-ethylimidazolium chloride mixed with 40 – 60 mol% AlCl3 is liquid at 25 °C. The AlCl3 also lowers the Lewis basicity of the solvent. These liquid salts have a wide redox stability range of ± 2 V, and so are ideal electrochemical solvents.

Their preparation needs care. Purification of the reagents is necessary. It is essential to keep out water. Aluminium trichloride reacts with water to give HCl and aluminium oxyhalides. The HCl can be removed by addition of EtAlCl2 (eqn 4.20)

EtAlCl2 + HCl → EtH + AlCl3 (4.20)

while aluminium oxyhalides can be removed by addition of phosgene (eqn 4.21).

COCl2 + aluminium oxyhalide → CO2 + AlCl3 (4.21)

These room temperature ionic melts are excellent preparative media for chloro complexes of transition metals, in a similar way to the high Page 76, Chapter 4 Molten framework solidstemperature melts. With their wide electrochemical stability range they offer attractive routes to chloro complexes of unusual oxidation states.

The system 1-methyl-3-ethylimidazolium chloride/copper(I) chloride gives similar room-temperature ionic melts.

Protons in acidic melts

When HCl is dissolved in a 1:1 mixture of 1-methyl-3-ethylimidazolium chloride (MEIC), 4.5, and AlCl3, the mixture behaves as a superacid with Hammett acidity function (see Section 2.1) Ho –12.6. In a 1:2 mixture of MEIC:AlCl3, Ho down to –18 can be obtained. Such highly acidic media are much easier to handle than the conventional superacids of HF/SbF5 or H2SO4/SbF5. In these acidic melts the only hydrogen-containing species is molecular HCl which may be solvated by AlCl4−, Al2Cl7−, and even by MEI+.

Tetraalkylammonium tetraalkylborates

The two room-temperature melts described above both have significant Lewis acid–base properties. Room-temperature ionic melts without such donor–acceptor properties are those based upon tetraalkylammonium tetraalkylborates. The most-studied example is [Et3(n-hexyl)N]+[Et3(n-hexyl)B]–. This salt is liquid at room temperature, but has significantly higher viscosity than a covalent molecular liquid containing similar units, e.g. triethylhexane. Solubility of solutes is similar to those in alkanes. Such liquid ionic compounds appear to have no particular advantages as reaction media.

Nitrates decompose to nitrites on heating (eqn 4.28), so molten nitrates also contain some nitrite.

NO3−⇌NO2−+12O2 (4.28)

Nitrites are stronger Lux–Flood bases than nitrates, with the equilibrium constant for eqn 4.29 some 1010 larger than for nitrate.

NO2−⇌NO−+O2− (4.29)

Molten nitrates thus contain NO2−, O2, NO+, NO2+, and also NO and NO2 formed by reaction of these ions. With this plethora of reactants, molten nitrates are powerful oxidizing agents. It is well to avoid contact with reactive metals such as aluminium, and also cyanides, organic compounds, or other easily oxidized materials, which can be oxidized with explosive power. Nitrate melts are used for electrolytically anodizing metals with a thick coat of metal oxide. Nitrate melts are used for preparation of metal oxides from their salts. Such commercially used oxides as alumina (Al2O3), zirconia (ZrO2), titania (TiO2), hafnia (HfO2), and yttria (Y2O3), which are used as catalysts and catalyst supports, can be so prepared.

4.6 Hydroxide melts as solvents

Molten metal hydroxides were used by Sir Humphry Davy in 1807 in his pioneering electrochemical preparation of group 1 and 2 metals. As sodium and potassium hydroxides have high mps (318 and 360 °C, respectively) studies are usually carried out on the 1:1 mixture (mp 170 °C). The hydroxide ion undergoes autoprotolysis (eqn 4.30).

2OH−⇌H2O + O2− (4.30)

In accordance with the definition of acids and bases in terms of production of the species formed in the self-ionization of a solvent, in molten hydroxides water is acidic, while metal oxides like CaO are basic. The acidity of water can be seen from its violent reaction with sodium metal in a molten hydroxide (eqn 4.31).

Na+H2O→12H2+Na++OH− (4.31)

The redox stability window is about 2 V, from reduction of the group 1 metal cation to the element to oxidation of hydroxide to superoxide.

Molten framework oxides will dissolve ionic oxides, lowering melting points. Thus, SiO2 and B2O3 will dissolve Li2O, CaO, FeO, CoO, and PbO lowering the mp by up to 400 °C. The melts have conductances reflecting the ions in solution. The viscosity is also much lowered. Addition of 2.5 mole% K2O to SiO2 at 1600 °C lowers the viscosity from 2×l06 to 2×l02Pas. As ionic oxides are added the oxide anions disrupt the framework structure of SiO2, and smaller anionic units are formed. So for 50% CaO in SiO2 (wolstonite) the structure consists of Ca2+ cations and Si3O96– anions.

Glass

These oxide melts are the basis for glass making. Melts of SiO2 with dissolved Na2O (added as Na2CO3) and CaO (added as CaCO3) cool to give normal ‘soda’ glass, which comprises 90% of glass production. Addition of 7‒10 % B2O3 forms the familiar borosilicate glass. Solutions of transition-metal oxides impart colour. Thus, added CoO makes blue glass, Cr2O3 green, NiO violet, and FeO green. Specialist glasses are formed by dissolving other additives. For instance, addition of PbO gives lead glass, useful for radiation protection. The solvent properties of SiO2 melts are fundamental to the production of fibre optic materials used for information transfer.
