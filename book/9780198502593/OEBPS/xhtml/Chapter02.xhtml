2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

Introduction

Non-aqueous solvents are used as media for carrying out reactions, and as carriers to deliver solutes to their point of use. In this chapter we look at the various classes of reaction in which the solvent can participate.

2.1 Acid-base reactions in non-aqueous solvents

Acid–base reactions can take place in the gas phase. When HCl and ammonia gases are mixed, ammonium chloride is formed (eqn 2.1).

HCl+HN3→NH4Cl (2.1)

Arrhenius — acids give hydrogen ions in water, and bases give hydroxide ions.

If the gases are dissolved in a solvent the same reaction takes place, but the involvement of the solvent depends on its nature. We shall examine the role of solvents in acid–base reactions in some detail.

Acid–base reactions in water have been extensively studied to provide an insight into the nature of acidity. Arrhenius (1887) defined acids as hydrogen-containing species which dissociate in water to give hydrogen ions. Conversely, bases are hydroxide-containing species which give hydroxide ions in water. Neutralization of acids by bases results from the combination of hydrogen ions with hydroxide ions to give water. There are many acids and bases (e.g. SO3 and NH3) which did not fit into this classification. Brønsted and Lowry (1923) independently proposed a resolution of these problems.

Brønsted–Lowry — acids donate protons to a base. Bases accept protons from an acid.

The Brønsted–Lowry theory (often called Brønsted theory) defines an acid, HA, as a moiety which can donate a proton to a suitable base, B (eqn 2.2).

HA+B⇌A−+HB+ (2.2)

A– is the conjugate base of acid HA, and HB+ is the conjugate acid of base B. So, in water, the solvent acts as a base with acids, e.g. HCl reacts as in eqn 2.3.

HCl+H2O→H3O++Cl− (2.3)

The chloride ion is the conjugate base of hydrogen chloride. By accepting a proton in water, hydronium ions, the conjugate acid of water, H3O+, are produced. Effectively, acids produce H3O+ ions. Bases are proton acceptors, Page 25, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solventsand, in water, the solvent acts as an acid donating a proton to a base. So, with the base ammonia, water reacts as in eqn 2.4.

H2O + NH3⇌OH−+NH4+ (2.4)

The ammonia is protonated, and hydroxide ions are rormed by H2O losing a proton. Bases thus produce hydroxide ions in water. Water, able to act both as a proton donor and proton acceptor, is amphiprotic. Neutralization of an acid by a base in water results from the combination of the H3O+, from the reaction of the acid with water, with OH–, from the reaction of the base with water (eqn 2.5).

H3O++OH−⇌2H2O (2.5)

There are many substances which act as acids in water, yet cannot be written as HA — for instance, SO3. Such compounds first react with H2O to produce a compound which can donate protons (eqn 2.6) and so produce H3O+ ions (eqn 2.7).

SO3+H2O→H2SO4 (2.6)
H2SO4+2H2O→2H3O++SO42− (2.7)

Brønsted theory can be extended to other self-ionizing solvents.

The same principles are readily extended to non-aqueous amphiprotic solvents such as liquid ammonia. Liquid ammonia self-ionizes like water (eqn 2.8).

2NH3⇌NH4++NH2− (2.8)

In liquid ammonia the NH4+ ion is analogous to H3O+ in water, and NH2- is analogous to OH– in water.

Any compound, such as HCl, capable of donating a proton to NH3 is an acid in liquid ammonia, and increases the concentration of NH4+ ions (eqn 2.9).

HCl+NH3→NH4++Cl− (2.9)

Also, any compound which dissolves in liquid ammonia, and reacts to form an intermediate which can donate a proton to an ammonia molecule will be an acid. So carbon dioxide first gives unstable carbamic acid (eqn 2.10).

CO2+NH3→H2NCO2H (2.10)

In liquid ammonia the ammonium ion, NH4+, is produced by acids, just as acids in water give H3O+.

This then reacts to give ammonium carbamate (eqn 2.11)

H2NCO2H+NH3→NH4++H2NCO2− (2.11)

In liquid ammonia, bases give amide ions, either directly (eqn 2.12)

NaNH2→Na++NH2− (2.12)

or by reaction with the solvent, accepting a proton (eqn 2.13).Page 26, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

NaOEt+NH3→Na++NH2−+EtOH (2.13)

Neutralization of an acid by a base in liquid ammonia is the combination of NH4+ and NH2– ions to give ammonia. For example, ammonium chloride will neutralize sodium amide to give sodium chloride (eqn 2.14).

NH4Cl+NaNH2→NaCl+2NH3 (2.14)

Brønsted’s theory of acids and bases, originating in aqueous solutions, can be generalized to any acid–base reactions in any self-ionizing amphiprotic solvent, HS. The self-ionization reaction is eqn 2.15.

2HS⇌H2S++S− (2.15)

The terms lyonium and lyate are derived from the standard endings ‘onium’ for a cation and ‘ate’ for an anion, together with the Greek ‘lusis’ to loosen.

Brønsted acids react to give H2S+ ions, while bases give S–. The ions H2S+ given by acids (H3O+ in water, NH4+ in liquid ammonia) are termed lyonium ions, while the ions S– given by bases (OH– in water, NH2– in liquid ammonia) are termed lyate ions. Acid–base neutralization reactions are reactions of the lyonium ions with lyate ions to give undissociated solvent (eqn 2.16).

H2S++S−→2HS (2.16)

When pKauto is large, calculated concentrations of species in solution are of limited value.

Measurements of such high pKauto values are very difficult, as the results are greatly affected by tiny amounts of solvent impurities. It is thus very hard to tell the difference between a non-self-ionizing solvent and one with an exceedingly small Kauto (pKauto > 35). If a base is added to a solvent with PKauto of 33.5 so that the concentration of lyate is 0.001 mol dm–3, the calculated concentration of lyonium ion is 3 × 10–31 M. If the RMM of the solvent is approximately 100 and its density about 1 g cm–3, one litre will contain some 10 moles, or 6 × 1024 solvent molecules. A volume of 5 million litres of solvent would have only a single molecule present as the lyonium ion! It is obvious that these calculations are more theoretical than practical.

Lewis acids and bases — donor and acceptor acids and bases

In a complementary approach to Brønsted’s, Lewis defined acid–base behaviour in terms of ability to donate or accept a pair of electrons. A Lewis acid is an electron-pair acceptor, a Lewis base is an electron-pair donor. An example is shown in eqn 2.25.

(2.25)

The link with Brønsted’s approach is that the proton, H+, is a Lewis acid, and both Brønsted bases and conjugate bases are Lewis bases. The basis for Brønsted’s theory, where a proton is transferred from proton donor HA to proton acceptor B (eqn 2.1) describes competition of the two Lewis bases A– and B for the Lewis acid H+.

In self-ionizing protogenic solvents, Lewis acids like SbF5 act as Brønsted acids. They increase the concentration of lyonium ion by coordinating a lyate ion (eqn 2.26).

2HS+SbF5→H2S++S−→SbF5 (2.26)

In self-ionizing protogenic solvents, Lewis bases, B, can act as Brønsted bases. They increase the concentration of the lyate ion, S–, by bonding to H+ (eqn 2.27).

B:+HS→B→H++S− (2.27)

HCl+HS→H2S++Cl− (2.28)

An electron-pair acceptor acid acts as a Lewis acid and accepts a lyate ion from the solvent, leaving the lyonium ion (eqn 2.29).

Non-chemists, seeing a row of bottles of aqueous acids in a laboratory, often ask ‘which is the strongest acid?’. Unfortunately it is not easy to give a quick answer. In aqueous solution, acid strength is measured by Ka, which is the equilibrium constant for the reaction of an acid, HA, with water (eqns 2.32 and 2.33).

HA+H2O⇌KaH3O++A− (2.32)
Ka=[H3O+][A−][H2O][HA] (2.33)

Relative acidities of non-aqueous solvents

Water is such a familiar solvent that the pH scale is well appreciated. Non-aqueous solvents range from extremely acidic (e.g. H2SO4) to extremely basic (e.g. liquid ammonia and ethylenediamine). The concept of pH usually applies only to water. The strict definition of pH is an operational one, which defines pH in terms of the EMF produced by electrodes under well-defined conditions (Fig 2.1). This operational definition can be expressed as the negative logarithm of the hydrogen ion activity, aH+, which is the concentration of H+ multiplied by its activity coefficient, γΗ+ (eqn 2.44).

pH=−log10(aH+)=−log10(γH+[H+]/M) (2.44)

It is necessary to divide by M (mol dm–3) to obtain a unitless quantity. You can take the logarithm only of a number, not of a concentration.

For rough calculations it is common to assume that activity coefficients approximate to unity (especially in dilute solution) when eqn 2.44 simplifies to eqn 2.45.Page 32, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

pH=−log10([H+]/M) (2.45)

In water, strong acids at a higher concentration than 1 M give a negative pH. Thus 10 M strong acid would give a pH of approximately –log(10), i.e.–1.

pH measurements can be made in solvents other than water, using a glass electrode and reference electrode as in water. In water, the normal scale of pH extends from 1 M strong acid (pH 0) to 1 M strong base ([OH–] = 1 M, [H+] = 10–14M, pH = 14). The length of the pH scale in water is thus 14 units, the same as pKauto.

The length of the pH scale in an amphiprotic solvent is similarly related to the value of the autoprotolysis constant pKauto. In liquid ammonia at –33 °C, pKauto is 33 and the pH scale is some 33 units long, from 1 M strong acid ([NH4+] = 1 M, pHam = 0), to 1 M strong base ([NH2–] = 1 M, [NH4+] = 10–33 M, pHam = 33). Fig. 2.2 shows this for any amphiprotic solvent, HS.

The question is how to relate scales of pH values in various solvents, namely, how does the acidity of a solution of a strong acid in water compare with the acidity of the same concentration of strong acid in liquid ammonia or acetic acid? There are a number of methods.

Transfer activity coefficients. The pH in a non-aqueous amphiprotic solvent, S, can be related to that in water by eqn 2.46.

pHs=pH+log10Γt(H+)S→water (2.46)

Γt(H+)S→water is the transfer activity coefficient for transferring a hydrogen ion from the non-aqueous solvent, S, to water. Such single ion activity coefficients cannot be measured directly, as there will always be both cation and anion present. Estimated values depend on the technique used for their measurement. These involve electrochemical studies with cells where effects of anions and liquid junction potentials are minimized.

Effective pH. A less precise, but very convenient idea is that of effective pH. This extends the aqueous pH scale at each end and allows acidity in various solvents to be related. Effective pH is the pH an aqueous solution would have if it could be made. Effective pH is an empirical way of estimating pHs. So an effective pH of – 10 implies the same acidity as an aqueous solution would have containing hydrogen ions at a concentration of 1010 mol dm–3. This is clearly a theoretical idea as such an aqueous solution could not be prepared.

Solvent basicity. As well as electrochemical estimates of Γt(H+)S→water another guide is to use pKa values of lyonium ions in water. The pΚa value of NH4+ in Page 33, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solventswater is 9.3, and it is assumed (tested over many solvents) that, pΚa values are roughly dependent on the solvent according to eqn 2.47.

pKa(solv)=pKa(abs)+solvent basicity (2.47)

The relative order is independent of the solvent. The solvent basicity of water is taken as zero, and then pKa(abs) = pKa(water). By definition, pATa(ammonia) of NH4+ is 0. Thus, the solvent basicity factor for liquid ammonia is -9.3. pHammonia 0 corresponds to pHwater 9.3.

Acidity functions. In aqueous solutions it is easy enough to measure pH. It is more difficult with very concentrated aqueous solutions (glass electrodes are then not reliable). This problem was addressed in 1932 by Hammett and Deyrup. They adapted the well-established method of using coloured indicators in aqueous solution. An acid–base indicator is a coloured compound, HIn, which, by loss of a proton, gives a differently coloured conjugate base, In- (eqn 2.48).

colour 1Hln⇌KaH+  colour 2+ln− (2.48)

Spectrophotometric measurements of the concentrations of In and HIn in a solution of known pH, enable the pΚa of the indicator to be determined (eqn 2.49).

pH=pKa+log[In−][HIn] (2.49)

Hammett and Deyrup defined an acidity function Ho by eqn 2.50.

Ho=pKa+log[In−][HIn] (2.50)

They extrapolated from dilute aqueous sulfuric acid to pure sulfuric acid using a series of indicators. Starting with indicator 1, with known pΚa1 in water, a value of Ho can be calculated in dilute sulfuric acid by measuring [Hln1] and [In1–] by spectrophotometry. Then [HIn2] and [In2–] for an indicator 2 with a more negative pKa are measured in the same sulfuric acid solution. As Ho is known pKa2 can be calculated. Then, more concentrated acid is used and measurements of [HIn2] and [In2–] for indicator 2 used to calculate Ho for this acid solution. Also, pKa3 for a third indicator 3 is calculated from [HIn3] and [In3–]. Eventually, using indicators of increasingly negative pΚa values, the acidity of pure sulfuric acid can be determined. This has a Ho of –11.9.

The acidity of solutions more acidic than pH 0 are best described by their Ho values. A similar scale, the H_ scale, is used to describe the acidity of Page 34, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solventssolutions more basic than pH 14 in water. Ho and H_ depend on the particular indicators used, and other acidity scales have been proposed.

Definition and quantitative measurement of pH in non-aqueous solvents is still the subject of research, but Fig. 2.3 gives a guide. Acidic solvents like HF, H2SO4, and AcOH have negative effective pH values. Basic solvents have effective pH values above 14. Hexane has no acid–base properties so its effective pH is determined by the solutes in it. The effective pH range for each solvent is pKauto

A 1 M solution of HClO4 in water has a pH(water) of 0. In liquid ammonia a 1 M solution of HClO4 has a pH(ammonia) of 0. In acetic acid solvent, although HClO4 is a much stronger acid than acetic acid, the dielectric constant of acetic acid is low (6.2) and so HClO4, although completely dissociated in acetic acid, exists largely as ion pairs [AcOH2]+[C1O4]– with only a small part present as free ions. Consequently, the effective concentration of AcOH2+ is lowered. The effective pH range attainable is thus shorter than pKauto by some two units at each end.

The solvents acetonitrile, dimethyl sulfoxide, HMPT, and pyridine contain electronegative atoms, and are readily protonated by strong acids (e.g. HClO4) to give the lyonium ions, HS+. They are very reluctant to lose protons, and autoprotolysis is barely detectable, or non-existent. They are useful for the study of highly basic solutions (superbases). Similarly, the low polarity solvents THF and SO2 can be protonated, but deprotonation is difficult (or impossible for SO2!).

Acid-base titrations in non-aqueous solvents

Non-aqueous solvents are used as titration media for several reasons:Page 35, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

to allow water-insoluble solutes to be used;

to allow solutes which react with water to be used;

to change acidity/basicity patterns to get clear end-points.

The easiest titrations to carry out in water are strong acid/strong base titrations. At the end-point there is a large change of pH on addition of a small volume of titrant (Fig 2.4a) and it is easy to locate the neutralization point (point of inflection). Titration of a weak acid with a strong base, or a weak base with a strong acid, gives a smaller change of pH at the end-point (Fig 2.4b). Titrations involving very weak acids/bases give ill-defined end-points, and location of the end-point is only approximate (Fig 2.4c).

The levelling/differentiating effects of solvents can be exploited to change weak acid/strong base or strong acid/weak base titrations into the more desirable strong acid/strong base titrations, and also to differentiate acids or bases from each other.

Levelling. Phenols are weak acids in water. To titrate them as strong acids it is necessary to use a basic solvent so that they are levelled to be strong acids. Phenols can be successfully titrated with a strong base in ethylenediamine as solvent. The strong base of preference is tetrabutylammonium hydroxide, [Bu4N]OH. This is readily soluble in ethylenediamine, and the tetraalkylammonium phenolate products are soluble too (eqn 2.51).

PhOH+[Bu4N]OH→[Bu4N]OPh+H2O (2.51)

In a similar way, amines are weaker bases than the hydroxide ion, and it is difficult to titrate amines with strong acids in water. In an acetic acid or formic acid solvent such amines are levelled to be strong bases, and can be readily titrated with perchloric acid (eqn 2.52).

RNH2+HClO4→RNH3++ClO4− (2.52)

Page 36, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

In these examples non-aqueous solvents are used to level weak acids and bases to strong acids and bases. Titration end-points for strong acid/strong base titrations are most precisely determined.

Differentiation. Non-aqueous solvents can help in distinguishing between different weak acids and bases. If a mixture of hydrochloric acid, carboxylic acid, and a phenol are titrated with a strong base in ethylenediamine solvent, two end-points are seen (Fig 2.5a). Both HCl and the carboxylic acid behave as strong acids and cannot be distinguished. Phenol behaves as a weak acid. Use of a non-amphiprotic solvent, such as acetone or acetonitrile, enables the acid mixture to be titrated with a strong base and the concentration of each component determined (Fig 2.5b). The solvents have a long potential range.

 

2.2 Redox reactions in non-aqueous solvents

Oxidation and reduction of the solvent

Many redox reactions take place in water. Atmospheric oxygen is in equilibrium with natural waters and their contents. However, reducing agents like metallic Na and LiAlH4 cannot be used in water — they reduce water to hydrogen (eqn 2.53).

2H2O+2Na→H2+2Na++2OH− (2.53)

Page 37, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solventsSimilarly, very powerful oxidants, like fluorine, cannot be used in water — they oxidize water to oxygen (eqn 2.54)

2F2+2H2O→4HF+O2 (2.54)

We can understand these reactions with the help of standard electrode potentials, Eo. At pH 0 (1 M acid) the half-equations for reduction of H+ in water to hydrogen, and oxidation of water to oxygen are eqns 2.55 and the reverse of eqn 2.56.

2H++2e−⇌H2(reduction of H+in water, Eo=0.0 V) (2.55)
O2+4H++4e−⇌2H2O(oxidation of water, Eo=1.23 V) (2.56)

Water can be oxidized to O2 or reduced to H2 by suitable reagents.

Any reductant with a standard electrode potential less than 0 V should reduce hydrogen ions to hydrogen. An oxidant with a standard electrode potential greater than 1.23 V should oxidize the water in aqueous 1 M acid to oxygen. Fluorine has a standard electrode potential well above that required to oxidize water (eqn 2.57) so fluorine oxidizes water to oxygen (eqn 2.54).

F2+2e−⇌2F−,(Eo=2.89 V) (2.57)

Similarly, iron, with a standard electrode potential less than 0 V (eqn 2.58), will reduce 1 M acid to hydrogen (eqn 2.59).

Fe2++2e−⇌Fe,(Eo=−0.44 V)

Theoretically, metallic lead, with a standard electrode potential less than 0 V (eqn 2.60), should reduce 1 M acid to hydrogen,

Pb2++2e−⇌Pb,(Eo=−0.125 V) (2.60)

while permanganate, with a standard electrode potential above 1.2 V (eqn 2.61), should oxidize water to oxygen.

MnO4−+8H++5e−⇌Mn2++4H2O,(Eo=1.52 V) (2.61)

These reactions are thermodynamically possible, but proceed extremely slowly. Practically, there is a ‘kinetic stability zone’ of about 0.5 V where reactions should take place, but may be very slow. So at pH 0 redox systems with standard electrode potentials in the range –0.5 V to +1.7 V can be studied, a range of over 2 V.

Standard electrode potentials in water vary with pH if there are hydrogen ions in the half-equation. The application of the Nernst equation to the reduction and oxidation of water reactions gives the EMF, EpH∗, at different pH values (eqn 2.62).Page 38, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

N2++6NH4++6e−⇌8NH3(Eo=0.04 V) (2.66)

Much modern electrochemistry is carried out in non-aqueous solvents to study species which would react with water. Redox titrations in non-aqueous solvents. Powerful oxidants such as chlorine and iodine, and even potassium permanganate, are not stable in water — they slowly oxidize it. In order to use powerful oxidants as titrants it is necessary to use solvents which are themselves resistant to oxidation. Acetic acid, although a solvent of low polarity, has proved the most useful, and oxidations by bromine in acetic acid can be used to determine sulphanilamide drugs. Acetonitrile is another solvent, more polar than acetic acid, which is resistant to oxidation and can thus be used with powerful oxidants.

2.3 Solvation, solvolysis, solubility, and solvates

Solvation

When a cation is dissolved in water, a polar solvent, the water molecules orient around the cation, forming hydration shells. The closest water molecules (primary hydration shell) are most firmly attached. The second shell of water molecules (secondary hydration shell) becomes important for cations of high charge (2+ and above), and for small cations like Li+ and Be2+. It is difficult to determine the number of water molecules attached in the hydration shells. NMR can be used to differentiate between bulk solvent and water molecules in the primary hydration shell, and is the best method of determining the primary hydration number. However, the time-scale of NMR experiments (10–5 s) is slower than the rate of exchange of water between the bulk solvent and the cation hydration shells for many cations, and NMR experiments at low temperatures in aqueous acetone are necessary to distinguish between bulk solvent and primary hydration sphere water molecules. NMR evidence suggests that there are six water molecules in the primary hydration sphere for many cations, apart from Li+ and Be2+ which have four.Page 42, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

Solvation of cations in non-aqueous solvents has been studied far less than hydration. The NMR method has been applied to cations in methanol, ethanol, ammonia, acetonitrile, dimethylformamide, and dimethyl sulfoxide. The results indicate that there are six solvent molecules in the primary solvation sphere round most cations, except for Li+, Na+, and Be2+ which have four.

Solvent molecules in the primary and secondary solvation spheres are continually exchanging their positions with solvent molecules from the bulk solvent (eqn 2.73).

[ MSn ]m+solvatedcation + S*bulksolvent ⇌ [ MS(n−1)S* ]m+ + S (2.73)

The exchange rates have been measured for aqueous cations by NMR. Far fewer studies have been made on non-aqueous solvents. The broad conclusion is that solvent molecules larger than water (methanol, ethanol, acetonitrile, dimethylformamide, and dimethyl sulfoxide) exchange up to 10 times slower than water, but ammonia exchanges somewhat faster (Table 2.9).

Anions are also solvated by dipolar solvent molecules. The positive regions of the solvent are attracted to the anion. Solvation numbers for anions have been studied less than for cations, but estimates from thermodynamic data indicate that anion solvation numbers are lower than those for cations. Anions can act as hydrogen-bond acceptors to hydrogen-bond donor solvents (mainly the amphiprotic solvents)(Fig. 2.9). Solvation free energies of anions are high compared with those of similarly sized cations. For example ∆G°hydration for Na+ and F– are –378 kJ mol–1 and –459 kJ mol–1, respectively.Page 43, Chapter 2 Chemistry in non-aqueous solvents

Solubility-governed reactions

The prediction of solubility of substances in solvents is difficult. Molecular substances dissolve to give the molecules solvated by the solvent, instead of being packed together in a solid. Ionic compounds dissolve to break the electrostatic forces binding anions and cations together, with the anions and cations separately solvated in the solvent (Section 1.6).

For a solute to dissolve, the free energy of solvation of the solute must exceed the free energy involved in breaking solvent–solvent bonds and solute–solute bonds. It is possible to choose solvents so that one desired product is insoluble. These are metathetical reactions (sometimes named double decompositions).

The well-known precipitation of silver chloride (eqn 2.74),

AgNO3 + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO3 (2.74)

can be carried out in sulfuric acid and acetic acid solvents, as well as in water. However, AgCl is soluble in liquid ammonia (see Section 3.3) so this is an inappropriate solvent to use. Suitable choice of solvent can facilitate the preparation of compounds readily hydrated in water. Thus, anhydrous CuSO4 can be prepared in acetic acid (eqn 2.75) and CuCl2 in sulfuric acid (eqn 2.76).

Cu(NO3)2 + H2SO4 →HOAc CuSO4↓ + 2HNO3 (2.75)
CuSO4 + 2NaCl →H2SO4 CuCl2↓ + Na2SO4 (2.76)

Tertiary amines, R3N, and alkyl halides, R′X, are soluble in fairly non-polar solvents like diethyl ether, but react together to give a precipitate of the insoluble ionic tetraalkylammonium salts (eqn 2.77).

R3N + R′X → R3R′N+X−↓ (2.77)

Solvolysis

Reaction with the solvent, where the solvent reacts but is not oxidized or reduced, is called solvolysis. The name of the solvent is sometimes incorporated into the description, viz. ethanolysis (reaction with ethanol) and ammonolysis (reaction with ammonia). Reactants which are cations, anions or neutral molecules can react with the solvent.

Solvolysis implies splitting the solvent molecule, and so excludes simple solvation processes.

 

Cations. Cations generally react with their solvation shell, so in liquid ammonia cobalt(III) cations are acidic (eqns 2.78 and 2.79).

[ Co(NH3)6 ]3+ + NH3 ⇌ [ Co(NH3)5(NH2) ]2+ + NH4+ (2.78)
(2.79)

This is the equivalent of cation hydrolysis in water. The factors (small cation size, large charge) which favour hydrolysis are similarly applicable.

 

Anions. Anions undergo solvolysis and, dependent on their properties, give (most commonly) the solvent anion (lyate ions) or (more rarely) the solvent cations (lyonium ions). So, in acetic acid, hydroxide ions will take a proton from the solvent to give acetate (eqn 2.80).

OH− + HOAc ⇌ H2O + OAc− (2.80)

whereas in liquid ammonia, hydrogen sulfate loses a proton to the solvent giving ammonium (eqn 2.81).

HSO4− + NH3 ⇌ SO42− + NH4+ (2.81)

Neutral molecules. Molecules with labile bonds can be solvolysed. This is especially noted with molecular halides. Prediction of the products can be made by considering the bond polarities (see Fig. 2.10). The oxohalide sulfuryl chloride thus solvolyses in acidic, neutral, and basic amphiprotic solvents, in each solvent giving the solvent lyonium ion (eqns 2.82 – 2.84).

SO2Cl2 + 4 HOAc → SO2(OAc)2 + 2 H2OAc+ + 2Cl − (2.82)
SO2Cl2 + 4 EtOH → SO2(OEt)2 + 2 EtOH2+ + 2Cl − (2.83)
SO2Cl2 + 4 NH3 → SO2(NH2)2 + 2 NH4+ + 2Cl − (2.84)
