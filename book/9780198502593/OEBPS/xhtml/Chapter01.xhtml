1.1 Introduction

Although water is the most abundant liquid on this planet, and is essential for living systems, non-aqueous liquids are widely used as solvents. In this chapter we will examine the range of liquid solvents available, and discuss how choice of solvents is related to the various solvent properties.

What are solvents for?

A reaction medium. Solvents are used to bring reactants together at suitable concentrations. For endothermic reactions heat can be supplied readily by heating the solvent; for exothermic reactions the solvent can act as a heat sink. If necessary, surplus heat can be removed by allowing the solvent to boil. After the reaction, products have to be freed of solvent. Solids do not react together well. Even finely ground powders (say 1 μm diameter) still have particles containing 109 formula units (if RMM = ~100) and complete mixing of reactants is difficult. As diffusion in solids is slow, solid–solid reactions are slow at room temperature. To get reactants to mix at a molecular level requires use of the gas phase, or solution. Gas-phase reactions are limited to those which involve volatile reactants. High pressures are necessary to increase reactant concentrations. A gas pressure of 24 atmospheres at 25 °C is required to give a concentration equivalent to that in a 1 molar solution in a solvent. When solutions are used, the concentrations of reactants can be readily adjusted over some ten orders of magnitude. Compounds too reactive to study in one solvent can be used in another solvent.

In solution, reactants can mix at a molecular level.

Solute concentrations can be varied greatly.

A reactant. Solvents frequently react with solutes. Substances insoluble in one solvent can dissolve in another by reacting with it. Thus, bone [hydroxyapatite, Ca5(OH)(PO4)3] is insoluble in most solvents but dissolves in 100% sulfuric acid, with protonation of the phosphate. When any solute is dissolved in a solvent possible solute–solvent reactions must always be considered. Solvents can be used to modify the properties of solutes. Nitric acid dissolved in water behaves quite differently to nitric acid dissolved in concentrated sulfuric acid.

A carrier. Solutions are a convenient way of delivering chemical compounds to their point of use in the required quantities. Samples are dissolved in solvents for injection to mass spectrometers and chromatographs; NMR and UV/Vis spectra are routinely measured in solvents. The ready removal of the solvent by evaporation leaves the solute where it is required. Coatings (e.g. paint, adhesive) can be applied and then the solvent removed. In solvent extraction, compounds dissolved in one solvent (e.g. water) are shaken with another solvent immiscible with water. Solutes which are more soluble in the non-aqueous solvent than in water are concentrated into the non-aqueous phase.

Solvents can transport a solute to where it is needed.

1.2 Polarity, polarization, and polarizability

What do ‘polar’ and ‘polarity’ mean?

The word ‘polar’ is used frequently when referring to solvents. We need to examine carefully the concept of polarity, as it can apply to individual bonds, individual molecules, or to a bulk solvent.

Polarity in molecules

In a simple homonuclear diatomic molecule, like dihydrogen or dinitrogen, the electron distribution is symmetrical, both atoms have the same electron density. The bond and molecule are non-polar. In a heteronuclear diatomic molecule, like hydrogen fluoride, there is charge separation. Electrons are drawn towards the electronegative fluorine atom, and so the hydrogen atom is slightly positive. The H–F bond is polar, and as the molecule has a dipole it is dipolar (often called polar). In polyatomic molecules the polarity of the molecule is broadly determined by the combined effect of all the bond dipoles present. Polyatomic molecules can be dipolar like water, with a net dipole, or like carbon dioxide with no net dipole. The charge distribution in carbon dioxide is not uniform since the oxygen atoms are negatively charged, leaving the carbon somewhat positive. Although carbon dioxide is a non-dipolar molecule, the C–O bonds are still polar.

Dispersion forces

Molecules which have no permanent dipole still have their electrons in movement. Although the time-averaged distribution of electrons is Page 5, Chapter 1 General propertiessymmetrical, at any instant the electrons are not uniformly distributed, so the molecule has a small instantaneous dipole, μ. This instantaneous dipole can polarize electrons in a neighbouring molecule, giving a small dipole in the neighbour, and the dipole–dipole interaction resulting in attraction between the molecules. This is the dispersion attraction responsible for molecules sticking together. These dispersion forces (sometimes called London forces after the theoretician F. London) are the weakest of all intermolecular forces. If the distance between two molecules is r, the dispersion interaction energy is proportional to 1/r6, so dispersion forces only operate over very short ranges. The van der Waals forces comprise the repulsive forces between electrons and nuclei on adjacent molecules, as well as the dispersion attractions.

Polarization and polarizability

In most liquids, molecules are randomly arranged and in constant movement. If the molecules have a dipole but there is no applied electric field the dipoles are randomly orientated (Fig. 1.3a). Dipole–dipole interactions between rotating dipolar molecules are small. Only for molecules with large dipoles, and where hydrogen bonding is absent, such as DMSO and acetonitrile, do dipole-dipole attractions contribute significantly to molecular association. The polarization, the average electric dipole per unit volume, is zero. In an electric field, such as that caused by a solute ion, the random orientations of dipoles disappear as the dipoles align along the direction of the electric field (Fig. 1.3b). This results in orientation polarization of the liquid in the electric field around the ion (Fig 1.3c).

Even if the liquid molecules have no net dipole, electrons in the molecule will be affected by an electric field. The ability of electrons to move in the presence of an electric field is called their polarizability. The movement of electrons results in an induced dipole, and the alignment of the induced dipoles with the electric field gives induced polarization.

Polarity of a solvent

In discussing non-aqueous molecular solvents the idea of polarity is central. The idea of solvent polarity refers not to bonds, nor to molecules, but to the Page 6, Chapter 1 General propertiessolvent as an assembly of molecules. Qualitatively, polar solvents promote the separation of solute moieties with unlike charges and they make it possible for solute moieties with like charges to approach each other more closely. Like the concept of electronegativity, the more precise you want the definition of polarity, the harder it is to define. Polarity is thus a rather ill-defined term. It covers the solvent’s overall solvation capability (solvation power) for solutes. The polarity depends on the action of all possible, nonspecific and specific, intermolecular interactions between solute ions or molecules and solvent molecules. It covers electrostatic, directional, inductive, dispersion, and charge-transfer forces, as well as hydrogen bonding forces, but excludes interactions leading to definite chemical alterations of the ions or molecules of the solute.

1.3 Assessment of solvent polarity

Assessment of solvent polarity on a quantitative scale has been the subject of much work. Polarity scales have used physical properties of the solvent. These include both bulk properties, such as dielectric constant (relative permittivity), refractive index, latent heat of fusion, and vaporization, and molecular properties, such as dipole moment. A second set of polarity assessments has used measures of the chemical interactions between solvents and convenient reference solutes. The polarity discussion is further hampered by the point of view of the user. To a synthetic organic chemist dichloromethane may be a polar solvent (considered in relation to toluene and hexane), whereas to an inorganic chemist, who is used to water, liquid ammonia, and concentrated sulfuric acid, dichloromethane has low polarity.

Polarity is a measure of a solvent’s solvation capability.

1.4 Polarity assessment from physical properties

Cohesive pressure of a solvent and solubility parameter

As a cavity has to be opened in a solvent in order to introduce a solute, the strength of solvent–solvent bonding will be a factor in determining solubility. The cohesive pressure, c, is defined by eqn 1.1.

C=ΔHvap−RTVm (1.1)

The cohesive pressure is a measure of the ‘stickiness’ of a solvent, the ease with which a cavity can be created for a solute.

where ∆Hvap is the molar enthalpy of vaporization and Vm is the molar volume. It reflects that all solvent–solvent bonds are absent in the vapour, and so covers the total strength of all solvent–solvent forces in the liquid. As a measure of solvent polarity it ignores all solvent–solute interactions, so works best when these are small, as for non-polar solutes dissolving in low polarity solvents. Hildebrand defined the solubility parameter, δ, as the square root of the cohesive pressure, (δ = √c), and showed that liquids which have similar solubility parameters (in practice within ±3 of each other) are miscible, while those with dissimilar solubility parameters are immiscible. The concept has been very useful and successful in predicting solubilities of non-electrolyte solutes in low polarity solvents. The cohesive energy, ΔE, is composed of a non-polar component, ΔEnp, from the dispersion forces between solvent molecules, a polar component, ΔEp, from the interaction of dipoles, and a hydrogen-bonding component, ∆EH, if hydrogen bonding takes place (eqn 1.2).

ΔE=ΔEnp+ΔEp+ΔEH (1.2)

Accordingly, the solubility parameter, δ, is composed of a non-polar part, δnp, a polar part, δp, and a hydrogen-bonding part, δΗ (eqn 1.3).

δ2=δnp2+δp2+δH2 (1.3)

The cohesive energy ΔΕ is the energy associated with the net attractive interactions of a material.

Dielectric constant (relative permittivity), εr

The dielectric constant (also called relative permittivity), εr, of a solvent is measured by placing it between the plates of a capacitor. If the electric field strength between the capacitor plates in a vacuum is Eo this is lowered to E when a solvent is introduced (Fig. 1.6). The dielectric constant is given by eqn 1.4.

εr=EoE (1.4)

Dielectric constants range from 2 (alkanes) to over 100 (N-methylacetamide, 191). Between the charged plates, molecules with permanent or induced dipoles are aligned into an ordered arrangement. Force∝q1q2r2εr (1.5)

Thus, the force attracting two ions of opposite unit charge is about 40 times less if they are in water, εr = 79, compared with an alkane (hexane), εr = 2. Ion pairs (see Section 1.7) are common in low polarity solvents. The dielectric constant of the solvent also appears in the Born equation, eqn 1.6. This calculates the Gibbs free energy of solvation, ΔGso of a mole of ions of charge ze (z the charges number, e the elementary charge) from considering transferring them from free space, εr = 1, to a solvent with dielectric constant εr (NA is the Avogadro constant).

ΔGso=−NAz2e28πεor(1−1εr) (1.6)

The usual ionic radii in tabulations are derived from interionic distances in crystalline solids, and may not be very reliable in solvation models. Also, the dielectric constant of a solvent near an ion may differ from that of the bulk liquid. Although the Born equation is only an approximation, it does indicate that free energies of solvation of ions will be larger as the solvent dielectric constant increases.

Dipolar solutes and solvent. In a similar way, Kirkwood calculated the Gibbs free energy change of a molecule (assumed spherical with radius r and dipole moment μ) going from free space to a solvent with dielectric constant εr by eqn 1.7.

ΔGso=−NAμ24πεor3(εr−1)(2εr+1) (1.7)

Accordingly, as the free energies of solvation of both ions and dipolar molecules are affected, dielectric constants are widely used as a measure of polarity. Such a simple approach is bound to have limitations. This electrostatic approach treats the solvent as a continuum, not as a collection of interacting molecules. Consequently, interactions between solvent molecules, such as hydrogen bonding, are not specifically treated. Similarly, specific solvent–solute interactions such as hydrogen bonding, or interactions between electron pairs on the solvent and electron-poor regions of the solute, are ignored.

The dielectric constant is a measure of the polarization of a solvent. It measures how solvent dipoles align with an electric field, and also includes the dipoles induced in an electric field.

Dipole moment

It might be thought that the molecular property, dipole moment, μ, would be a good measure of polarity. However, dipole moments are calculated for individual molecules. Solvent–solvent and solvent–solute interactions are poorly calculated from μ, as models generally assume point dipoles.

Dipole moments are determined from dielectric constant and refractive index measurements. The dielectric constant, εr, gives a measure of polarization (induced) + polarization (orientational). The refractive index, nD, gives a measure of polarization (induced), as orientational polarization is absent at the high frequencies of visible light. These measurements then give (by difference) polarization (orientational), and thus the dipole moment.

Table 1.9 shows the dipole moments of some common solvent molecules. While dipole moments are high for HMPA and DMSO, the values for water, HF, and THF are similar. Water and HF, which both readily dissolve ionic compounds, are much more polar solvents than THF. The use of dipole moments as a polarity indicator, seriously under-estimates the polarities of water and HF as solvents.

1.5 Polarity assessment from chemical properties

The assessment of polarity by physical properties of the solvent is an attractive idea, but will always ignore chemical interactions between solvent and solute. There have been many attempts to assess solvent polarity in a more chemical way. The most important of these are now described.

Donor number

An important chemical measure of solvent polarity is the donor number, DN, devised by Viktor Gutmann. It is a measure of the Lewis base donor power of the solvent. DN is defined as the negative enthalpy for the reaction of the solvent with the standard Lewis acid SbCl5 (eqn 1.8). The enthalpy measurements are carried out in an inert solvent, 1, 2-dichloroethane, which has a donor number of zero. The units are kcal/mol for historical reasons (1 kcal = 4.18kJ).

(1.8)

DNs range from zero (solvents like hexane, tetrachloromethane), through modest donors (acetonitrile 14.1, acetone 17), to good donors like water (18), to superb donors like DMSO (29.8) and, best of all, HMPA (38.8) (Table 1.10). In fact, solvents with hydroxyl groups, like alcohols and water, solvolyse SbCl5, and so DNs are assigned by indirect methods. The DN reflects the solvent’s ability to solvate cations, Mn+, and other Lewis acids. The DN enables us to rationalize why a solvent such as nitromethane, (εr = 35.8) is considered to be fairly non-polar, although it has a higher dielectric constant than diethyl ether (εr = 4.2) and tetrahydrofuran (εr = 7.6) which are often thought to be more polar solvents than their dielectric constants would indicate. The DN of nitromethane is only 2.7, compared with that of 19.2 for diethyl ether and 20 for tetrahydrofuran. These ether solvents are much better electron-pair donors than nitromethane.

In SbCl5 the Sb atom formally has 10 valence electrons. It can achieve 12 valence electrons and become isoelectronic with the sulfur atom in sulfur hexafluoride by forming the adduct S–SbCl5with a solvent molecule, S.

Acceptor number and the Ε TΝ scale

The converse of the donor number is the acceptor number, AN, of a solvent. This is a measure of its power to accept electron pairs as a Lewis acid. There are a number of ways of assessing the acceptor ability. One convenient way is to use NMR to measure the interaction between a standard reference Lewis base, (C2H5)3PO, and the solvent by looking at the 31P shift (eqn 1.9).

(1.9)

The standards are set as zero for solvent hexane, and 100 for solvent SbCl5. The range of ANs is thus similar to that for DNs.

Another widely used solvent parameter, especially popular with organic chemists, is the ΕTΝ parameter proposed by Dimroth and Reichardt. The UV spectrum of the dye 1.1 strongly depends on the nature of the solvent. There is a change in electron distribution in the ground and excited states, and consequently the two states have different dipole moments. The longest wavelength band, λs, lies in the visible region. Thus, for diphenylether λs = 810 nm, while for water λs = 453 nm. The dye has a different colour according to the interaction with the solvent – it is solvatochromic. The transition energy of the longest wavelength solvatochromic absorption band(in units of kcal/mol) gives the solvent parameter ΕT [sometimes written as ET(30)]. This ranges from 30.7 (for tetramethylsilane, TMS, little interaction with 1.1) to 63.1 (for water). A normalized parameter, ETN, lying between 0 (TMS) and 1 (water) is more convenient to use than ΕT. The ΕTΝ solvent parameter measures two aspects of a solvent. Firstly, it measures non-specific interactions between the dye, 1.1, and the solvent. Secondly, because of the negative phenolic oxygen atom of 1.1, it measures the Lewis acidity of the solvent. The Lewis base properties of the solvent are not measured, as the positive charge associated with the pyridine’s nitrogen is buried within the molecule and is also extensively delocalized.

Solvatochromic compounds change colour as solvent polarity changes.

Other solvatochromic methods of solvent assessment

As the assessment of solvent acceptor power by the ETN method proved so successful and easy to do, further solute molecules were sought which would give colour changes dependent upon the Lewis acid–base properties of the solvent, and the general solvent polarity other than Lewis acid–base properties. This work is proving successful, although not yet as widely accepted as the ΕTΝ assessment of solvent Lewis acidity.

The molecules 1.2 and 1.3 can both act as Lewis bases (and hydrogen-bond acceptors, HBA) via the oxygen atoms of the nitro groups. Molecule 1.2 can hydrogen bond to a hydrogen-bond acceptor solvent (see Section 1.7) via the NH2, whereas 1.3 cannot. By comparing the ways the UV/Vis spectra of 1.2 and 1.3 change when they are placed in a series of solvents the Lewis base, HBA properties of the solvent can be quantified (the β scale). Similarly both 1.1 and 1.4 have nitrogen and oxygen atoms, but the betaine 1.1 has an O– which very willingly hydrogen bonds to a hydrogen-bond donor (HBD) solvent. Studies of the UV/Vis spectra of molecules akin to these in solvents give rise to a quantitative assessment of the hydrogen-bond donor capacity of a solvent (the α scale). Finally, a measure of ‘solvent dipolarity/polarizability’ (the π* scale, including both polarization and polarizability) can be established by looking at p→π* and π→π* electronic transitions in the UV/Vis spectra of 1.2, 1.3, and 1.4 in various solvents. Hardness and Softness in solvents

The HSAB (hard and soft acids and base) principle is that ‘hard’ acids prefer to interact with a ‘hard’ base, and ‘soft’ acids with ‘soft’ bases. ‘Hard’ bases are not polarizable, and include those with O donor atoms. ‘Soft’ bases are more polarizable, and include S-donor bases. When assessing solvents the idea of ‘hardness’ and ‘softness’ has been explored, but is not yet widely employed. Solvent hardness/softness can be assessed by comparing the Gibbs free energy of transfer of a ‘soft’ cation like Ag+ from ‘hard’ water to the solvent with the Gibbs free energy of transfer of similarly sized ‘hard’ cations like Na+ and K+. Summary

In choosing solvents for any application some assessment of the solvent polarity is necessary. Quantitative measures of polarity become increasingly more complex as the chemical nature of the solvent is considered. If the application involves low polarity solutes, then dielectric constant, together with cohesive pressures, enable a reasonable selection of solvents. Solvents of higher polarity will be required to dissolve more polar compounds, or ionic compounds. Simple concepts such as dielectric constant need to be supplemented by more rigorous assessments of a solvent’s Lewis acid–base strengths. The choice can be made by using a combination of dielectric constant, as a broad polarity measure, followed by an assessment of the solute–solvent donor–acceptor interactions. Use of DN and AN (or ETN) can then be applied. Helpful as these assessments of solvent polarity are, it is important to bear in mind the existence of specific solvent–solute interactions, and possible chemical reaction of the solute with the solvent.

1.6 Correlation of physical properties of solute with solvent

Many physical properties of compounds are sensitive to the solvent in which they are dissolved. A simple, but effective, approach is to suggest that the solvent exerts influence on the solute depending only on its Lewis acid–base properties. This gives eqn 1.10.

Q=Qo+a(Lewis acidity)+b(Lewis basicity) (1.10)

Q is the value of the physical property in a solvent, while Qo is its value in a solvent with zero Lewis acid–base properties. If ETN is used as a measure of Page 15, Chapter 1 General propertiesthe Lewis acidity of the solvent and the DN is used as a measure of the solvent’s Lewis basicity, we can write eqn 1.11.

Q=Qo+αETN+βDN (1.11)

Solubility

For a solute A to be soluble in a solvent S, the S–S intermolecular forces must be broken to create a cavity in the solvent Also A–A intermolecular (or inter-ionic) forces must be broken to split the solute into its constituents. Both these processes require energy. When the solute is in the solvent S–A interactions can take place. If these are large enough they compensate for breaking the S–S and A–A links.

Ionic compounds are an important group of compounds. We can use a Gibbs free energy cycle to analyse the dissolution of an ionic solid, MX, consisting of M+ cations and X– anions, in a solvent.Page 16, Chapter 1 General properties

The Gibbs free energy of solution, ∆Go (Solution), of MX comprises first breaking up the lattice into gaseous ions, (Gibbs free energy ∆G° (LE)) and then solvating the ions (Gibbs free energy ∆Go(solv, ion)). From the Gibbs free energy cycle we get eqn 1.12.

ΔGo(solution)=ΔGo(LE)+ΔGo(solv,M+)+ΔGo(solv,X−) (1.12)

The solubility of MX in a solvent is usually measured by a solubility product, Ksp. In a saturated solution of MX, [M+] and [X–] satisfy eqn 1.13.

Ksp=[ M+ ][ X− ] (1.13)

and this is related to ∆Go (solution) by eqn 1.14.

ΔGo(solution)=−RTlogeKsp (1.14)

If a compound is fairly soluble, such that its saturated solution is 0.1 M, Ksp ≈ 10–2 M2. If it is fairly insoluble, so that a saturated solution is 1 × 10–6 M, Ksp is 10–12 M2. The Gibbs free energy difference between these two is 57 kJ mor–1at 25°C.

For NaCl, ∆Go(LE) is 763 kJ mol–1. The sum of the ionic hydration free energies is –722 kJ mol-1 (–375 kJ mol–1 for Na+, –347 kJ mol–1 for Cl–). The difference between ΔGo(LE) and ΔGo(solv, Na+)+ΔGo(solv,Cl−) is 41 kJ mol. Solution free energies for ionic compounds are the difference between two large quantities, and small difference in solution free energies are reflected in large differences in observed solubility.

The solubility of an ionic compound depends on the difference between large quantities — lattice Gibbs free energy and ionic solvation free energies. Small changes in solution free energies cause big changes in solubilities.

On changing solvents from water to a non-aqueous solvent, solubilities will be affected as the free energies of solvation of the ions will change. The Born equation (eqn 1.6) attempts to calculate free energies of ion solvation from the solvent dielectric constant and the size of the ion. It is only a rough approximation, which is satisfactory if the order of magnitude of ∆Go (solv, ion) is required, but uncertainties in ΔGo(solve,M+) are of the order of the 57 kJ mol–1 found comparing ∆Go (solution) of a soluble compound with that of a fairly insoluble compound. The Born approximation indicates that as εr decreases, so will solvation free energies. The sum ΔGo(solve,M+)+ΔGo(solve, X−) will be less Page 17, Chapter 1 General propertiesnegative, so ∆Go (Solution) will be more positive — MX will dissolve less in a solvent that is less polar than water (which has a high εr).

In general, ionic compounds are most soluble in dipolar solvents with high εr. They are usually more soluble in hydroxylic amphiprotic solvents such as water and methanol than in the non-protogenic solvents like acetonitrile. Molecular compounds need to be matched to their solvent. Molecular compounds where dipole–dipole interactions between molecules are absent or weak will dissolve in solvents where these intermolecular interactions are weak. Non-polar fats and waxes are soluble in low polarity hydrocarbons. Polar molecules with intermolecular hydrogen bonds, like sugars, are soluble in polar hydrogen-bonded solvents.

 

Modelling solvents

Many theoreticians have attempted to model solvents. A popular model is the ‘mean spherical approximation’ which treats solvent molecules as hard spheres with a point dipole at the centre. The model requires molecular information consisting of:

(a) the dipole moment,

(b) the molecular diameter (derived from gas solubilities),

(c) the molecular polarizability.

The aim of the calculations is to predict bulk properties of a solvent such as density, enthalpy of vaporization, heat capacity, and dielectric constant form the molecular data. Whilst such modelling is quite successful for many properties, successful prediction of dielectric constants is still difficult. Such models predict the dielectric constant of water to be considerably higher than that measured. It is clear that solvents like ethanol and acetonitrile cannot be accurately thought of as hard spheres with a central dipole. The models have been extended by including a fourth, ‘stickiness’, parameter. This allows for (a) electrostatic quadrupoles as well as the dipole and (b) the solvent molecule departing considerably from being spherical. With this fourth parameter good agreement between measured and predicted dielectric constants is observed for both polar and non-polar solvents. As computers become ever more powerful, it becomes possible to simulate larger assemblies of solvent molecules, with more refined estimates of the interactions between them.

Even the simplest models of liquids require many different parameters.

Electrolytic and non-electrolytic solvents

Solvents of high polarity (water, dimethyl sulfoxide, etc.) dissolve ionic compounds to give solutions which conduct electricity well. They are thus suitable solvents for electrochemical reactions and are conveniently called electrolytic solvents. (Older texts sometimes called them ‘ionizing’ solvents.) These solvents have molecular dipoles and are often prefaced by the descriptor dipolar. Solvents of low polarity (hexane, tetrachloromethane) dissolve ionic compounds poorly. Even if, by careful molecular design, ionic compounds (e.g. tetra-n-butylammonium tetraphenylborate) are made soluble in such solvents, conductances are very low as the ions congregate into cation–anion ion pairs, see Section 1.7. Such solvents are termed non-electrolytic solvents. (Some authors use the term ‘associating’ solvents as ions aggregate.) Solvents with dielectric constants less than about 20 are usually non-electrolytic.

with the usual logarithmic definition pKauto = –log10Kauto. It is difficult to detect such autoprotolysis if pKauto > 35. A non-protogenic solvent (sometimes termed an aprotic solvent) is one which does not easily give hydrogen ions (like benzene).

Aprotic solvents - a misleading term

The term aprotic solvent has been used to describe solvents of both high and low polarity which do not readily give a proton to a base, B (eqn 1.23).

(1.23)

These are solvents like N, N-dimethylformamide (DMF), dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO), and acetonitrile (AN) (very polar solvents); tetrahydrofuran (THF) and diethyl ether (low polarity solvents); and benzene, cyclohexane, and alkanes (non-polar solvents). These solvents all contain protons, and with a superbase will give up a proton — although not readily.

Thus, benzene can accept a proton from a superacid (see Section 3.14) and can lose a proton when treated with BuLi+TMEDA (a ligand which complexes Li+ to give 1.5) (eqn 1.24).

C6H6+BuLi/TMEDA→C6H5−+HBu+Li(TMEDA)+ (1.24)

It is thus not strictly a non-protogenic solvent, but is so weakly protogenic and protophilic that its autoprotolysis is undetectable, with pKauto >> 35. Many non-electrolytic solvents are similar to benzene. Potentially they are protogenic solvents, but self-ionization is undetectable, and they normally behave as non-protogenic solvents.

It is better to avoid the use of the term aprotic for any solvent containing hydrogen atoms, and reserve it for solvents like SO2, BrF3, which contain no hydrogen atoms.

Although is sensible to reserve the term ‘aprotic’ for solvents containing no hydrogen atoms, chemists often use ‘aprotic’ to refer to weakly protogenic solvents like benzene, diethyl ether, and acetonitrile.

Hydrogen bonding

Hydrogen bonds are formed when any hydrogen-bond donor (HBD) group is near to a hydrogen-bond acceptor (HBA) atom or ion. Hydrogen bonding can take place inside a molecule (intramolecular hydrogen bonding), or between molecules (intermolecular hydrogen bonding). Hydrogen bonds are weaker than normal covalent bonds, with bond dissociation energies in the ranges 15 – 60 kJ mol–1 (compare with C–C in alkane of 347 kJ mol–1).

Solvents containing HBA atoms (N or O) are good Lewis base solvents. Solvents containing HBD groups will also be HBA Lewis base solvents since they will contain a N, O, F atom with lone pairs. Only R3NH+ ions can act as a hydrogen-bond donor without the corresponding acceptor function since the quaternary nitrogen has no free lone pairs. While F– ions are good hydrogen-bond acceptors, the F atom in a C–F bond is not, so there is no hydrogen bonding in fluorocarbon solvents.

Amphiprotic solvents are extensively involved in hydrogen bonding. Non-protogenic solvents are not. Hydrogen bonding can affect both solvent–solvent and solvent–solute interactions. Solvent–solvent intermolecular hydrogen bonding is common. Intermolecular hydrogen bonding raises the boiling points of many amphiprotic solvents over those expected from a consideration of the polarizability of the solvent molecule. Electrolytic amphiprotic solvents all have intermolecular hydrogen bonding in the solvent, and form hydrogen bonds with suitable solute molecules or solute ions.

Hydrogen bonding donor solutes, such as carbohydrates (e.g. sucrose) or NH4+, will hydrogen bond to the solvent’s hydrogen-bond acceptor sites, while HBA solutes, such as ethers and oxoanions, will form hydrogen bonds from the solvent’s HBD groups.

Non-electrolytic solvents (like tetrahydrofuran and aniline) can be involved in hydrogen bonding. Their electronegative O or N atoms principally act as HBA sites. These solvents will form hydrogen bonds with solutes such as NH4+ or organic ROH compounds.

Dissociation and ion pairs

When ionic compounds dissolve in water or other solvents of high dielectric constant (εr > 40), the force between the opposite charged ions is overcome, and the solvated ions can separate.Page 22, Chapter 1 General properties

Both solvated ions are responsible for the electrical conductance of the solution (Fig 1.14d). Ionic solutes dissolving in solvents with low dielectric constants (εr < 10 – 15) do not give free ions, but are present as contact ion pairs with no free solvent between the ions (Fig 1.14a). As the ion pair has no net charge, conductances are low. In solvents of intermediate dielectric constants (10 < εr < 40), ion pairs with solvent molecules between the ions can be formed. These ion pairs can be solvent-shared ion pairs (Fig 1.14b) or solvent-separated ion pairs (Fig 1.14c). Conductance data can be used to distinguish between free ions and ion pairs, while UV/Vis spectroscopy can, in some cases, distinguish between contact ion pairs where the cation–anion interaction is strong, and ion pairs with solvent between the ions.
