8.1 Introduction

Prostaglandins exert a wide range of pharmacological effects. Perhaps the most important is their effect on the female reproductive system.

Cloprostenol sodium, the active ingredient in ‘Estrumate’, is a synthetic analogue of the naturally occurring prostaglandin F2α. The product, which entered development in the early 1970s, was shown to be effective in the synchronisation of oestrus and this led to its application in planned breeding in cattle. Innumerable synthetic routes to the prostaglandins and their analogues have been published. One of the most versatile routes, which allows the preparation of side-chain variants, is that devised by Corey. The Corey route was modified and improved by ICI chemists so that cloprostenol sodium could be manufactured on a large scale and this chapter describes how some of the chemical problems were solved.

An inherent problem with the Corey route is the tendency of the alkylated cyclopentadiene 8.1 to isomerise, giving a more highly substituted olefin. Use of thallous cyclopentadienide in the alkylation step alleviates this problem but thallium reagents are too toxic for plant-scale manufacture. Oxidation of the alcohol to produce Corey aldehyde 8.6 is achieved with Collins reagent (chromium trioxide pyridine complex in dichloromethane) but this process was considered unsuitable for scale-up (hazardous reagent, effluent problem).

8.3 The acetoxyfulvene route

ICI research chemists devised a modified route in which the aldehyde function is introduced in the form of an enol ester. The oxidation step is thus replaced by a simple hydrolysis. Moreover use of 6-acetoxyfulvene 8.7 in the Diels-Alder reaction eliminates the problem of double-bond migration in the 5-membered ring. The aldehyde group is unmasked to allow the correct stereochemistry to be established at the bridgehead and then re-protected as an acetal until required for the olefination step.

The terms anti- and syn- indicate the relationship between the bridgehead substituent and the double bond.

8.3.1 Aldehyde isomerisation 8.9–8.10

The research process involved hydrolysis of the enol acetate 8.8 with 2N hydrochloric acid in dioxan (85°C, 4 days). Protonation from the least hindered side gives the anti-aldehyde 8.9 which transforms to the thermodynamically more stable syn-isomer 8.10 via acid-catalysed enolisation. However isomerisation is slow because the necessary change in the bridgehead bond angle (sp3→sp2) is disfavoured by the constraints of the ring system. Not surprisingly, the yield of syn-aldehyde 8.10 was low (about 50%) because of competing side reactions (e.g. nitrile hydrolysis). Moreover, dioxan (an animal carcinogen) is not an attractive solvent.

A better process was sought. Other acidic systems were examined without success but the problem was solved by the discovery that isomerisation of 8.9–8.10 is facilitated by formation of a Schiff base. A simple process was developed in which a solution of anti-aldehyde 8.9 in toluene is treated with p-chloroaniline (25°C, 8 hours). When isomerisation has stopped (≥95% of 8.10) p-chloroaniline is removed by extraction with 2N hydrochloric acid, thus providing a solution of the syn-aldehyde 8.10 (in 85% yield) ready for use in the next stage. p-Chloroaniline was convenient to use for the Schiff base because it was already available in bulk for the manufacture of another product. Aniline and p-toluidine (slightly stronger bases) were tried but gave inferior results, with tar formation.

Aldehyde 8.9 is a 2:1 mixture of endo/exo chloronitrile isomers.

Anti-aldehyde 8.9 is prepared separately in high yield by hydrolysis of enol acetate 8.8 under mild conditions (0.1N hydrochloric acid, 65°C, 18 hours).

By analogy with the Corey synthesis, chloronitrile 8.11 can be converted into ketone 8.13 via a cyanohydrin. The research process using potassium hydroxide in DMSO gave a variable amount of chloroamide 8.12 as an unwanted by-product. Experiments showed that, surprisingly, chloroamide 8.12 can be converted into ketone 8.13 in good yield by treatment with potassium hydroxide in ethanol at reflux (presumably via an α-lactam). Chloroamide 8.12 is a very stable crystalline compound and therefore offered the prospect of a simple purification step and a convenient point in the synthesis for storage of an intermediate. All the other intermediates in the first ten stages of the synthesis are oils (which cannot be purified easily) or unstable low-melting solids.

 

 

It was a simple matter to develop a process for making chloroamide 8.12 efficiently from chloronitrile 8.11. The transformation is achieved with alkaline hydrogen peroxide at 0–5°C.

8.3.3 Introduction of the side-chain at C-12

Ketone 8.13 is transformed after several steps into Corey aldehyde 8.6. Research chemists traditionally performed the side-chain olefination step under anhydrous conditions with 1,2-dimethoxyethane as solvent and n-butyl lithium as base. The latter is potentially hazardous and requires special precautions for scale-up. Alternative methods were examined and it was soon discovered that the two-phase system of toluene and aqueous sodium hydroxide gives an equally good yield of enone 8.17.

Acid-catalysed hydrolysis of the acetal group in 8.16 was troublesome on scale-up, often producing large amounts of the αβ–unsaturated aldehyde by elimination of p-phenylbenzoic acid. This problem led to the examination of a route variation, successfully operated on the plant scale, in which the C-11 protecting group is introduced later in the synthesis.

8.4 The hydroxyenone route

This is a useful method of generating tri-n-butyltin hydride in situ. The two reagents are cheap and readily available. However, use of organotin compounds on a very large scale poses an environmental problem.

Ketone 8.13 is subjected to Baeyer–Villiger oxidation (cold alkaline hydrogen peroxide) followed by iodolactonisation (potassium tri-iodide). This affords unstable iodohydrin 8.14 which cannot be stored. Iodohydrin 8.14 is deiodinated with bistributyltin oxide and polymethylhydrogen siloxane in toluene at 80°C and the required hydroxyacetal 8.18 is extracted into water. The aqueous solution is treated with hydrochloric acid to give hydroxyaldehyde 8.19 but this product is difficult to isolate because it is very water-soluble and relatively unstable (elimination!). Instead hydroxyaldehyde 8.19 is reacted in situ with phosphonate 8.20 and potassium carbonate, using THF as a co-solvent. After unconsumed phosphonate 8.20 has been removed by the addition of glyoxylic acid (which gives a water-soluble product), hydroxyenone 8.21 is extracted into toluene. Concentration of the toluene solution affords the crystalline product in about 35% overall yield from ketone 8.13.

8.4.1 Reduction of the C-15 carbonyl group

According to the literature, borohydride reagents are those most commonly used to reduce the C-15 carbonyl group, with complex borohydrides offering some degree of stereoselectivity in favour of the required 15α-hydroxy isomer at low temperature. The original Corey route used a p-phenylbenzoate ester group at C-11, the main advantages of this group being that it provided nicely crystalline intermediates and allowed easy separation of the C-15 isomers 8.22a and 8.22b by chromatography.

 

 

ICI chemists showed that the transformation at C-15 can be achieved by a Meerwein–Ponndorf–Verley reduction. Aluminium isopropoxide is cheap and readily available. However its reactivity is decreased by association in solution and reflux temperatures are generally required. DAI is suitable for large-scale manufacture since it does not suffer from the potential hazards associated with the use of borohydrides.

It was found that the cheaper benzoate ester can be used equally well in place of the C-11 p-phenylbenzoate. However in both cases the fractional crystallisation had to be done under carefully defined conditions to avoid the 15β-isomer crystallising out. Obviously it was undesirable to waste the 15β-isomer so the possibility of carrying out an inversion at C-15 (e.g. by the Mitsunobu process) was examined briefly but the results were not encouraging (messy reaction, chromatographic isolation, low yield). The option of oxidation at C-15 (Jones’ reagent) followed by re-reduction was inefficient and was also discounted.

8.4.2 Stereoselective reductions

The achievement of a stereoselective reduction at C-15 in the prostaglandin synthesis was a challenging goal. Corey showed that the nature of the C-11 substituent exerts stereochemical control over the reduction at C-15. In the most favourable case a p-phenylphenylcarbamoyl group at C-11 gave rise to a 92:8 ratio of 15α- and 15β-hydroxy isomers after reduction with thexyllimonylborohydride at –120°C. However, these conditions are impracticable for large scale operation because a temperature of –120°C is difficult to acheive and the borohydride reducing agent is not readily available. Also, cleavage of the carbamoyl protecting group requires forcing conditions (aqueous lithium hydroxide, 120°C, 3 days).

ICI chemists had found that reduction of hydroxyenone 8.21 with the aluminium reagent DAI in toluene/THF at 25°C gives a 2:1 ratio of isomers at C-15. The reaction is slow and this was attributed to relative insolubility of the substrate (or its aluminium complex); the scope for examining the process at low temperature was therefore restricted. It was argued that formation of a trimethylsilyl (TMS) ether at C-11 would greatly increase substrate solubility and allow the stereoselectivity of the reduction to be studied at sub-zero temperatures. Accordingly, hydroxyenone 8.21 was silylated with a mixture of trimethylsilyl chloride and hexamethyldisilazane and the TMS ether 8.24 was reduced with DAI at –78°C; the ratio of isomers at C-15 was 93:7.

8.24 is a non-isolated intermediate.

This discovery led to the development of a process capable of being operated on the kilogram scale. The TMS ether in alcohol 8.25 is sufficiently stable for the product to be isolated and recrystallised from ethyl acetate/hexane which removes the small amount of unwanted 15β-hydroxy isomer. The yield of pure alcohol 8.25 (65% from hydroxy-enone 8.21) is more than twice that obtained by the original process involving fractional crystallisation of a 1:1 mixture of isomers possessing a benzoate ester group at C-11.

Other C-11 silyl derivatives were found to give slightly better isomer ratios upon reduction with DAI but cheapness and simplicity of use favour the trimethylsilyl derivative in large scale manufacture.

Trimethylsilyl ethers are rapidly cleaved by treatment with dilute aqueous acid or alkali.

Deprotection of alcohol 8.25 with 0.1N hydrochloric acid gives diol 8.26 which is a highly crystalline, stable intermediate suitable for storage.

 

8.4.3 Introduction of the side-chain at C-8

A lactol ring is the stable tautomeric form of a γ-hydroxyaldehyde. The process requires an excess of ylid because of the acidic proton in lactol 8.27.

 

Diol 8.26 is converted in two steps to the bistetrahydropyranyl lactol 8.27 which is subjected to a Wittig reaction with the ylid from 4-carboxybutyltriphenylphosphonium bromide.

Research chemists used the dimethylsulphinyl anion, obtained by heating DMSO with sodium hydride, as the base to make the ylid from the phosphonium salt. Whilst this process gives high yields in the Wittig reaction, the preparation of sodium methanesulphinylmethylide is potentially hazardous and violent explosions have been reported in the literature. Furthermore, isolation of the product involved evaporation of DMSO under high vacuum.

A new process was developed using potassium tert-butoxide as the base in toluene at 90°C. This process gives an equally high yield (≥90%) of the bisTHP-protected prostaglandin 8.28 and a more convenient work-up in which the product is extracted into an aqueous solution and phosphorus by-products are removed in the organic layer. The Wittig reaction produces olefin with a cis/trans ratio of about 95:5, the unwanted trans-isomer being removed by recrystallisation of the final product. Air must be carefully excluded during the Wittig reaction to prevent oxidative dimerisation of the ylid which produces troublesome C10 diacid impurities.

8.5 Conclusions

  1. 1. A route has been developed which allows kilo-scale manufacture of a synthetic prostaglandin analogue. The product has five stereogenic centres and two olefinic double bonds.
  2. 2. The use of acetoxyfulvene as an aldehyde precursor avoids problems associated with the Corey route.
  3. 3. Control of olefin stereochemistry is achieved by the use of Wittig reactions.
  4. 4. A change of protecting group and reducing agent allows the carbonyl group to be reduced with a high degree of stereoselectivity under conditions which can be scaled up.
