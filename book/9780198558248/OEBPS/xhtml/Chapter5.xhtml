5 Thromboxane antagonists

Thromboxane A2 is a naturally occurring substance which, whenever it is released in the body, has the undesirable effects of bronchoconstriction, aggregation of the blood platelets and constriction of the blood vessels. Inhibition of the effects of thromboxane A2 could lead to a potential treatment for circulatory disorders or respiratory disorders such as asthma.

Note the trans relationship of the two side chains about the dioxan ring.

 

 

A series of thromboxane antagonists has been developed in ICI. They have similar structures to thromboxane A2 (TXA2) and can thus block the receptor site in the body.

 

Page 28, 5 Thromboxane antagonists 

The key stereochemical features of the molecules which are necessary for biological activity are:

The cis stereochemistry is opposite to the trans stereochemistry in TXA2

  1. (a) A cis arrangement of the phenol and the alkenoic acid side chain about the dioxan ring.
  2. (b) A cis alkenoic acid side chain.
  3. (c) A cis substituent in the 2 position of the dioxan ring.
  4. (d) Possibly a single enantiomer would be required.

The synthetic challenge for the compound to be viable as a pharmaceutical agent is to achieve the stereochemical control using methods which can be operated on a large scale.

5.1 Initial research route

Initially these compounds were synthesised in small quantities using a route which readily enabled the synthesis of many analogues for biological evaluation.

The compounds were all made as racemates however for clarity throughout this only one isomer is illustrated in the structural drawings. The actual compounds and intermediates made were a 50/50 mixture of enantiomers but retaining the indicated relative stereochemistry in the individual molecules.

 

 

Page 29, 5 Thromboxane antagonists 

Basically the route consisted of reaction of an aroyl chloride with the magnesium enolate of allylmalonic ester 5.1 followed by decarboxylation to yield the β-ketoester 5.2. Reduction with a variety of reducing agents gave rise to a mixture of diols 5.3, which could not be separated. Reaction of the mixed diols with dimethoxypropane gave a mixture of dioxans 5.4 which now were separable by chromatography to give the desired cis-dioxan 5.5.

ca 15% of the trans isomer was formed.

Ozonolysis cleaved the allyl group to give aldehyde 5.6 enabling the alkenoic acid 5.7 to be formed by a Wittig reaction.

Finally the free phenol ICI 180080 was liberated by cleavage of the methyl protecting group with sodium ethanethiolate. Both these stages required chromatography to isolate and purify the product.

5.2 Manufacturing route

The research synthesis was likely to cause severe difficulties on scale up to manufacture kilogram quantities. In particular:

  1. (a) Chromatography is only suitable for gram quantities. The very large volumes of solvents needed would be impracticable. Additionally the resolution of chromatography columns falls off markedly with increased diameter columns necessary for large-scale synthesis.
  2. (b) Ozonolysis is only a laboratory technique. A highly specialised (and expensive!) plant is necessary to operate ozonolysis safely on a large scale due to the potential explosive nature of the intermediate ozonides and the fire risk of oxygen and organic solvents.
  3. (c) Low temperatures (below –20°C) can be difficult to achieve on a large scale due to embrittlement of glass and metal reaction vessels.
  4. (d) The odour of ethanethiol would require special containment and use of sodium hydride in DMF can give rise to very exothermic ‘runaway’ reactions.

Small quantities of the compounds were made (to enable further biological evaluation) by scale up of the above reactions to 20 litre glassware. The large-scale chromatography necessary to separate the dioxan mixture 5.4 to obtain just a few grams of product was a severe limitation. A fundamentally new approach was likely to be required.

5.2.1 Stereochemical control

The two key stereochemical features which needed to be controlled are:

  1. (a) The cis alkenoic acid top side chain for which a Wittig reaction is a very attractive method. This requires an aldehyde precursor.
  2. (b) The cis arrangement of the phenyl group and the top side chain about the dioxan ring.

This led to the idea of locking the elements which are to form the dioxan ring in the correct stereochemical orientation by ring formation and generating the aldehyde function by the well established technique of reduction of a lactone to a lactol.

Retrosynthetic analysis showed that a lactone with the aromatic group and the carboxyl trans to each other would be required to give the required cis orientation about the dioxan ring. The carboxyl is to be reduced to aPage 30, 5 Thromboxane antagonists hydroxymethyl function to form the skeleton of the dioxan and the lactone is to be reduced to an aldehyde function for the Wittig reaction.

 

 

5.2.2 Initial investigations

The Perkin reaction is a very simple example of a base catalysed aldol reaction. The aldehyde adds to the methylene and lactonisation occurs between the formed hydroxyl and the carboxyl formed by ring opening of the succinic anhydride.

Lactones of the above structure 5.9 are trivially known as paraconic acids and have been known for many years, being synthesised from an aromatic aldehyde and succinic anhydride by a Perkin reaction.

 

 

Low temperatures are necessary to prevent over-reduction of the aldehyde function to the corresponding alcohol

Reduction of the carboxyl to the hydroxymethyl 5.10 was achieved with diborane in THF without reduction of the lactone. Selective reduction of the lactone group to the lactol 5.11 (latent aldehyde) was achieved using di-isobutyl aluminium hydride (DIBAL) at –78°C.

A Wittig reaction with the ylid formed from 4-carboxybutyl triphenyl-phosphonium bromide readily attached the heptenoic acid top side chain to give diol 5.12 and finally cyclisation with dimethoxypropane yielded ICI 159651 as single cis isomer identical to the material synthesised previously by the allyl malonate route.

Dimethoxypropane is the acetal of acetone and is considerably more reactive in the cyclisation reaction.

Although this had only been operated on a small laboratory scale it was considered that this route could be the basis of a viable manufacturing synthesis for this series of compounds, since the chromatographic separations had been avoided by making a single isomer of the paraconic acid 5.9. This method was then applied to the synthesis of the corresponding 2-hydroxyphenyl analogues which were now gaining biological importance.

Ring opening and reclosure of the lactone ring occurs to effect the isomerisation.

Scale up of the Perkin reaction was readily achieved using zinc chloride and triethylamine to form the enolate of succinic anhydride. This led to enhanced yields but the product was a mixture of cis and trans isomers. However it was shown that this mixture of acid isomers could be isomerised to the thermodynamically more stable trans isomer 5.14 by heating in dilute aqueous sulphuric acid solution. The required trans isomer has lower solubility than the cis isomer and thus isomerisation is achieved.

5.2.4 Lactol

 

 

The scale up of borane and DIBAL reduction steps was very difficult. Initially a special reaction plant was needed to handle toxic and highly flammable borane and provide the low temperatures necessary for the selective reduction with the pyrophoric DIBAL.

 

 

Further investigation showed that the two reduction stages could be achieved in a single stage by use of a stoichiometric amount of DIBAL at 0°C on the corresponding ethyl ester of 5.14 (5.14a R = C2H5).

This reaction illustrates that cyclic esters (lactones) are more stable and resistant to reduction than open chain esters. This simple process could thus be operated on a large scale in conventional plant once the hazards of pyrophoric DIBAL had been accommodated by provision of an inert atmosphere.

5.2.5 Wittig reaction

 

 

The Wittig stage operated extremely well under the inert nitrogen atmosphere necessary for all large scale processing, using a mixture of toluene and tetrahydrofuran solvent. Using the lactol 5.16 substrate had the additional advantage of producing less than 5% of the trans isomer and this could readily be removed by crystallisation of the product 5.17. Neutral triphenylphosphine oxide arising from the Wittig reaction could easily be separated from the acid product 5.17 by acid base extraction procedures.

5.2.6 Dioxan ring formation.

No stabilisation of the carbonium ion is possible next to a strongly electron-withdrawing trifluoromethyl group.

The 1,3 dioxan ring is unstable to acid (e.g. ICI 180080 has a half life of ca. 1 minute at pH 2 in aqueous solution at 25°C). To increase acid stability ICI 185282 and ICI 192605 were developed. The trifluoromethyl group is a particularly powerful acid stabilising group since it strongly repels a positive charge on the adjacent carbon atom (half life of ICI 185282 is ca 7 years at pH2).

All attempts to form the 1,3 dioxan ring from trifluoroacetaldehyde and diol acid 5.17 under acid conditions resulted in scrambling of the stereochemistry at the benzylic position. A different synthetic route was used.

Diazomethane is highly toxic and potentially explosive. Its use is thus confined to the laboratory only.

Diazomethane formed the methyl ester 5.18 with retention of stereochemistry. Acid catalysed esterification caused extensive scrambling at the benzylic position as well as formation of the methyl ether. Dimethyl sulphate in presence of potassium carbonate smoothly formed the ester 5.18 without loss of stereochemistry, and was used on the large scale. Selective activation of the primary hydroxyl was achieved with methanesulphonyl chloride to yield 5.19 (Y= CH3SO2), and reaction with trifluoroacetaldehyde hydrate gave the hemiacetal 5.20. Cyclisation was effected with potassium carbonate to give a mixture of epimers 5.21 at the 2 position (~85:15). Hydrolysis with NaOH gave the mixed free acids which could be separated by crystallisation to yield the desired cis epimer 5.22.

Telescoping together of two or more reaction stages is very effective for large-scale operation. It is essential however to ensure complete reaction before proceeding to the next stage.

Only the starting diol acid 5.17 and the dioxan 5.22 are crystalline so a telescoped process was used on the large scale whereby the oily product of one stage is not isolated but in solution (after appropriate purification by washing with e.g. acid or base) is taken through to the next stage. This worked very effectively over the four stages and enabled an overall 50% yield of dioxan 5.22 from diol 5.17 to be achieved.

The dioxan ring formation for ICI 192605 was very much easier. Reaction of the corresponding diol hexenoic acid 5.23 with 2-chlorobenzaldehyde gave the highly crystalline product 5.24 which formed a very effective workup from the phosphorus-containing residues of the Wittig reaction. A single epimer was formed, presumably due to the much greater steric hindrance of the aromatic group compared to the small trifluoromethyl group.

5.2.7 Demethylation

In the original research route to this series of compounds, protection of the phenolic hydroxyl group was achieved using a methyl group which was removed at the end of the synthesis by use of sodium ethanethiolate in DMF. For the new route the protecting group needs to be stable to severe conditions (strong acid for the isomerisation of paraconic acid, strong base and reducing conditions for the DIBAL and Wittig stages). Several alternative protecting groups were evaluated, but none proved suitable.

 

 

Use of ethanethiolate deprotection led to severe difficulties due to some attack at the 4 (benzylic) position of the dioxan ring and additionally in the case of ICI 192605 due to displacement of the chlorine to form the corresponding ethyl thioether. The gross mixture of products could only be separated by chromatography.

Many alternative agents were investigated for this demethylation (e.g. BBr3, Lil, TMSI) with little success. Either no reaction occurred or considerable decomposition accompanied the demethylation.

Lithium diphenylphosphide however proved to be remarkably selective and achieved very clean demethylation.

The diphenylphosphide anion is very bulky and this contributes to the very high selectivity.

This reagent is prepared by action of butyl lithium on diphenylphosphine.

 

 

This was fine for initial laboratory investigations, but the very ready oxidation of diphenylphosphine meant that the reaction was not very reproducible.

A far superior method started from the stable, readily available triphenyl phosphine. Reaction with two equivalents of lithium metal forms an equimolar mixture of phenyl lithium and lithium diphenylphosphide. The former can be selectively destroyed by use of one mole of t-butyl chloride, leaving a solution of lithium diphenylphosphide.

 

 

Addition of the methyl ether substrate to the freshly prepared reagent gives rapid, clean demethylation. This method proved to be extremely suitable for large-scale operation since the reagent could be prepared in one vessel and the demethylation carried out in the same vessel. Transfer of the odorous phosphine was thus avoided.

A complication arose with the demethylation of the methyl ether of ICI 192605, 5.24. The aromatic chlorine underwent reaction with diphenylphosphide anion yielding a mixture of products.

This was neatly circumvented by formation of an intermediate acetonide 5.25, which could be demethylated (without complication from the chlorine) to give the phenol 5.26.

Both acetonides 5.25 and 5.26 are highly crystalline and so offer the possibility of purification.

Finally, exchange with 2-chlorobenzaldehyde gave ICI 192605 ready for final purification.

 

 

5.2.8 Final purification.

Compounds used as pharmaceutical agents have to be of extremely high chemical purity and obviously free from potentially toxic impurities. This is in general accomplished by crystallisation under ultra-clean plant conditions.

This is an example of use of a highly crystalline salt to effect large scale purification in place of chromatography.

ICI 185282 required special treatment. The demethylation is at the final stage and inevitably traces of phosphorus-containing residues were formed by oxidation (especially diphenylphosphinic acid Ph2POOH, which being an acid was not easily removed from the acid ICI 185282 by extraction techniques). Purification was effected by formation (and recrystallisation if necessary) of an intermediate highly crystalline piperidine salt, followed by regeneration of ICI 185282 free acid.

This technique was successfully operated on the production scale to give pure ICI 185282 containing less than 20 parts per million phosphorus.

For ICI 192605 a similar degree of purity could be achieved by crystallisation of the intermediate acetonides 5.25 and 5.26 before the final exchange with 2-chlorobenzaldehyde.

5.3 Conclusions

This study illustrates many of the fundamental issues concerning the synthesis of fine chemicals in a high degree of purity on a large scale. The initial research route was not suitable because of the chromatographic separations involved and difficult reagents necessary for some of the transformations involved. The alternative route proved to be very challenging but resulted in a very high purity product by getting the stereochemistry correct at the start of the synthesis.

The particular difficulties which the route elegantly solved are :

  1. 1. Cis dioxan (using trans paraconic acid)
  2. 2. Cis alkenoic acid (using the Wittig reaction)
  3. 3. Cis trifluoromethyl (epimer separation by crystallisation)
  4. 4. Clean demethylation (lithium diphenylphosphide)
  5. 5. Final purification (piperidine salt)
  6. 6. Enantiomeric synthesis (resolution of paraconic acid).

This is covered in detail in the next chapter.

A product containing less than 0.1% of stereoisomers was readily manufactured on a production scale.
