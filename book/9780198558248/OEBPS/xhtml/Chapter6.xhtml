6 Homochiral thromboxane antagonists

The earlier series of thromboxane antagonists had been developed as racemates (still retaining the cis relative stereochemistry about the dioxan ring) mainly due to the fact that whilst ICI 192605 racemate is a highly crystalline solid, the active single (–) enantiomer is a non-crystalline oil. This would give rise to obvious purification and formulation difficulties.

 

6.1 Racemic and single enantiomer pharmaceuticals

Pharmaceutical compounds are designed to interact beneficially with living biological systems. Biological systems are homochiral, being made up from homochiral amino acids and sugars. It therefore follows that different enantiomers are likely to have different biological effects. Broadly three types of activity are observed.

  1. (a) All the desired activity resides in one enantiomer; the opposite enantiomer is inactive. This is a commonly observed effect. It follows therefore that separation of the racemate into a single enantiomer should lead to a more potent pharmaceutical agent, which in principle is also less toxic.
  2. (b) Enantiomers differ in potency. This also is a fairly commonly observed effect. Most of the activity resides in one enantiomer, and the opposite enantiomer exhibits weaker activity. This is explained by some binding, but weaker binding, occurring between the active biological site and the less active enantiomer.
  3. (c) Both enantiomers are equipotent. This is rarely observed and not expected since activity depends on the pharmaceutical agent interacting with a homochiral biological site. It is usually indicative of in vivo racemisation.

Thus there is a strong case in terms of potency and toxicity to develop pharmaceutical agents as single enantiomers.

6.2 Methods for synthesis of homochiral pharmaceutical compounds.

6.2.1 Separation at end of synthesis

This is often performed in the research laboratory to obtain small samples of both enantiomers to enable the relative biological activity to be assessed. It is normally achieved by classical resolution techniques. For example by formation of a diastereoisomeric salt with a homochiral base, recrystallis-ation of the salt to achieve high enantiomeric purity and then regeneration of the free homochiral acid.

The racemate contains only 50% of the required enantiomer and losses occur during the separation.

From a manufacturing viewpoint, this is a very wasteful technique at the end of a multi-stage synthesis. Often it is not possible to racemise and re-use the unwanted enantiomer particularly if the molecule contains more than one stereogenic centre. Thus to obtain 1 kg of pure enantiomer requires synthesis of approximately 3 kg racemate.

6.2.2 Resolution at the start of the synthesis.

Separation into enantiomers is achieved at the start or early in the synthesis and the homochiral molecule obtained is then elaborated into the final single enantiomer product. This is more efficient than separation at the end of a synthesis.

Inversion of the sign of optical rotation occurs during the reduction stage to lactol. The absolute stereochemistry is not affected.

In the previous it was shown that 2-methoxyphenylparaconic acid could be resolved into enantiomers by formation of a salt with (+) ephedrine. Re-crystallisation of the salt, followed by regeneration of the free acid gave enantiomerically pure (+) -trans-2-methoxyphenylparaconic acid, which was subsequently taken through the synthesis to give the (–) isomer of ICI 192605 as a non-crystalline oil.

6.2.3 Incorporation of a homochiral fragment

This is an effective method if a suitable homochiral molecule is commercially available or is readily available from natural sources. Synthetic peptides are synthesised from naturally occurring (S)-amino acid fragments.

This is exemplified in full in 9.

ICI D7114 was developed within ICI as a potential anti-obesity agent. The synthesis used (R)-glycidyl butyrate to introduce the homochirality into the molecule.

6.2.4 Use of a homochiral auxiliary

The principle of the method is to introduce a homochiral auxiliary fragment into the substrate prior to the chemical reaction which forms a new stereocentre. The homochiral auxiliary is then removed (and possibly reused). The residual molecule containing the new stereogenic centre is thenPage 40, 6 Homochiral thromboxane antagonists elaborated into the desired homochiral target molecule. This method is illustrated in more detail later in this chapter.

6.2.5 Use of a homochiral catalyst

This is similar to the use of a homochiral auxiliary except that the homochiral active site is regenerated in situ to enable further conversion to take place, e.g. catalytic Sharpless epoxidations, Noyori catalytic reduction of keto-esters etc.

6.3 Dual action thromboxane antagonist/synthetase inhibitors

 

 

ICI D1542 is of interest because not only does it inhibit the action of thromboxane A2 by blocking the active site, but also it inhibits the synthesis of TXA2. It is a crystalline single (–)-enantiomer. Synthesis on the multi-kilogram scale of stereochemically pure material is required.

6.3.1 Initial research route

Preliminary quantities to enable biological evaluation were made by a route involving the Evans asymmetric aldol reaction as the key step generating the required absolute and relative stereochemistry.

The key steps involved are :

  1. 1. Attachment of a homochiral oxazolidinone 6.1 to give allyl compound 6.2.
  2. 2. Chelation step with dibutylboron triflate (Bu2BOTf).

Difficulties were encountered on attempted scale up of this route namely:

  1. (a) Extensive chromatography to achieve purification of non-crystalline intermediates.

6.3.2 Resolution of the pyridylparaconic acid

Synthesis of the (±) pyridylparaconic acid 6.10 and 6.11 was achieved using a modified Perkin reaction from 3-pyridinecarboxaldehyde and succinic anhydride. Magnesium chloride and triethylamine is used to generate the enolate from succinic anhydride. Two equivalents of magnesium chloride are necessary; one to complex with the pyridine nitrogen and the second to complex with succinic anhydride. Only the required trans isomer crystallises from the reaction.

The planned approach was to resolve the pyridylparaconic acid into separate enantiomers. Both the acidic carboxyl group and the basic pyridine nitrogen offer the possibility of salt formation with a homochiral acid or base. The separated required enantiomer would then be taken through the route successfully used to make the previous compounds such as ICI 192605.

 

 

However it proved impossible to achieve separation of the pyridylcarboxylic acid into its enantiomers, using a wide range of homochiral acids or bases. Salt formation did not occur, probably due to the zwitterionic nature of 6.10 and 6.11.

This approach failed at the first stage.

 

6.3.3 Octenedioic acid route

The research route used an Evans homochiral aldol reaction to achieve the correct relative stereochemistry about the dioxan ring and correct absolute stereochemistry. It was not possible to use the route on a large scale because of the ozonolysis required to generate the aldehyde for the Wittig reactionPage 43, 6 Homochiral thromboxane antagonists and also because of the chromatography to separate the 15% trans isomer from the required cis alkene.

Use of cis oetenedioic acid 6.13 solved both these difficulties, by introducing the cis alkenoic acid top side chain at the start of the synthesis.

cis-Cyclo-octadiene 6.12 contains the required cis stereochemistry and may be oxidised in low yield (ca 20%) to cis oetenedioic acid 6.13 using chromium trioxide.

A low yield at the start of a synthesis is tolerable if the materials are cheap and readily available. A low yield at the end of the synthesis is unacceptable due to the high value of the late stage products.

This key intermediate forms the backbone of the required final product and could then be elaborated using an Evans asymmetric aldol reaction similar to the original research route.

6.4.2 Control of the stereochemistry by chelation

Evans and others showed that a synthetically useful aldol reaction could be achieved by incorporation of a second carbonyl into the substrate followed by chelation with boron. This chelation ring thus controls the relative stereochemistry of the addition of the incoming nucleophile. Additionally if the added moiety is homochiral, then the absolute stereochemistry of the addition may also be controlled and an asymmetric aldol addition achieved.

6.5 Manufacturing route to D1542

Combination of cis-octenedioic acid 6.13 to introduce the cis-hexenoic acid top side chain with 3-pyridinecarboxaldehyde in an asymmetric aldol addition sets up the required stereochemical skeleton of the molecule in enantiomerically pure form.

A statistical mixture of diester 6.20, diacid 6.13, and the desired monoester 6.21, which are difficult to separate, is produced. Even after recycling of 6.13 and 6.20 an overall yield of only 40% is achieved.

6.5.1 Synthesis of homochiral substrate.

The dimethyl ester 6.20 is readily formed by reaction of cis-octenedioic acid with methanol and sulphuric acid. Partial hydrolysis with one equivalent of sodium hydroxide gives the monomethyl ester 6.21.

A superior method of formation of monoester 6.21 was developed, involving reaction of diacid 6.13 with one mole of methanol in a two-phase system (water-dichloromethane) with sulphuric acid catalysis. The diacid is soluble in the aqueous phase but as soon as reaction to form the monoester 6.21 occurs it enters the dichloromethane phase, effectively preventing formation of the diester 6.20. A yield in excess of 90% is achieved in a single step reaction.

A thin film evaporator proved very effective for purification on the large scale.

The acid chloride 6.22 is formed by reaction with thionyl chloride and initially was purified by vacuum distillation.

The anion of (S)-4-isopropyloxazolidinone 6.1 was formed by reaction with butyl lithium at 5°C and reaction with acid chloride 6.22 produced the substrate 6.23. This compound is an oil and not easily purified. A toluene solution was used directly in the next stage.

6.5.2 Asymmetric aldol reaction

In the research laboratory, dibutylboron triflate is usually used to form the chelate. Supplies of this reagent are limited and it was not possible to obtain the quantities needed for the development of D1542 from commercial suppliers.

A synthesis in situ was used starting from commercially available triethylborane

Triethylborane Et3B is manufactured on a large scale for use as an initiator in the plastics industry.

Reaction of triethylborane with one equivalent of trifluoromethane-sulphonic acid (‘triflic acid’) formed diethylboron triflate. This was not isolated, but immediately reacted with the homochiral substrate 6.23 to form the chelate. Generation of the anion using the highly hindered base di-isopropyl ethylamine, followed by an aldol addition with 3-pyridine-carboxaldehyde gave a good (70%) yield of a highly crystalline product 6.24.

This reaction proved to be remarkably stereoselective, achieving the three desired stereochemical transformations in the one reaction.

  1. (a) Addition to the methylene adjacent to the oxazolidinone and without activation of either the methylenes adjacent to the carbomethoxy group or adjacent to the olefin.
  2. (b) The addition gives the desired relative stereochemistry of the hydroxyl introduced.
  3. (c) The absolute stereochemistry is correct due to the directing influence of the homochiral oxazolidinone.

The crystalline product also afforded considerable purification. The synthesis had only three solid products throughout the whole twelve stages.

A top layer of toluene was added to the triflic acid to ensure anhydrous conditions.

In order to achieve the high yields that the reaction is capable of, it is essential to ensure completely anhydrous conditions and a complete absence of oxygen. This was achieved on a plant scale by drying the vessels by distillation of toluene and conducting the reaction under a nitrogen purge. Both the reaction to form diethylboron triflate and the subsequent addition of 3-pyridinecarboxaldehyde were performed in the same reaction vessel to avoid the possibility of contamination with oxygen or water during transfer between vessels. A special dispensing system was devised to add the highly hygroscopic triflic acid to the reactor without ingress of water.

6.5.3 Removal of the homochiral auxiliary

This was achieved by reduction using sodium borohydride. This reaction achieved two synthetically useful steps. Not only was the oxazolidinone cleaved from the molecule, but the carbonyl was reduced to a methylene to give 6.9 which formed the skeleton of the dioxan ring.

 

 

The recovered oxazolidinone could then be re-used for subsequent manufacture.

Separation of the basic reduced product 6.9 from the neutral oxazolidinone 6.1 was easily achieved by extraction with dilute hydrochloric acid.

 

6.5.4 Completion of the synthesis

The direct reaction of diol 6.9 and aldehyde 6.26 is very slow and requires high temperatures for completion. This causes some decomposition which renders purification extremely difficult. The acetonide 6.8 method lowers the energy for the cyclisation. The thermodynamically most stable product D1542 is completely formed.

The diol 6.9 could then be elaborated into D1542 by fairly standard procedures. Reaction with dimethoxypropane gave acetonide 6.8 which was not isolated, but reacted directly with aldehyde 6.26 under acid conditions to yield D1542 methyl ester which on hydrolysis with NaOH gave D1542 crude ready for final purification.

6.6 Conclusions

This study illustrates a very efficient method for the synthesis of an enantiomerically pure compound on a large scale. The key stereochemical bond-forming aldol step results in the following benefits:

  1. 1. Control of the regiochemistry of the addition to the octenedioic acid moiety.
  2. 2. Control of the relative stereochemistry of the aldol to yield the precursor for a cis diol.
  3. 3. Control of the absolute stereochemistry due to the homochiral auxiliary.
  4. 4. Purification due to the crystalline product.
  5. 5. The homochiral auxiliary is recovered as part of the synthesis and can be re-used. This is very desirable on both economic and environmental grounds.
