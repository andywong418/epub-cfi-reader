4 Fun with cephalosporins

4.1 General comments

Starting with a background of no experience in the chemical development of ß-lactam antibiotics, progress with the first ICI product to be selected as a candidate drug was frustratingly slow. Process development chemists who were used to working with stable aromatic heterocycles soon realised that cephalosporins have to be treated differently! Main learning points were:

Ring strain inhibits normal amide resonance and renders the molecule susceptible to attack at the carbonyl group:

1. Products containing the β-lactam ring are relatively unstable. Reactions are preferably done in organic solvents and heat is best avoided. If aqueous systems are used, processes are confined to a narrow pH range since extremes of pH cause rapid decomposition.

2. A specific method for measuring product purity or strength must be devised as quickly as possible. Strength is defined as the proportion of desired component in a sample and is usually expressed as %w/w. Products which appear to be a single component by HPLC analysis (UV detector) can be very impure. By-products, and particularly polymers produced by decomposition of the β-lactam ring, are often UV-inactive. TLC is unhelpful since it is not quantitative and decomposition often occurs on the stationary phase. Naively, samples of the first development product were submitted for elemental analysis and the results were in good agreement with theoretical values for the structure; subsequently the strengths were determined to be in the range 39-65%! Progress was made possible by the discovery that strengths can be measured accurately (±1%) by high resolution NMR spectroscopy, comparing H-6 or H-7 signals against an internal standard (e.g. maleic acid).

3. At an early stage effort should be applied to the search for a crystalline form of the final product. β-Lactam products are often amorphous and cannot be purified except by chromatography. Even this technique rarely achieves the high purity (≥98%) needed for a drug. The problem of purification is usually solved by a crystallisation process but the discovery of a crystalline form requires hard work and luck. The time taken to achieve success can vary from weeks to years.

4.2 ICI 156488

4.2.1 Introduction

ICI 156488 is an orally active cephalosporin which was considered to have potential use in the treatment of urinary tract infections. Kilogram quantities were required to support the development programme. The research route to ICI 156488 started from 7-aminocephalosporanic acid (7-ACA) attachingPage 16, 4 Fun with cephalosporins first a mercaptotriazole group at C-3′ (a sub-structure common to other commercial cephalosporins) followed by the novel step of introducing an imidazole group at the C-7 nitrogen.

 

 

4.2.2 Comments on the research route

7-ACA is a commodity chemical available from several companies (price about £200/kg). It is made by a sequence of fermentation and chemical steps. ICI had neither experience of nor suitable production facilities for the fermentation process so it was accepted at the outset that the scale-up route for ICI 156488 should start from purchased 7-ACA.

The impure product could be partially purified by filtration through alumina.

Patent literature describes the reaction between 7-ACA and 5-mercaptotriazole 4.2 (and many other mercaptoheterocycles) using a simple aqueous process (phosphate buffer at pH6, 50–60°C, 4 hours). The product 4.1 (7-TACA) is precipitated in about 60% weight yield by adjusting the pH of the solution. Operationally such a process appeared to be ideal for scale-up! Unfortunately it soon became clear that the product from this process is very impure (strength about 60%) due to the presence of polymeric impurities caused by destruction of the β-lactam ring.

Research chemists had prepared small samples of ICI 156488 by treating 7-TACA 4.1 with the triphenylmethyl-protected derivative 4.4 of 2-fluoroimidazole but the yield was low (about 30%). In addition, 2-fluoroimidazole 4.3 is very difficult to make.

4.2.3 2-Fluoroimidazole 4.3

2-Fluoroimidazole 4.3 is made by photochemical decomposition of the diazonium fluoroborate (Balz-Schiemann reaction) obtained by diazotisation of 2-aminoimidazole in 50% aqueous fluoroboric acid. The product tends to trimerise and this is prevented by formation of the triphenylmethyl derivative 4.4.

Solutions containing fluoride salts are generally unsuitable for use in glass vessels.

The yield of 2-fluoroimidazole 4.3 was 30-40% at best, falling to 10% on scale-up to 100g (probably because of extended reaction times) and output was low (2% w/v concentration). Use of the process to make multi-kilo quantities of 2-fluoroimidazole 4.3 was out of the question because large scale photochemical reactors are rare and in any case aqueous fluoroboric acid gradually dissolves glass!

Intense process development activity failed to find either a better way of making 2-fluoroimidazole 4.3 or an alternative imidazole with similar reactivity at the 2-position.

4.2.4 The cyanamide route

A review of other ways of making 2-aminoimidazoles led to an investigation into the use of 2,2-diethoxyethylcyanamide 4.5 which is readily prepared by the action of cyanogen chloride or bromide on the corresponding amine.

 

 

Treatment of 7-TACA 4.1 with an excess of cyanamide 4.5 in 1,2-dimethoxyethane (DME) containing hydrochloric acid gave a low yield (about 20%) of ICI 156488. NMR studies showed the product to be contaminated with a substantial amount of the isomeric imidazole 4.6 resulting from an alternative mode of ring closure.

The freeze-drying technique is used to isolate a sensitive product from an aqueous solution when distillation would cause decomposition.

The cyanamide process was chosen to make 150 g of ICI 156488 for initial development studies. Because of the difficulty of adhering to a precise reaction time (heating up and cooling down takes longer on the large scale) the yield deteriorated to <10% on scale-up and the purity of the crude product was only 25–30%. Purification (achieved by a sequence of chromatographic steps using reversed-phase silica, ion-exchange and polystyrene based resins) required a heroic effort, affording over 30 small batches of freeze-dried product which were blended. After allowing for water content, the purity was 93%.

Cyclisation onto the relatively hindered nitrogen at C-7 occurs because protonation decreases the electron density on the remaining guanidine nitrogen atoms.

 

 

Cyanamide 4.5 is unstable to the reaction conditions used. By-products identified were imidazolone 4.7 and 2-ethoxyimidazole 4.8.

 

 

A kinetic analysis of the numerous competing reaction pathways offered no hope of improved yield or regioselectivity in the guanidine cyclisation. Therefore the process was abandoned and the search for a manufacturing route continued.

 

4.2.5 The carbodiimide route

Zwitterionic compounds like 7-ACA are often insoluble in organic solvents.

A simple variation of the cyanamide approach led to an efficient production process. Treatment of cyanamide 4.5 with trimethylchlorosilane affords the silyl-protected carbodiimide 4.9, a stable distillable liquid. Acid-catalysed reaction between carbodiimide 4.9 and 7-ACA (solubilised by formation of the trimethylsilyl ester) affords guanidine 4.10 which can be cyclised exclusively to the required imidazole 4.11 (7-IACA) without formation of isomer 4.12.

Initially trifluoromethanesulphonic acid (non-nucleophilic counter-ion) was used to catalyse guanidine-formation but sulphuric acid (much cheaperPage 19, 4 Fun with cephalosporins and easier to handle) was found to be equally effective. A problem of gum deposition during the cyclisation step was avoided by using boron trifluoride acetic acid complex in place of concentrated hydrochloric acid. A simple work-up was devised in which the reaction mixture is diluted with water and the product adsorbed on a resin column. Elution with aqueous acetonitrile and concentration of the appropriate fraction gives pure crystalline 7-IACA 4.11 in 50% yield.

Silicon is electropositive and the Si-N bond is strongly polarised. This favours cyclisation to give the required regioisomer.

 

 

The ease of crystallising 7-IACA 4.11 compared with 7-TACA 4.1 was a decisive factor in changing the order of introducing the C-7 and C-3′ substituents.

4.2.6 Substitution at C-3′

Sulpholane (tetramethylene sulphone) is an inert, non-toxic, polar solvent.

From discussions with representatives of other β-lactam producing companies it became clear that the most effective way of introducing a mercaptoheterocycle at C-3′ is with boron trifluoride catalyst under anhydrous conditions so this approach was investigated for ICI 156488.

In the preferred process, 7-IACA 4.11 is treated with the sodium salt of 5-mercaptotriazole 4.2 in the presence of boron trifluoride sulpholane complex (this is suitable for large-scale production whereas boron trifluoride etheratePage 20, 4 Fun with cephalosporins is unacceptable because of the extreme flammability of diethyl ether). After quenching the reaction with water ICI 156488 is isolated in 80% yield by adsorption on a resin column and elution with aqueous acetonitrile. Conversion to the crystalline triethylamine salt gives product of >99% purity.

 

 

A major concern for plant-scale manufacture was safe-handling of 5-mercaptotriazole 4.2. Chemical hazard studies showed that the free thiol is explosive whereas the sodium salt is not. Hence the latter was used in all development work.

 

4.3 IC1194008

4.3.1 Introduction

ICI 194008 is a broad-spectrum antibiotic which was selected for development. The compound is an analogue of the commercial product ceftazidime but differing in the nature of the C-3′ substituent.

 

 

Introduction of the novel C-3′ substituent was a crucial step and in the research synthesis was achieved by a multi-stage sequence involving reductive alkylation of a primary amine. This synthetic strategy evolved from the research requirement for a versatile intermediate capable of making a wide variety of C-3′ analogues. Indeed some of the potential development candidates lacking an Af-alkyl substituent were obtained by direct reaction of primary amine 4.16 with an activated heterocycle.

4.3.2 Problems with the RD route

The RD route suffered from major drawbacks for scale-up:

1. Azide 4.14 was prepared by treating 7-ACA with sodium azide in an aqueous system (pH 6.8, 60°C, 3.5 hours), giving a low yield of low strength product (30% and 60% respectively). The potential explosion hazard of azides discouraged further development of this process.

Reverse osmosis involves concentration of a solution by allowing water to pass through a membrane under the influence of pressure.

2. Reduction of azide 4.15 acylated at the C-7 nitrogen was performed with Raney nickel in a mixture of trifluoroacetic acid and methanol (a dissolving metal process). Scale-up was ruled out because of the chemical hazard (vigorous exotherm and hydrogen evolution) and effluent problem. Product was isolated by adsorption onto a resin column, generating a large volume of aqueous waste containing nickel. Useful fractions were concentrated by reverse osmosis and freeze dried to givePage 22, 4 Fun with cephalosporins amine 4.16 of 70% purity in 35% yield. Many attempts to obtain a crystalline form of amine 4.16 failed and there was no way of improving its quality. A significant by-product from the process was lactam 4.19.

 

 

3. Amine 4.16 was reductively alkylated with acetaidehyde in the presence of sodium cyanoborohydride. Inevitably further alkylation occurred so the product contained about 10% of the diethyl-derivative as well as unreacted starting material. Only partial purification could be achieved even with chromatography.

4.3.3 The benzaldehyde imine process

Many ways of introducing an ethylamino group at C-3′ were tried, including use of different cephalosporin substrates and a wide range of nucleophiles. Nearly all attempts were completely unsuccessful. Both ethylamine and the ethyl am inoheterocycle 4.20 failed to give the required product even with reactive substrates such as the C-3′ iodide. Ethylamine caused cleavage of the β-lactam ring.

 

 

Eventually the benzaldehyde imine 4.21 was found to be a suitably protected form of ethylamine, reacting with iodomethyl cephalosporin 4.22 to give a low yield of amine 4.23 after hydrolysis of the intermediate quaternary salt.

 

 

Page 23, 4 Fun with cephalosporinsThe trimethylsilyl-protected form of iodide 4.22 can be made in high yield by treatment of 7-ACA with trimethylsilyl iodide. The latter is extremely moisture-sensitive and might not be considered an ideal reagent for use on the plant scale. However trimethylsilyl iodide is easily made in situ from hexamethyldisilane and iodine (reflux in dichloromethane), thus allowing an efficient process for iodide 4.22 to be developed. A telescoped process to amine 4.23 was considered appropriate because of the sensitive nature of the intermediates (particularly iodide 4.22) but this made it more difficult to interpret the experimental results. The relationship between factors controlling the chemistry is complex and a large number of experiments were performed before the optimised process was available. Key points are:

Epichlorohydrin can be used when a neutral reagent is required to remove a halogen acid.

  1. 1. Imine 4.21 does not displace iodide while the carboxylic acid is protected as a silyl ester. Therefore epichlorohydrin is added to scavenge hydrogen iodide and the alcohol thus formed desilylates ester 4.24.
  2. 2. Careful adherence to optimised reaction conditions (times, temperatures, reagent stoichiometry) is important to obtain the best yield of amine 4.23.
  3. 3. The use of chloroform as reaction solvent gives a significant yield advantage over dichloromethane (50% vs 40% from 7-ACA to amine 4.23). This is probably a solubility effect. However, chloroform is particularly harmful to anaerobic waste treatment processes and its use on the plant scale requires great care to avoid accidental spillage or contamination of aqueous effluent.
  4. 4. A simple non-chromatographic method of isolating the product is essential. The breakthrough came with the discovery of a crystalline p-toluenesulphonate salt and the definition of reproducible crystallisation conditions appropriate to the chemical process.

4.3.4 Addition of side chain substituents

The reactivity of the C-7 amino group is decreased by steric hindrance and the electron-withdrawing effect of the β-carbonyl group.

4.3.4.1 Substitution at C-3′nitrogen

Of the two amino groups in amine 4.23, that at C-3′ is the most reactive. Therefore the strategy was to introduce the bicyclic heterocycle first and an efficient process was developed quickly to give aminopyrimidine 4.24 in 80% yield. On the plant scale contract manufacturing facilities were needed to contain the liberated methanethiol from reagent 4.18.

 

 

4.3.4.2 Acylation at C-7 nitrogen

In this step, removal of the tert-butyl ester protecting group was troublesome.

Thioester 4.25 is a useful reagent for acylation of the cephalosporin C-7 amino group. Advantages over the more usual acid chloride coupling method are: avoidance of handling and using moisture sensitive chlorinating reagents and elimination of the need for protecting the 2-aminothiazole group (e.g. as a triphenylmethyl derivative). Thioester 4.25 is commercially available in bulk.

Tert-butyl esters are resistant to alkaline hydrolysis but alkyl-oxygen cleavage occurs under acid catalysis with liberation of isobutylene.

Aminopyrimidine 4.24 coupled smoothly with thioester 4.25 but removal of the tert-butyl ester group from the product could not be achieved. There is much precedence in cephalosporin chemistry for cleavage of the tert-butyl protecting group with strong acids (e.g. trifluoroacetic acid or conc. hydrochloric acid/formic acid mixture) but in the case of ICI 194008 these reagents caused rapid destruction of the cephalosporin (>90% loss in one hour at 25°C). The acid-lability of ICI 194008 is obviously associated with the bicyclic substituent at C-3′ but the mechanism of the decomposition was not elucidated.

 

 

Page 25, 4 Fun with cephalosporinsAn alternative approach was needed. Removal of the tert-butyl group prior to the coupling step was an attractive option but there was no literature precedent for this. Thioester 4.25 did not survive treatment with strong acid but trimethylsilyl iodide proved to be effective and a simple process was developed, with the help of NMR spectroscopy to follow the deprotection (changes in the tert-butyl and trimethylsilyl signals). The new process was operated successfully on the plant scale, affording ICI 194008 in 80% yield.

 

 

Two problems were encountered and solved during initial scale-up of the process:

1. Extended reaction times with trimethylsilyl iodide led to a new impurity in the cephalosporin which was identified as the anti-oxime isomer. A literature search revealed precedent for acid-catalysed syn- to anti-oxime conversion. Hence careful optimisation of the reaction time was required.

 

 

Migration of the double bond gives a more flexible dihydrothiazine ring system, thereby decreasing ring strain. Compounds with the double bond in the 2,3-position exhibit no antibacterial activity.

2. A substantial impurity identified as the 2,3-double-bond isomer was observed in the first large-scale preparation. Double-bond isomerisation in cephalosporin esters under basic conditions is well known. Silylated aminothiazole intermediate 4.26 is itself a potential silylating agent. Evidently on scale-up cephalosporin intermediate 4.24 had been dried more thoroughly than usual, allowing silylation of the C-4 carboxylic acid and thusPage 26, 4 Fun with cephalosporins promoting double-bond isomerisation in the presence of excess triethylamine. By chance, in earlier laboratory preparations cephalosporin intermediate 4.24 contained sufficient water to inhibit silyl transfer. The solution was simple—one equivalent of water was added to the thioester 4.25/trimethylsilyl iodide reaction mixture immediately prior to the coupling reaction. The problem of isomerisation did not recur.

 

 

4.3.5 Purification

Three crystalline froms of ICI 194008 were discovered: a dihydrochloride salt, a zwitterion (solvate with N,N-dimethylacetamide) and a sodium salt. The last two were particularly effective, giving material of >98% purity. Because of its solvent of crystallisation the zwitterion was not considered to be pharmaceutically acceptable. The high solubility of the sodium salt in water made it the ideal choice for an injectable product.

4.4 Conclusions

  1. 1. β-Lactams are relatively unstable compounds and high-yielding processes depend on the use of mild reaction conditions.
  2. 2. A substituted carbodiimide has been found to be a highly effective synthon for the construction of an imidazole ring.
  3. 3. It is necessary to protect ethylamine as an imine in order to achieve successful displacement of an iodo-substituent from the 3′-position of the cephalosporin.
  4. 4. Processes using trimethylsilyl iodide have been developed and operated successfully on the large scale.
