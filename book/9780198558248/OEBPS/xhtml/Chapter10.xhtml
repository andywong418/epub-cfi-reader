10 Reagents for large-scale operation

The previous chapters have covered examples of many different types of reaction and synthetic reagents which have successfully been operated on a large scale. Often the choice of a particular reagent is governed by many factors in addition to the obvious ones of yield and selectivity (purity of the product). Cost, availability, ease of disposal of residues, safety, reaction conditions, and solvent compatibility all need to be considered before use of the reagent on a large scale.

10.1 Solvent

Solvent recovery is increasingly desirable on environmental as well as cost grounds.

Ideally a single solvent should be used for a chemical reaction This facilitates the charging of the solvent to the reactor and simplifies the operations if recovery and re-use are to be considered. The desirable properties of a solvent used for large scale are :

        Solubility of reactants

        Chemical inertness to the reagents being used

        Ease of isolation of the product

        Cheap

        Readily available

        Readily recovered

        Environmentally friendly

        Non toxic

        Low freezing point (below –10°C).

It is unlikely that a solvent which satisfies all these criteria will be available and the final choice is of necessity a compromise.

10.1.1 Water

Sometimes water can be advantageously used in a two-phase system with a phase transfer catalyst.

This solvent fulfils many of the above criteria and ideally should be used wherever possible. However relatively few organic substrates are sufficiently soluble in water and many synthetic reagents react with and are decomposed by water.

10.1.2 Toluene

Toluene is attacked by halogenation and electrophilic reagents.

Toluene is readily available, cheap and easily recovered by simple distillation. In general it is chemically inert to many reagents and many organic substrates and reagents have adequate solubility in toluene. For many reactions it is an ideal solvent. It is a suitable replacement for benzene (a human carcinogen), which surprisingly is still used in research laboratories.

10.1.3 Methyl t-butyl ether

Methyl t-butyl ether can be cleaved by acidic reagents. Grignard reactions are not possible due to cleavage by the magnesium halides.

This solvent is now very widely available due to its extensive use in lead-free petrol. It is of similar polarity to diethyl ether and many organic substances have high solubility in it, making it ideal both as a reaction medium and as an extraction solvent. It is relatively inert towards formation of peroxides.

10.1.4 Ethyl acetate

n-Butyl acetate is more stable and can often be used advantageously.

Ethyl acetate is often used as a solvent for extracting a product from an aqueous phase, due to its relatively high polarity and the high solubility of organic products. It is slowly cleaved by both aqueous acid and alkali, so caution is necessary in large-scale use due to the longer times necessary for work up of a reaction. When used as an extraction solvent from an aqueous phase, the ethyl acetate layer typically contains ca 5% water. The consequences of this need to be considered when the solvent extract is concentrated; the water can cause hydrolysis to acetic acid and damage to acid sensitive products.

10.1.5 Methanol

Methanol is cheap, readily available, readily recovered, and many substrates are very soluble. It is however reactive to many reagents and is somewhat toxic.

10.1.6 Ethanol

It is possible to use ethanol on a large scale providing Customs and Excise regulations are complied with.

Ethanol is only available when denatured by another solvent, e.g. methanol, pyridine, toluene, which can lead to complications.

10.1.7 Dimethylformamide/N-methylpyrrolidinone

N-Methylpyrrolidinone is considerably more thermally stable than DMF and is advantageously used on a large scale under basic conditions.

These dipolar aprotic solvents have many uses due to the high solubility and relative inertness of the solvent to many reagents. They are not readily recovered due to the high boiling point. They are miscible with water The product is often isolated by drowning out the reaction mixture into water and filtration of the solid product. This can lead to a large volume of aqueous waste which is difficult to dispose of.

10.1.8 Tetrahydrofuran

Often used for Grignard reactions.

This is an ideal solvent for many reactions due to the good solubility properties and excellent co-ordination to many metals. It is relatively expensive and tends to form peroxides easily. It is water miscible, but can be used to extract the product from an aqueous drown-out by saturating the aqueous phase with inorganic salts, e.g. sodium chloride.

10.1.9 Cyclohexane, hexane, petroleum fractions

These are relatively non-polar solvents which are in general chemically inert to many reagents. Many substrates have only limited solubility, which restricts their use as reaction solvents. They are however very useful solvents for isolation of products from more polar reaction solvents by dilution with, e.g. hexane and filtration of the now insoluble product.

10.1.10 Dichloromethane

This solvent has excellent solubility properties and is relatively chemically inert (apart from under basic conditions). The major disadvantage is that it is very toxic to sewage bacteria which means that all aqueous phases that have been in contact with dichloromethane require special treatment before disposal. Incineration of the aqueous phase is an expensive but often the only practicable option.

10.1.11 Acetonitrile

Although relatively toxic, this is a useful polar aprotic solvent. It is used as a reaction solvent when anhydrous conditions are required and also in the purification of organic products by recrystallisation.

10.1.12 Acetone

Acetone is cheap and of low toxicity. It is however too reactive to have widespread use as a reaction solvent. It is used extensively for plant vessel cleaning.

 

10.2 Oxidants

10.2.1 Hydrogen peroxide and peroxides

Operation under a nitrogen atmosphere is a basis of safety for large scale operation. Evolution of oxygen can compromise this safety.

Hydrogen peroxide is cheap, readily available, and gives rise to non-toxic by-products, oxygen and water. It is however only available as an aqueous solution which is a major limitation. Care must be taken in use to prevent catalytic decomposition by traces of metals or finely divided solids, e.g. carbon, which can lead to rapid evolution of large volumes of oxygen. Often hydrogen peroxide can be used for the in situ generation of peracids, e.g. peracetic acid and performic acid.

t-Butyl hydroperoxide is a very useful non-aqueous peroxide which has found widespread applicability especially in asymmetric epoxidation reactions such as the Sharpless epoxidation.

m-Chloroperbenzoic acid is a selective reagent for the epoxidation of double bonds. Due to its potentially explosive properties, especially in the vicinity of hot plant equipment, its use is confined to the laboratory only.

10.2.2 Chromic acid

Chromium trioxide is widely used as a relatively non-selective powerful oxidising agent. Often it is necessary to utilise considerably more than a stoichiometric amount to achieve complete reaction. This leads to generation of large amounts of chromium-containing waste which requires specialist and expensive disposal. It can be used both in aqueous and non-aqueous media, e.g. acetic acid solvent.

10.2.3 Potassium permanganate/manganese dioxide

Powerful relatively non-selective oxidants, which are used in at least stoichiometric amounts and give rise to metal residue disposal issues.

Page 79, 10 Reagents for large-scale operation

Manganese dioxide can be used catalytically with air as re-oxidant to achieve some oxidations. Often considerable investigation is necessary to define precise reaction conditions, but the ensuing advantages such as low cost and virtually zero residue disposal costs make catalytic oxidation much more preferable to stoichiometric metal oxidations.

10.2.4 Ozone

Selective powerful oxidant especially useful for the functionalisation of double bonds. A special plant is necessary for the generation of ozone from oxygen and its use, due to the potential fire hazards. Ozone is a potentially very useful reagent for large scale use, since the by-product oxygen is environmentally very friendly. The major disadvantage is the relatively high capital cost of the special plant. There are relatively few examples of large-scale use of ozone.

 

10.3 Reducing agents

10.3.1 Hydrogen

The catalyst can be recovered and re-used.

Use of hydrogen with a catalyst, e.g. palladium, is a very attractive process for the large scale. Special plant is necessary to contain the catalyst and to prevent the possibility of hydrogen and air explosions. Catalytic hydrogenation is highly selective and leads to a very simple work-up of merely filtering off the palladium catalyst. The by-product is often water whichisenvironmentally very acceptable.

10.3.2 Lithium aluminium hydride

The cylinder containing the solution is placed outside the building and the solution fed to the reactor through a pipe using nitrogen pressure.

The dissolved plastic can sometimes complicate the work-up by coming out of solution on addition of water. Additionally the plastic can contaminate the product of the reaction.

Powerful reducing agent which reacts violently with water and alcohols with the potential of ignition. The reagent can be purchased as a concentrated solution in tetrahydrofuran, and this is preferable for large-scale use, since it minimises the handling hazards and minimises the quantity actually in the vicinity of the reaction vessel.

Alternatively the reagent can be purchased in sealed plastic bags which are readily soluble in solvents such as tetrahydrofuran. The bag is charged directly to the reactor vessel. This offers considerable safety advantages.

The work up of lithium aluminium hydride reactions generates aluminium hydroxide which may be filtered off or dissolved in water (acid or sodium tartrate solution) for disposal.

10.3.3 Sodium borohydride

This mild reducing agent is often used in aqueous solution, but can also be used in methanol (some decomposition), dimethoxyethane or diethylene-glycol dimethyl ether. The reagent decomposes to give hydrogen gas especially under acidic conditions and this needs to be safely dispersed.

Sodium cyanoborohydride is more stable to acid conditions. It is however considerably more expensive than sodium borohydride and care is needed in its use on a large scale due to the toxicity of cyanide.

10.3.4 Di-isobutyl aluminium hydride

Selective reducing agent especially useful for the generation of aldehydes from esters. It may be purchased as a solution in toluene, and can be handled in similar plant to that used for lithium aluminium hydride.

10.3.5 Iron metal

Cheap readily available reducing agent. Often used in aqueous solution but can also be used in a solvent, e.g. acetic acid. Although iron is used in often greater than stoichiometric amounts, the disposal of the non-toxic iron residues is relatively easy.

 

10.4 Acids and bases

10.4.1 Sulphuric acid

Very versatile acid not only for neutralisation reactions, but also as a solvent for electrophilic nitration of aromatic rings, as a Lewis acid and general dehydration reagent.

Stainless steel is relatively inert to sulphuric acid and this acid is advantageously chosen for operation in metal plant vessels.

10.4.2 Hydrochloric acid

This acid is widely used for neutralisation purposes. It is obtained as an aqueous solution and this can cause stress corrosion of metal vessels, so its use is largely confined to glass-lined plant.

For the formation of hydrochloride salts anhydrous hydrogen chloride gas from cylinders can be used.

10.4.3 Phosphoric acid

This is a very viscous liquid which can cause handling difficulties. Its main use is as a Lewis acid or dehydration reagent. It is often used as reaction solvent in much greater than stoichiometric amount. Work-up leads to large amounts of phosphate residues which can give rise to disposal problems.

10.4.4 Methanesulphonic acid and p-toluenesulphonic acid

e.g. aminonitrile 7.9 in Chapter 7

These are strong acids which are readily soluble in most organic solvents and are widely used for many reactions requiring acidic conditions.

p-Toluenesulphonic acid contains a mole of water of crystallisation which can sometimes interfere with a reaction. Methanesulphonic acid is a liquid which makes it easier to charge to a reactor vessel. Both acids are widely used to form crystalline salts to enable basic products to be isolated and purified.

10.4.5 Acetic acid and formic acid

These weak acids (formic acid is stronger than acetic acid) are widely used for neutralisation where the product is sensitive to strong acids. Additionally both have been employed as reaction solvents. Work-up usually involves dilution with water and filtration of the precipitated product. This generates81, 10 Reagents for large-scale operation large volumes of effluent containing substantial organic material, which can cause problems in the subsequent disposal.

10.4.6 Sodium hydroxide

e.g. phosphonate in Chapter 8.

Strong base which is used for neutralisation and as a base for many reactions. It may be used both in aqueous solution and in alcoholic solvents.

Sometimes reactions traditionally performed with much stronger bases e.g. butyl lithium or sodium hydride can be achieved using a two-phase system of concentrated aqueous sodium hydroxide solution and an organic solvent in the presence of a phase transfer catalyst.

10.4.7 Ammonia

e.g. amide formation and Birch reductions.

Aqueous ammonia solution is used for neutralisation when the product is sensitive to strongly basic conditions. The buffering effect prevents the pH from rising too high.

Disposal of solutions containing large amounts of nitrogen compounds places a high load on the sewerage system and sodium hydroxide is used wherever possible.

Anhydrous ammonia can be used both as a reagent and as a solvent for reactions.

10.4.8 Triethylamine and pyridine

These bases are used in anhydrous conditions for many reactions where a base is necessary either to generate an anion or to neutralise an acid being generated by the reaction.

10.4.9 Butyl lithium

This very powerful base is supplied as a solution in hexane or other inert hydrocarbon solvent. It is potentially pyrophoric if concentrated solutions are exposed to air. All operations must be conducted in a nitrogen or argon atmosphere. It is very widely used to generate anions from very weakly acidic protons and for metallation reactions followed by attack by an electrophile.

10.4.10 Sodium hydride

Chemically this is a very useful base since it generates anions irreversibly, due to the liberation of hydrogen gas. It reacts with water violently and is usually supplied mixed with liquid paraffin oil to protect the reagent from atmospheric moisture. This can cause complications due to the oil contaminating the product. It is inadvisable on safety grounds to remove the oil before use on a large scale. Care is necessary using sodium hydride in dimethylformamide due to the potential of very exothermic decomposition of the solvent. N-Methylpyrrolidinone is a safer, more thermally stable alternative solvent.

10.4.11 α-Methylbenzylamine

This base is relatively cheap and readily available in both enantiomerically pure forms on a large scale. It is widely used for the resolution of acids by82, 10 Reagents for large-scale operation formation of diastereoisomeric salts. It is a strong base and forms crystalline salts with many weak acids, which accounts for its widespread application.

 

10.5 Halogenation reagents

10.5.1 Halogens

Trifluoroethanol was the purchased fluorine-containing raw material for ICI 162846. see Chapter 7.

Chlorine and bromine are frequently used in the electrophilic addition to, and substitution of, activated aromatic and carbonyl systems. Iodine is less frequently used due to its expense and lack of availability. Fluorine, although cheap cannot be used in general multi-purpose equipment due to its extremely high reactivity and the potential attack of glass-lined vessels. It is usual to purchase from a specialist supplier the fluorine-containing fragment and elaborate this into the final product.

10.5.2 N-Bromo- and N-chlorosuccinimide

These reagents are used to perform radical halogenation, e.g. of an alkyl group a to an aromatic ring or olefin. The reaction requires a radical initiator, e.g. dibenzoyl peroxide or α,α' - azobisisobutyronitrile and an inert solvent such as cyclohexane, chlorobenzene or acetic acid. Such halogenations give a mixture of mono- and di-halogenated products, so it is important to optimise the stoichiometry and reaction times and temperatures for each process.

10.5.3 Thionyl chloride

It is sometimes necessary to catalyse the reaction by adding a small amount of DMF. This forms the more reactive Vilsmeier complex

Thionyl chloride is a popular reagent for the conversion of a carboxylic acid into its acid chloride which may then be coupled to a further fragment of the target molecule.

The reaction produces acidic gases as by-products and these must be absorbed by a scrubbing system containing sodium hydroxide solution. On a large scale it is normal to use only a small excess of thionyl chloride in the process. The rate of reaction and evolution of gases is controlled by the rate of addition of thionyl chloride.

10.5.4 Phosphorus oxychloride

Phosphorus oxychloride is used to chlorinate amidic and hydroxylic systems, e.g. in the conversion of a pyridone into a chloropyridine. Usually an excess of the reagent is required. Work-up of the reaction produces phosphate salts which can cause effluent disposal problems.

10.6 Alkylating agents

Because of their potential ability to react with and modify DNA, alkylating agents are considered to be potential carcinogens. Appropriate precautions to protect personnel must be taken when these reagents are used on a large scale.

10.6.1 Dimethyl sulphate

Dimethyl sulphate is cheap and readily available. It is used to methylate carboxylic acids, phenols and nitrogen atoms, especially heterocyclic NH83, 10 Reagents for large-scale operation groups. The reaction is often performed in methanol in presence of aqueous sodium hydroxide as base. The reaction can also be performed in a two-phase system with a water-immiscible solvent such as toluene. Since dimethyl sulphate is consumed by alternative reaction pathways, it is necessary to use an excess of the reagent. For example dimethyl ether may be formed as by-product and this can pose a containment problem because of volatility (b.p. –23°C) and inertness which means it cannot be absorbed into aqueous based gas absorption systems.

10.6.2 Methyl iodide

Methyl iodide is used in cases where the very active leaving group iodide is of a distinct advantage and in cases where dimethyl sulphate does not work well. It is considerably more expensive than dimethyl sulphate and its volatility dictates the need for special handling.

10.6.3 Methyl tosylate

Methyl tosylate is cheap, readily available and has a reasonable activity. The major disadvantage is that tosic acid has a relatively high molecular weight. This leads to the necessity to use a high weight to introduce a low molecular weight methyl group. Additionally the mole of tosic acid produced is of high weight which leads to expensive disposal.

10.7 Enzymes

The ß-lactam antibiotics, e.g. penicillin, are largely produced by enzymic processes.

Many examples of enzyme catalysed processes have been published in the chemical literature. Enzymes are attractive reagents because they are often very specific for a particular transformation and they perform under very mild conditions (e.g. in water at ambient temperature). Enzymes are particularly useful in the synthesis of enantiomerically pure compounds because one isomer in a racemic substrate reacts much more quickly than the other (an example of kinetic resolution). ICI developed such a process for the manufacture of (S)-2-chloropropionic acid (used for the synthesis of pheno-oxypropionic acid herbicides) in which the unwanted (R)- isomer is selectively hydrolysed by a dehalogenase enzyme. This process is currently operated on a scale in excess of 1000 tonnes per annum. Racemic esters are similarly resolved by a selective hydrolysis using a lipase enzyme. Immobilisation of the enzyme on an inert support allows easier recovery and re-use, which may be an important factor in the viability of a large scale process.

10.8 Sulphur reagents

10.8.1 Thiols and sulphides

Sometimes it is necessary to commit to building the special plant before the biological activity of the compound is proven.

The widespread use of alkyl- and arylthio groups in organic synthesis is partly attributable to the fact that they are both good nucleophiles and also good leaving groups. However simple thiols are difficult to handle in multipurpose plant because of their potent disagreeable smell. The odour threshold (level of detection) is typically one part in 109 and special plant or equipment may be necessary to achieve this level of containment. The chemical Page 84, 10 Reagents for large-scale operation advantages of such a process need to be carefully weighed against the considerable capital cost of building a special plant.

In such cases the process development chemist will often find an alternative route which avoids the use of a thiol. There are cases however where the use of a thiol is very beneficial in terms of chemical selectivity, high yield and relatively low materials cost.

10.8.2 Hydrogen sulphide

Hydrogen sulphide is a useful reagent for conversion of alcohols into thiols and nitriles into thioamides. However, special plant is required to contain the odour (see 10.8.1) and the reagent is highly toxic.

10.8.3 Sodium sulphide

Sodium sulphide is a cheap reagent used for the selective reduction of an aromatic nitrogroup and in the formation of dialkyl sulphides.

10.8.4 Sodium thiocyanate

Sodium thiocyanate is a bifunctional reagent in which the sulphur acts as a nucleophile and the nitrile as an electrophile. Thus it can be used in the formation of heterocyclic ring systems.

10.8.5 Thiourea

Sulphur is more nucleophilic than nitrogen

Thiourea is an odourless crystalline solid which is often used as an in-situ source of alkylthiols. The compound readily alkylates on sulphur and basic hydrolysis of the thiouronium salt liberates the thiol.

Thiourea is also used to prepare thiazoles, e.g. by reaction with an α-haloketone.

10.8.6 Sodium metabisulphite

This is a reducing agent which is commonly used to decompose excess peroxide or peracid after an oxidation so that the product may be isolated safely.

 

10.9 Phosphorus reagents

e.g See polyphosphoric acid and phosphorus oxychloride above.

Phosphorus reagents are employed increasingly on a large scale due to the versatility of the chemical transformations achievable. Although phosphorus reagents potentially create severe effluent disposal issues, the advantages of the selectivity of the transformations achievable are preferable to longer alternative less selective procedures which would generate considerably more waste for special disposal.

10.9.1 Phosphorus halides

These include phosphorus trichloride, phosphorus oxychloride, phosphorus pentachloride, phosphorus tribromide. They are used to convert hydroxyl functions into the corresponding halo-derivative, and as Lewis acids in electrophilic aromatic substitution reactions. A large excess of the reagent frequently has to be employed to achieve complete conversion. Their use isPage 85, 10 Reagents for large-scale operation decreasing due to the difficulties of safely disposing of effluent containing large amounts of phosphates.

10.9.2 Wittig and related reactions

e.g. See the synthesis of the cis alkenoic acid topside chain in the Thromboxane antagonists in Chapter 5 and the Prostaglandins in Chapter 8.

The Wittig reaction of an ylid (derived from a trialkyl or triaryl phosphonium salt) and an aldehyde to give an alkene is a well known use of a phosphorus reagent. The reaction can be used to produce either cis or trans alkenes. Although the reaction generates large quantities of e.g. triphenylphosphine oxide which requires special disposal, there are no other techniques which would generate the alkene so efficiently.

Operation under a nitrogen atmosphere is a general safety requirement for all plant operations involving solvents.

This reaction requires anhydrous conditions free from oxygen ingress. This is readily achieved on a plant scale, by distilling toluene from the vessel to dry the system, and by nitrogen purging. There is only limited surface area exposed relative to the large volume, which favours maintenance of the dry anaerobic conditions.

 

10.10 Boron reagents

10.10.1 Diborane

Care must be taken to ensure that free diborane is not present in the vapour phase above the reaction mixture.

Diborane is highly flammable and forms dangerously explosive mixtures with air. However several complexes (e.g. borane.THF and borane.pyridine) are commercially available and can be handled safely on a large scale. They are particularly useful for performing selective transformations on a large scale under very mild conditions (e.g. carboxylic acids to hydroxymethyl compounds and amides to amines).

10.10.2 Boron trifluoride

Boron trifluoride is a popular choice as a Lewis acid. The diethyl ether complex is commonly used in the laboratory. This would cause serious flammability concerns. Boron trifluoride gas (available in cylinders) is preferred on the plant scale.

10.10.3 Dialkyl borinates

See Chapter 6 for chiral aldol reactions.

These reagents are readily prepared from a trialkylboron and one mole of an acid, e.g. trifluoromethanesulphonic acid. They are widely used to promote aldol condensations.

10.10.4 Aryl boronic acids.

These reagents are readily prepared from an aryl Grignard reagent and a trialkyl borate. They are particularly useful to promote biaryl coupling. This is a powerful method for synthesis of substituted biaryls that would be difficult to obtain by other syntheses.

10.11 Silicon reagents

Silicon reagents are used to protect a range of functional groups (e.g. alcohols, amines, carboxylic acids) during organic synthesis.

10.11.1 Chlorotrimethylsilane and N,O-bis(trimethylsilyl)acetamide

The trimethylsilyl group is easily removed by treatment with dilute aqueous acid or an alcohol.

Chlorotrimethylsilane is cheap and readily available. It is normally used in conjunction with a base such as triethylamine. N,O-Bis(trimethylsilyl) acetamide is rather more expensive but does not require basic catalysis.

10.11.2 t-Butyldimethylchlorosilane

Fajrly stable silyl ethers are formed with t-butyldimethylchlorosilane. This reagent is expensive and for its use to be economically viable the silicon compound would probably need to be recycled after deprotection.

 

10.12 Palladium reagents

10.12.1 Palladium metal

This is most frequently encountered as the finely divided metal on carbon. It is widely used as a catalyst for hydrogenation reactions (see 10.3.1).

10.12.2 Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium

The maximum permitted level of palladium in a pharmaceutical product is typically 10 parts per million.

This reagent is used as a catalyst for an important series of coupling reactions (e.g. the Heck and Suzuki reactions) by virtue of its ability to form nucleophilic aryl complexes. However, the reagent is air sensitive and often it may be successfully replaced by palladium chloride, relying on an in situ reduction to palladium metal. This reagent is preferred on a large scale. However potential contamination of the product with palladium is sometimes a problem in the manufacture of pharmaceuticals.
