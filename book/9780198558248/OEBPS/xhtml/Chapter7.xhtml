7 H2 blockers

The stomach normally has a pH of around 2, and this is achieved by the secretion of dilute hydrochloric acid to aid the digestion process. Excessive activity of the secretory glands leads to too strongly acidic conditions and a tendency towards the formation of stomach ulcers. Surgical removal of the ulcer merely treats the symptoms and not the cause. Similarly the use of anti-acids such as sodium bicarbonate or magnesium carbonate offers only temporary relief since the excessive acid secretion is not being reduced.

The H2 blockers are a class of pharmaceutical compounds which block the H2 receptors in the stomach lining and so lower the secretion of HCl. Under these milder conditions ulcers can heal naturally without the necessity for surgery. The recurrence of ulcers is also minimised under the more alkaline conditions

7.1 Initial research route

The compound was first synthesised from three fragments.

7.1.1 Synthesis of a 3 substituted pyrazole

Nitration of pyrazole with nitric acid-sulphuric acid mixture leads to essentially complete nitration in the 1 position giving 7.1 which may be thermally isomerised to the thermodynamically more stable 3 isomer 7.2 by heating in an inert solvent at about 160°C.

7.1.2 Synthesis of the 4 carboxamidobutyl moiety

All attempts to alkylate 3-nitropyrazole 7.2 with 4-bromobutylcarboxamide 7.3 led to the inevitable cyclisation to piperidone 7.4.

 

 

This difficulty was overcome by use of the nitrile 7.5, followed by subsequent hydrolysis to the amide later in the synthesis.

The reaction of dibromobutane with cyanide anion gives a statistical mixture of 7.5, unreacted 7.6 and adiponitrile 7.7.

The optimum conversion could be achieved by use of 0.6 eq cyanide and separation of unreacted 7.6 by distillation and re-use in a subsequent preparation.

Nitrile 7.5 was readily synthesised from tetrahydrofuran. Hydrolysis with hydrobromic acid yielded 1,4-dibromobutane 7.6, which could be reacted with sodium cyanide to give the desired 5-bromovaleronitrile 7.5.

 

 

7.1.3 Alkylation of 3-nitropyrazole

Reaction of 3-nitropyrazole with 5-bromovaleronitrile 7.5 gave rise to mainly N1 alkylation together with ca 20% of N2 alkylation. These could be easily separated by crystallisation to give a 64% isolated yield of 7.8.

 

 

7.1.4 Trifluoromethylguanidine formation

Reduction of the nitro-group to the corresponding amine 7.9 was achieved by catalytic hydrogenation over palladium-carbon. This was reacted with trifluoromethylisothiocyanate 7.10 to give the crystalline thiourea 7.11.

Trifluoromethylisothiocyanate 7.10 (bp 93°C) is a highly toxic molecule formed by reaction of trifluoroethylamine 7.12 (bp 36°C) with thiophosgene 7.13 (bp 70°C) and separation by distillation.

 

 

The reaction proceeds via desulphurisation with mercury to give an intermediate carbodi-imide which then adds on ammonia to give the asymmetrically substituted guanidine.

The thiourea 7.11 could be converted into the guanidine nitrile 7.14 by reaction with ammoniacal mercuric oxide.

The synthesis was completed by hydrolysis of the nitrile 7.14 to the amide ICI 162846.

7.1.5 Manufacturing difficulties

Whilst this route enabled the initial quantities to be synthesised to establish the biological interest in the compound and to support the initial development studies it was apparent the route would be almost impossible to operate on anything larger than a laboratory scale. The particular difficulties which were likely to be encountered were :

  1. (a) Nitropyrazoles are explosive compounds and could not be made in general purpose chemical plant.
  2. (b) Thiophosgene is highly toxic and of limited availability. Specialist handling facilities would be needed to use the reagent safely and dispose of waste arising from its use.
  3. (c) Trifluoroethylamine is toxic and very volatile. Containment of the vapour would be a serious concern. The compound is also of limited availability.
  4. (d) The use of mercuric oxide to desulphurise the thiourea 7.11 is highly undesirable both on environmental grounds and because there is the potential of mercury carrying through the synthesis and contaminating the final product ICI 162846.

7.2 Manufacturing route

This needed to address the issues of the explosive nature of nitropyrazoles and the difficulties associated with thiophosgene and mercury.

7.2.1 Synthesis of 3-aminopyrazole

Reaction of 2-chloroacrylonitrile 7.15 with hydrazine hydrate in presence of potassium carbonate gives a good yield of 3-aminopyrazole 7.16 which is a high boiling point liquid and may be isolated by distillation for laboratory work.

 

 

For plant manufacture 7.16 was not isolated but a solution used directly in the next stage.

7.2.2 Alkylation of 3-aminopyrazole

There are 3 possible positions of alkylation of 3-aminopyrazole: N1, N2, and the exocyclic 3-amino group. With 5-bromovaleronitrile 7.5 alkylation occurred predominantly at the desired Nl position accompanied by ca 25% alkylation at N2 and negligible amounts of alkylation of the 3-amino group.

 

 

Initially a modest separation was achieved by crystallisaton of the free base 7.9 followed by a somewhat wasteful removal of the residual N2 isomer later in the synthesis. Formation of the p-toluenesulphonate salt of 7.9 was subsequently shown to achieve a very effective removal of the unwanted N2 isomer and give pure 7.9.

This is an example of a very beneficial telescoping of two stages together to avoid distillation of 3-aminopyrazole and relying on a highly crystalline final product to achieve the necessary purity.

A very effective two stage process was developed wherein 3-aminopyrazole was obtained from hydrazine and 2-chloroacrylonitrile and a toluene solution reacted with 5-bromovaleronitrile 7.5 under phase transfer conditions to form 7.9 followed by isolation and purification as a tosylate salt.

7.2.3 Trifluoroethylamine

Only small quantities of trifluoroethylamine 7.12 are commercially available. For manufacture a large-scale synthesis of this key intermediate would be necessary. Two potentially useful precursors are available on a large scale: trifluoroethanol CF3CH2OH, and trifluoroethyl chloride CF3CH2Cl. In principle trifluoroethyl chloride is a more useful material, however the chlorine proved to be remarkably resistant to nucleophilic displacement with ammonia.

The electronegative CF3 group gives no stabilisation of the carbonium ion in SN1 displacement and strongly repels an incoming nucleophile for SN2 reactions.

The synthesis actually operated started from trifluoroethanol. Reaction with tosyl chloride in presence of aqueous sodium hydroxide led to the formation of the crystalline tosylate 7.17 which was then treated with excess aqueous ammonia under ca 200 psi pressure at 120°C in an autoclave to yield the required trifluoroethylamine. 7.12 These extreme conditions required to displace the tosyl group indicate the very low reactivity of a leaving group β to an electron withdrawing group.

Isolation of the volatile trifluoroethylamine (b.p. 36°C) was achieved by distillation from the aqueous reaction mixture using a condenser cooled by circulating refrigerant.

7.2.4 Formation of the guanidine moiety

The original route formed the guanidine by desulphurisation of a thiourea to give an intermediate carbodiimide followed by addition of ammonia.

Guanidines containing a NH group can be synthesised from addition of an amine to a cyanamide which can be formed by reaction of cyanogen halides with amines.

 

 

Attempts to apply this principle to 3-aminopyrazole formed not only the required cyanamide 7.18 but also substantial amounts of the symmetrical guanidine 7.19 by addition of two molecules of 7.9.

The second mole of amine simply neutralises the hydrogen halide and forms an insoluble amine salt which was recovered and re-cycled in a subsequent batch.

Trifluoroethylamine is a very weak nucleophile because of the electronegative CF3 group. Advantage of this fact was taken to design a successful synthesis. Reaction of two moles of trifluoroethylamine 7.12 with one mole of a cyanogen halide gave the intermediate trifluoroethylcyanamide 7.20 which, because of the poor nucleophilicity of 7.12, showed only weak tendency to add on a second mole of amine.

The cyanamide 7.20 is somewhat unstable and readily undergoes trimerisation in the solid state to form a triazine 7.21. This reaction was avoided by manufacturing the cyanamide in dilute solution and storing at low (0°C) temperature until used in the next stage.

Reaction of cyanamide 7.20 with the nitrile 7.9 under acid conditions smoothly formed the guanidine nitrile 7.14 which could be conveniently isolated as a bicarbonate salt.

Only the strongly basic guanidine 7.14 forms a salt with the weakly acidic carbonic acid. Unreacted 7.9 and trifluoroethylamine 7.12 are weak bases and do not form bicarbonate salts. This formed a very effective purification.

 

 

In practice it proved convenient to use 7.9 as its crystalline tosylate salt to provide the acid catalysis necessary for the reaction.

This sequence of reactions could be easily scaled up to a full production scale. Within ICI (now Zeneca) there is a chemical plant which manufactures cyanogen chloride for use in other products, and it proved convenient to utilise this facility for the synthesis of the cyanamide 7.20.

7.2.5 Formation of the guanidine from a urea

An alternative synthesis of the guanidine moiety was also successful. Reaction of nitrile 7.9 with phenyl chloroformate gave rise to the urethane 7.22 which was then reacted with trifluoroethylamine to give the highly crystalline urea 7.23.

Presumably the reaction occurs via formation of the same carbodiimide intermediate formed in the mercury desulphurisation of the thiourea 7.11.

This intermediate, on reaction with phosgene followed by reaction with aqueous ammonia, gives rise to the same guanidine nitrile 7.14.

7.2.6 Hydrolysis of the nitrile

The mole of water necessary for the hydrolysis is derived from the bicarbonate salt as well as the 1% present in concentrated sulphuric acid.

This was readily achieved by use of concentrated sulphuric acid. Dissolution of guanidine-nitrile 7.14 (isolated as a bicarbonate salt) in concentrated sulphuric acid at 20°C gave slow hydrolysis to ICI 162846, which was isolated by aqueous drown-out, neutralisation with sodium hydroxide and extraction into a solvent followed by crystallisation.

 

 

Probably formed both during the initial aqueous acid drown-out and also during the NaOH neutralisation of the sulphuric acid.
The buffering effect of ammonium sulphate prevents excessively high pH and over hydrolysis to the acid 7.24.

This procedure however led to some carboxylic acid impurity 7.24 which was difficult and wasteful to remove by aqueous extraction procedures due to the moderate solubility of ICI 162846

A better procedure was to drown the sulphuric acid solution INTO aqueous ammonia solution and then extract the ICI 162846 base into a solvent. Negligible levels of acid impurity 7.24 were formed leading to greatly enhanced yields.

One plant batch of this hydrolysis stage gave an unexpected result, forming substantial quantities of an impurity which was subsequently shown to be the t-butyl analogue 7.25. This was traced to be formed by a Ritter reaction arising from carry-over of t-butyl methyl ether, the solvent used in the isolation stage, in the guanidine–nitrile 7.14 substrate.

In sulphuric acid solution t-butyl methyl ether is readily cleaved to give the t-butyl carbonium ion which adds to the nitrile, yielding the t-butyl amide on hydrolysis.

 

 

 

7.2.7 Final purification

Recrystallisation of ICI 162846 from ethyl acetate gave good chemical purification (impurity removal) but gave a product of variable crystalline form and also variable melting point. This was shown to be due to the existence of polymorphs.

Subsequently use of a mixture of toluene and n-butanol gave the necessary chemical purity and a single crystalline product.

7.3 Polymorphism

Elemental sulphur is a common example of a pure substance existing in several polymorphic forms.

A pure substance can sometimes crystallise in more than one crystalline form called polymorphs. This arises due to a different packing arrangement of the molecules in the crystal unit cell. Polymorphs have different crystal lattice energies and this leads to differently shaped crystals which have different properties such as melting point and solubility. These physical properties can be of high importance in pharmaceutical compounds since the solubility can affect the bioavailabilty of the active compound and the crystalline form can affect the mechanical stability of tablets. Control of the polymorphic form is thus of high importance.

DSC is essentially a melting point technique which enables not only the temperature to be measured, but also the energy input necessary to achieve melting.

ICI 162846 is a simple example of a pure compound exhibiting polymorphism. This could be studied by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). A sample of ICI 162846 crystallised from ethyl acetate often exhibits a trace showing TWO distinct endotherms of melting at 380 K and also at 403 K together with a broad exotherm at about 390 K. This occurs due to the existence of two polymorphs in the crystalline solid sample. As the temperature rises to 380 K the energy absorption is small and is being used to heat the solid. At 380 K a large absorption of energy occurs to melt one of the polymorphic forms. This is followed by an evolution of energy as the molten material resolidifies. Further heating raises the temperature of the solid to 403 K where there is a further large absorption of energy to melt the entire solid.

 

 

Page 56, 7 H2 blockers 

Crash cooling will tend to favour crystallisation of the metastable polymorph.

In general it is preferable to use the most stable (highest melting) polymorph for pharmaceutical purposes since this minimises the possibility of subsequent polymorphic changes during milling operations and formation of tablets. The less stable polymorph is in a metastable state, being of higher lattice energy. The more stable polymorph is usually obtained by slow controlled crystallisation at a high temperature.

In the case of ICI 162846 the high temperature crystallisation was achieved from a mixture of toluene and w-butanol solvent. This solvent combination also achieved the required chemical purity, and consistently gave the more stable higher melting polymorph on a large scale.

7.4 Conclusions

This product demonstrates many of the principles of process development. The initial research route could not be operated on a large scale because of :

  1. 1. The explosive nature of nitropyrazoles.
  2. 2. The high toxicity of thiophosgene would require use of special plant to contain the vapours.
  3. 3. The use of mercury is undesirable on environmental grounds and because of potential contamination of the final product.

The developed route avoided these issues by :

  1. 1. Use of aminopyrazole.
  2. 2. Use of cyanogen chloride to replace thiophosgene and mercury. Although cyanogen chloride is highly toxic there was expertise available within ICI for the safe use and containment of the gas.
  3. 3. A facile synthesis of the relatively inaccessible trifluoroethylamine was developed and used on a large scale.
  4. 4. A facile synthesis of 5-bromovaleronitrile from tetrahydrofuran was developed.
  5. 5. The synthesis is highly efficient since it is convergent and it was possible to operate the manufacture of three separate streams of intermediates simultaneously before joining the fragments together at the end of the synthesis.
